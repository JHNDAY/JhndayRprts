Talking Through Ideas vs. Getting the Facts

Generative AI is your partner for discussing and assessing ideas — RAG is your tool for retrieving accurate, citable facts

BOLLI Study Group Leaders · AI Literacy Series

✍️ Basic Generative AI: The Foundation

▼

Your thinking partner for discussing, interpreting, and assessing ideas—drawing on vast general knowledge to help you explore a topic, weigh arguments, and sharpen your own thinking.

▶️ See How it Works

When you want to think through a topic—explore its tensions, weigh competing interpretations, draft discussion questions, or assess the strength of an argument—generative AI is a brilliant, tireless interlocutor. It has absorbed enormous breadth of knowledge and can engage seriously with almost any idea your BOLLI course raises. What it cannot do is go fetch a fact for you: it cannot look up a current source, verify a claim against a live document, or tell you what was published last month. It reasons from what it already knows, not from what it retrieves.

Example — Generative AI as idea partner

"What are the strongest arguments for and against crediting Rosalind Franklin as a co-discoverer of the DNA double helix?" — the AI engages the intellectual and ethical dimensions, weighs the evidence, and helps you think through the question, drawing on its broad training.

Where Generative AI reaches its limit

"What does the latest scholarship say about Franklin's role, and where can participants read more?" — for that question, you need RAG. Generative AI can discuss ideas about the topic; only RAG can go get you today's facts and sources.

⚡ RAG: The Cosmic Leap Forward

▼

Your fact-retrieval engine—goes out to the live web or your uploaded documents, pulls the actual sources, and grounds every answer in citable, verifiable content you can point participants to.

When you need a fact—a current source, a verifiable claim, a real article participants can read—RAG is the tool. RAG-enabled AI (Claude or ChatGPT with web search, Perplexity AI, or any AI with document upload) goes out and retrieves actual content: live web pages, your uploaded readings, your session transcripts. It doesn't reason from memory; it reads what you give it—or what it finds on the web—and cites every claim with a link or page reference. The distinction is clean: use generative AI to think, use RAG to find and verify.

Example — RAG in action for course preparation

"Based on this week's assigned reading on Rosalind Franklin, what three discussion questions would probe the most contested interpretations?" — a RAG system reads the actual uploaded text and crafts questions grounded in its specific arguments and evidence, with page references.

Example — RAG applied to session follow-up

Upload your Zoom session transcript and ask: "Where did our discussion diverge most sharply from the assigned reading's conclusions, and what additional sources might resolve those gaps?" RAG reads both documents and produces a sourced synthesis in seconds.

💡 The Foundation and the Leap

▼

Generative AI is the foundational technology—and the right tool whenever the task is intellectual: discussing an idea, assessing an argument, drafting a question, exploring a theme. It is a brilliant thinking partner. But it works from what it already knows, not from what it can retrieve. For ideas, it is remarkable.

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) is the tool for facts. When the question is "what does a credible source actually say, and where can I find it?"—RAG goes and gets the answer. It retrieves, reads, and cites. It doesn't opine; it produces evidence.

The rule of thumb: if you're thinking and discussing, use Generative AI. If you're fact-checking and source-hunting, use RAG. The best BOLLI course preparation uses both—in the right order.

🎓 Why RAG is a Cosmic Leap for BOLLI Course Work

▼

The distinction is simple and powerful: use Generative AI to talk through and assess ideas—use RAG to get the facts. Basic generative AI is a remarkable thinking partner for background context, concept exploration, and intellectual discussion. But when a BOLLI participant asks "where does that claim come from?" or "what are people writing about this now?"—that's a RAG question, not a Generative AI question. The question isn't just "What do you think about Mary Wollstonecraft?"—it's "What has been written about her lately, and where can I point participants to read more?"

The transformation in practice: With RAG-enabled AI, a Study Group Leader can ask: "Find me three accessible, well-sourced web articles on the current scholarly debate about Rosalind Franklin's credit for the DNA discovery." The AI retrieves real pages and produces a sourced list with links participants can follow. It is not opining—it is finding facts on your behalf. That is a categorically different tool from one that discusses ideas, however brilliantly.

For BOLLI's intellectually rigorous learners, this matters. Discussion should be grounded in evidence, not in an AI's impressions. RAG lets the SGL say: here is what a credible source actually says today—here is the link, here is where the debate stands now. Generative AI helps you think about what it means.

  • Live Source Discovery: Ask "Find recent accessible articles on the ethics of AI in healthcare" and get links to real, current web sources—not a synthesis of training-data impressions
  • Fact-Checking in Real Time: When a claim arises in preparation, RAG can retrieve a current source to confirm or complicate it—rather than relying on potentially stale training data
  • Supplementary Reading Lists: Ask "What are good introductory web resources on quantum entanglement for a general audience?" and receive a cited, linked list drawn from actual current pages
  • Keeping Pace with Living History: For courses touching on recent events, living figures, or active research fields, RAG retrieves what has actually been published—not what the model last knew
  • Reducing Hallucination Where It Matters: Recent developments and evolving scholarship are exactly where basic Gen AI is most likely to confabulate—RAG anchors responses to real retrieved pages, dramatically lowering that risk
  • Multiple Perspectives, One Query: Ask for coverage of a topic from different angles—scientific, humanistic, journalistic—and RAG retrieves actual representative sources rather than producing a generic summary

The Bottom Line: Generative AI is for talking about and assessing ideas—it thinks with you, argues with you, helps you see a topic from multiple angles. RAG is for getting facts—it retrieves current sources, verifies claims, and produces citable evidence with links. The best BOLLI course preparation uses both: Generative AI to deepen your thinking, RAG to ground your discussion in what credible sources actually say today.
