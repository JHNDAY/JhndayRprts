Prompt Scaffolding Guide

Seven stages from starter sentence to rich conversation

0
Pick Your Purpose
▼
Before you type anything, ask: why am I doing this? Your intention shapes everything that follows and determines what a good response looks like.
I want to learn something I don't know I want to argue a position and stress-test it I'm genuinely ambivalent and want to explore I want to challenge something I just read I want to find evidence for or against a claim
1
Choose a Starter
▼
No card selected yet — click one from the list
The starter removes the blank page. React to it rather than inventing from nothing.
2
Add Your Context
▼
This is what most beginners miss. The prompt is not a question — it's a conversation setup. Add what you already know, half-remember, believe, or worry about. Your context is what makes the response yours.
What you think you know about the topic A personal experience that connects to it Something you read or heard recently A hunch or intuition you want to test A constraint: "explain it to a non-expert"
3
Execute the Prompt
▼
Commit and send. Resist the urge to over-edit. An imperfect prompt that launches a conversation is worth more than a perfect one you never send.
Don't overthink the wording You can always clarify in the next turn Shorter is often better than longer
4
Read the Response
▼
Slow down and actually read it. Most people skim, then complain the AI wasn't useful. Ask yourself these questions:
Did it surprise me? Did it miss something important? Did it get something wrong? Did it answer what I actually meant? What would I want to know next?
5
Continue the Conversation
▼
This is where AI shifts from search engine to thinking partner. Most beginners stop at Stage 4. The real value begins here. Choose your move:
← Push Back

"I don't think that's right because..."
"You're missing the fact that..."
"But what about...?"

Go Deeper →

"Tell me more about..."
"What's the evidence for...?"
"How does this connect to...?"

These are different cognitive habits — one challenges, one explores. Both are worth practicing.
