Philosophy

"Experiment!" — Cole Porter. The animating principle is learning by doing with a purpose. Active, critical, confident use of AI.

What AI Literacy Actually Means

AI Literacy encompasses three core competencies: mastering the mechanics of computer interfaces (most notably the web browser); understanding the foundational concepts of artificial intelligence; and developing the skill of conversing effectively with AI agents.

Central to this course is the deeply conversational nature of AI. Unlike a Google keyword search, working with AI is an iterative dialogue — each prompt and response builds on the last, peeling back layers as you go deeper into a topic. Equally important: learning when not to trust AI and distinguishing digital intelligence from organic human judgment.

Week 1 · Digital Fluency

Getting Started: Your Browser as Your Launchpad

Foundations of Digital Fluency — Safari & Chrome from the Ground Up

📅 90-minute session · Hybrid Zoom + In-Person

Learning Objectives

  • Open and navigate a web browser with confidence
  • Understand the address bar, tabs, and back/forward buttons
  • Use Safari and Chrome tutorials as self-paced reference tools
  • Open and view an AI chat interface (Claude.ai)
  • Type and send your first AI message

Guiding Questions

  • What is a web browser and why does it matter for AI?
  • How is AI different from a Google search?
  • What does it feel like to have a conversation with an AI?
  • What is Digital Fluency and why do we start here?

What Is Digital Fluency and Why Does It Come First?

📊 Visualization
Generative AI → RAG: What Is This Thing You're About to Talk To?

Instructor opens the Generative AI vs. RAG interactive visualization and walks through it with the class. This is the conceptual bridge between "we're learning the browser" and "we're about to have a conversation with an AI." Covers: how basic generative AI works (training, patterns, prediction); what RAG is and why it's a leap forward; and critically — what AI doesn't know and why that matters. Students leave this block with a working mental model before their first hands-on interaction. When they type their first message and get a response, it won't be a magic box — it will be a system they have a framework for.

1:05–1:20
📊 Visualization
Foundations: Intelligence & Conversation — Now You Have Words for What You Just Felt

Students have just done two things: had their first AI conversation (Week 1), and completed their first split-screen immersive reading session (minutes ago). This is the ideal moment to open the Foundations: Intelligence & Conversation visualization — not as a cold introduction, but as a retrospective framework. Walk through its four sections with the class: Digital & Organic Intelligence (what's different about each kind of mind); Importance of Conversation (why iterative dialogue beats one-shot prompting); Balancing the Strengths (your judgment + AI's reach); and Meaning of Words (how AI actually interprets language, and why precise wording matters). The Proust framework — Pensée Perdue, Pensée Nouvelle, Bibliothèque Infinie — can be woven in here as a poetic complement to the viz's analytical framing.

💡 Instructor note: Invite students to connect each section back to something they experienced in the split-screen activity. "Did you notice that?" questions work better here than lecture. This is a consolidation moment, not new content.

1:20–1:30

Week 3 · Current Events & Political Discourse

AI as a News Companion & Civic Thinking Partner

From Headlines to Dialogue — Using AI to Deepen Political Understanding

📅 90-minute session · Hybrid Zoom + In-Person

Learning Objectives

  • Use the Current Events Portal as a live research tool with AI
  • Ask AI to summarize and contextualize breaking news
  • Request structured left/right analysis of a policy issue
  • Recognize AI's limitations and biases in political content
  • Use AI to find common ground and steelman opposing views

Key Framing Questions

  • Can AI help us understand people we disagree with?
  • How do we verify AI-generated news summaries?
  • What does "balanced" mean when asking AI about politics?
  • How is using AI for current events different from reading the paper?
Session Timeline
0:00–0:15
Opening
The Current Events Portal — Live Demo

Instructor opens the Current Events Portal and walks through it as a curated news resource. Demonstration: paste a headline into Claude and ask for background context. Students try with a headline of their choosing. This session is connected directly to the BOLLI Current Events Study Group.

0:15–0:40
Core Activity
Going Deep on a Story: AI as Research Partner

Students select a current news story from the portal. Guided conversation exercise: (1) Ask Claude for a neutral summary. (2) Ask: "What background do I need to understand this?" (3) Ask: "What are the strongest arguments on each side?" Students conduct this 3-step conversation and share back their most interesting moment. Instructor highlights: noticing when AI hedges, citing its sources, and the importance of follow-up questions.

0:40–1:05
Anchor Demo
Political Discourse: Left & Right on a Single Issue

Instructor selects a current policy theme (e.g., immigration, healthcare funding, environmental regulation — not tied to a specific event but to a durable tension). Demo prompt: "Explain the strongest progressive argument for [X]. Then explain the strongest conservative argument. Then tell me where the two sides might actually agree." Class discusses: Does this feel fair? What surprised you? Does using AI change how you think about political disagreement? This exercise models AI as a tool for civic empathy, not political opinion.

1:05–1:20
Critical Thinking
When Not to Trust AI: News, Facts & Hallucinations

Live demonstration of an AI limitation: ask Claude about a very recent event and observe its hedging or error. Discuss: Why does this happen? What is the knowledge cutoff? When should you verify with a primary source? Key skill: knowing when AI is guessing vs. when it genuinely knows.

1:20–1:30
Wrap-Up
Discussion: AI and the Future of Civic Literacy

Brief open discussion: How might AI change the way people engage with news and politics? Is that good or bad? Student reflections and questions.

Week 4 · Literature & History

Reading Deeply, Thinking Historically

Immersive Text Exploration and AI-Powered Study Guides

📅 90-minute session · Hybrid Zoom + In-Person

Learning Objectives

  • Use split-screen to engage deeply with a literary text
  • Generate discussion questions and character analyses with AI
  • Understand RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) conceptually
  • Use RAG to create a study guide from a historical work
  • Compare AI-assisted reading with traditional close reading

This Week's Examples

  • Literature: Immersive reading of a short play or story — exploring character, theme, and language with Claude alongside the text
  • History: Nick Holmes's Roman History series — using RAG to build a study guide from the actual course material
Session Timeline
0:00–0:10
Opening
From Reading to Dialogue

Brief framing: BOLLI members are experienced, careful readers. AI doesn't replace that — it becomes a conversational reading companion that can answer questions you'd ask a knowledgeable friend. Today we put that to work with real literary and historical texts.

0:10–0:45
Literature Demo + Practice
Immersive Reading of a Short Play or Story

A short literary text is distributed in advance (e.g., a scene from a one-act play, or a short story excerpt — instructor's choice). Students use the Week 2 split-screen setup: text on one side, Claude on the other. Guided prompts: (1) "What is the central tension in this scene?" (2) "What does [character's name] really want here?" (3) "Give me 3 discussion questions I could use with a reading group." (4) "What was the historical or cultural context when this was written?" Students work in pairs (in-room) or independently (Zoom). Share-out: What did Claude add? What did it miss?

0:45–0:55
Concept Introduction
What Is RAG? — A Cosmic Step Forward

Brief, accessible explanation of Retrieval-Augmented Generation. The key insight: basic AI draws only from its training; RAG allows it to draw from your documents. Interactive visualization: Generative AI vs. RAG as "a cosmic step forward." This sets up the history demo.

0:55–1:20
History Demo
RAG in Action: Study Guide for Nick Holmes's Roman History Series

Instructor uploads or pastes materials from Nick Holmes's Imperial Rome course into Claude. Live demo: "Based on these course materials, create a 5-question study guide with answers." Then: "What are the three most important arguments in this series?" Then: "If I wanted to connect this to something happening in the world today, what parallel would you suggest?" Students see RAG move AI from generic knowledge to course-specific intelligence. Discussion: How could an SGL use this to prepare their own course?

1:20–1:30
Wrap-Up
Reflection: AI & the BOLLI Reading Experience

Does AI deepen the reading experience or distract from it? Open discussion. Preview of Week 5: Science and Technology — how AI is transforming medicine and human civilization.

Week 5 · Science & Technology

AI, Medicine & the Shape of Civilization

Understanding AI's Role in Science — and Open Questions for the Road Ahead

📅 90-minute session · 60 min instruction + 30 min open Q&A

Learning Objectives

  • Use AI to explore and decode medical and scientific topics
  • Understand how AI is transforming medical research and diagnosis
  • Explore AI's broader impact on civilization historically and today
  • Reflect on AI as a Gutenberg Moment — a civilizational inflection
  • Ask any remaining questions in an open, structured discussion

This Week's Big Questions

  • How can I use AI to understand a medical condition or diagnosis?
  • Is AI reliable for health information — and when is it not?
  • How has technology changed civilization before, and what's different now?
  • What do we want to hold onto that AI can't give us?
Session Timeline
0:00–0:10
Opening
Course Reflection: Where We've Come From

Brief look back at the arc of the course: from browser basics to AI conversations across five intellectual domains. Students share one thing they'll use going forward. Sets the tone for a reflective, forward-looking final session.

0:10–0:35
Medical Science Demo
AI as a Medical Literacy Tool

Demonstration: How to use Claude to understand a medical diagnosis, medication, or research finding. Guided prompts: (1) "Explain [medical term] in plain language." (2) "What questions should I ask my doctor about [condition]?" (3) "What does the current research say about [treatment]?" Critical framing: AI as a preparation tool, not a substitute for medical advice. When to trust it, when to verify, and when to stop. Students practice with a health topic of their own choosing.

0:35–1:00
Civilization Demo
Technology and the Arc of Civilization

Guided AI conversation on a large theme: "How has transformative technology changed human civilization — from the printing press to the internet to AI?" Demo: asking Claude to draw parallels between the Gutenberg moment and the current AI moment. Students explore: What was gained and lost each time? Instructor introduces the "Gutenberg Moment" framing from the BOLLI course design history. Discussion prompt: Is AI the biggest civilizational shift since the printing press? What's your evidence?

1:00–1:30
Open Discussion
★ Reserved: Open Q&A and Final Reflections

A full 30 minutes set aside for open questions, lingering confusions, and forward-looking discussion. Instructor facilitates but does not lead — this is the students' time. Suggested prompts to spark discussion if needed: "What's one thing you'll do differently after this course?" "What's one thing AI still can't do that matters to you?" "What would you tell a friend about AI Literacy?" Close with the course's animating principle: Experiment.
