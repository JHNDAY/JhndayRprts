Philosophy

"Experiment!" — Cole Porter. The animating principle is learning by doing with a purpose. Active, critical, confident use of AI.

What AI Literacy Actually Means

AI Literacy encompasses three core competencies: mastering the mechanics of computer interfaces (most notably the web browser); understanding the foundational concepts of artificial intelligence; and developing the skill of conversing effectively with AI agents.

Central to this course is the deeply conversational nature of AI. Unlike a Google keyword search, working with AI is an iterative dialogue — each prompt and response builds on the last, peeling back layers as you go deeper into a topic. Equally important: learning when not to trust AI and distinguishing digital intelligence from organic human judgment.

Week 3 · Current Events & Political Discourse

AI as a News Companion & Civic Thinking Partner

From Headlines to Dialogue — Using AI to Deepen Political Understanding

📅 90-minute session · Hybrid Zoom + In-Person

Learning Objectives

  • Use the Current Events Portal as a live AI research tool for breaking news
  • Understand why RAG-powered AI performs differently from basic generative AI on political topics
  • Request structured left/right analysis of a contested policy issue
  • Use the "Fruit Salad" prompting demo to see how prompt construction shapes what AI produces
  • Recognize where AI can help civic thinking — and where it cannot

Key Framing Questions

  • Can AI help us understand people we genuinely disagree with?
  • What changes when AI has access to today's news vs. only its training data?
  • How does the way you phrase a prompt shape the political analysis you get back?
  • Is AI-assisted civic thinking a tool for understanding — or a shortcut around it?
Session Timeline
0:00–0:15
Opening
The Current Events Portal — Live Demo

Instructor opens the Current Events Portal and walks through it as a curated news resource. Demonstration: paste a headline into Claude and ask for background context. Students try with a headline of their choosing. This session is connected directly to the BOLLI Current Events Study Group.

0:15–0:40
Core Activity
Going Deep on a Story: AI as Research Partner

Students select a current news story from the portal. Guided conversation exercise: (1) Ask Claude for a neutral summary. (2) Ask: "What background do I need to understand this?" (3) Ask: "What are the strongest arguments on each side?" Students conduct this 3-step conversation and share back their most interesting moment. Instructor highlights: noticing when AI hedges, citing its sources, and the importance of follow-up questions.

0:40–1:05
Anchor Demo
Political Discourse: Left & Right on a Single Issue

Instructor selects a current policy theme (e.g., immigration, healthcare funding, environmental regulation — not tied to a specific event but to a durable tension). Demo prompt: "Explain the strongest progressive argument for [X]. Then explain the strongest conservative argument. Then tell me where the two sides might actually agree." Class discusses: Does this feel fair? What surprised you? Does using AI change how you think about political disagreement? This exercise models AI as a tool for civic empathy, not political opinion.

1:05–1:20
Critical Thinking
When Not to Trust AI: News, Facts & Hallucinations

Live demonstration of an AI limitation: ask Claude about a very recent event and observe its hedging or error. Discuss: Why does this happen? What is the knowledge cutoff? When should you verify with a primary source? Key skill: knowing when AI is guessing vs. when it genuinely knows.

1:20–1:30
Wrap-Up
Discussion: AI and the Future of Civic Literacy

Brief open discussion: How might AI change the way people engage with news and politics? Is that good or bad? Student reflections and questions.

Week 4 · Literature & History

Reading Deeply, Thinking Historically

Immersive Text Exploration and AI-Powered Study Guides

📅 90-minute session · Hybrid Zoom + In-Person

Learning Objectives

  • Read a literary text — Parker, Jackson, Hemingway, or Lee — with Claude as a conversational companion using the split-screen workflow
  • Bring Gibbon's Decline and Fall into dialogue: upload an extract, let Claude unpack the Latin allusions, trace the argument, and place figures in context
  • Compare Claude's conversational depth with Gemini's research sweep — and know when to reach for each
  • Encounter the Proust framework as a lens on what AI conversation can recover and create: Pensée Perdue, Pensée Nouvelle, Bibliothèque Infinie
  • Distinguish between AI as a reading companion and AI as a summary machine

This Week's Big Questions

  • What can AI add to a literary close reading that you couldn't get any other way?
  • How is uploading a Gibbon passage to Claude different from searching for a summary of Gibbon?
  • When should you use Claude's conversational depth vs. Gemini's research breadth?
  • Can Proust's metaphors — lost thoughts, new thoughts, infinite library — describe what happened when you talked to Claude about a text?

From Reading to Dialogue

Week 5 · Science & Technology

How Science and Technology Shape Civilization

Understanding AI's Role in Science — and Open Questions for the Road Ahead

📅 90-minute session · 60 min instruction + 30 min open Q&A

Learning Objectives

  • Read Turing's 1950 essay "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" with Claude as companion — the paper that first asked whether machines can think, read alongside a machine that thinks
  • Engage with Schrödinger's What is Life (1944) as a model for what happens when a great scientific mind crosses disciplinary boundaries into the unknown
  • Place AI in civilizational perspective using the Gutenberg Moment framing: what changed when information became universally accessible, and what is changing now
  • Understand HTML as the lingua franca that makes AI's visual intelligence visible — the answer to a question five weeks in the making
  • Bring any remaining questions, confusions, or provocations to a full 30-minute open discussion

This Week's Big Questions

  • Turing asked in 1950: "Can machines think?" — 75 years later, what's your answer?
  • Schrödinger crossed from physics into biology and helped spark a revolution. What disciplines is AI crossing into now?
  • Every great information technology — the printing press, the internet, AI — changed who has access to knowledge. What does that mean for us?
  • What do you want to hold onto that AI cannot give you?

Alan Turing — Computing Machinery and Intelligence

The paper that started it all. Written in 1950, Turing's landmark essay introduced the question "Can machines think?" and proposed what became known as the Turing Test. Reading it with Claude alongside is a remarkable experience — you are conversing with a machine about the paper that first asked whether machines could converse. The ideas are surprisingly accessible and the questions Turing raised remain unresolved.

🧬
What is Life — Erwin Schrödinger (1944)

A small book with an enormous legacy. Schrödinger's 1944 Dublin lectures asked whether physics and chemistry could account for the events inside a living cell — and in doing so, helped inspire the molecular biology revolution. The book is credited with drawing a generation of physicists into biology, including Francis Crick. Reading it today with Claude as a companion offers a rare experience: a landmark scientific mind thinking out loud, on the threshold of a discovery he couldn't quite see.

The Gutenberg Moment Framing

A framework for placing AI in civilizational perspective: the printing press didn't just make books cheaper — it rewired who had access to knowledge, how ideas spread, and what became possible in science, religion, and politics. AI is a comparable inflection point. This framing invites a historical rather than anxious conversation about what is actually changing, and why the question of access to intelligence matters as much as intelligence itself.
