Estate Planning Reference

Inheritance Decision Guide

A $2 Million Inheritance — Understanding Your Options

I The Step-Up in Basis Foundation Concept
▶ What Is the Step-Up? The core concept explained

When someone inherits assets, the IRS resets the cost basis of those assets to their fair market value on the date of the decedent's death. This is the "step-up."


Example: The deceased bought stock for $200,000 decades ago. It's now worth $2,000,000. That $1,800,000 in embedded capital gains simply disappears for tax purposes. Your wife inherits it with a $2,000,000 basis.

If she sold it the next day, she would owe zero capital gains tax on that pre-death appreciation. It is one of the most valuable tax benefits in the entire U.S. tax code.

▶ Is the Step-Up Exclusive to the In-Kind Option? A common misconception

No. This is critical to understand. The step-up has already occurred at the moment of death, at the estate level — regardless of how the inheritance is later distributed.


Cash Payout

  • Estate sells assets using the stepped-up basis
  • Estate pays little or no capital gains tax
  • Wife receives clean cash, no embedded liability

In-Kind Transfer

  • Assets transfer with stepped-up basis intact
  • Future gains measured from date of death only
  • Wife controls when she sells

The step-up does not favor either option. The pre-death appreciation is permanently forgiven in both cases.

▶ What Gains Are Still Taxable? Post-death appreciation

Only gains that accrue after the date of death are subject to capital gains tax. The stepped-up basis serves as the new starting point. Any growth from that date forward — whether held by the estate or by your wife — is taxable when eventually sold.

Key implication: The in-kind option gives your wife control over when she realizes those post-death gains — spreading them across tax years, offsetting with losses, etc. That timing control is the real tax advantage of in-kind, not the step-up itself.
II Cash vs. In-Kind — The Choice Decision Point
▶ Accepting In-Kind — Pros & Cons Receive the actual investments

Advantages

  • Retains stepped-up basis on all assets
  • No immediate tax event at transfer
  • Control over timing of future sales
  • Can spread rebalancing across tax years
  • May keep strong assets already in portfolio

Disadvantages

  • May inherit a portfolio that doesn't fit her needs
  • Illiquid assets (real estate, art) bring burdens
  • Ongoing costs on physical assets
  • Valuation complexity for non-traded assets
▶ Accepting Cash — Pros & Cons Receive liquidated proceeds

Advantages

  • Clean, simple, immediately deployable
  • Invest from scratch per her own strategy
  • No inherited complexity or illiquid assets
  • Ideal if she has specific financial goals

Disadvantages

  • Estate absorbs transaction costs and timing risk
  • No control over which assets are sold or when
  • If markets are depressed at liquidation, she gets less
  • Loses strategic ability to time individual sales
▶ Which Is Better? The bottom line

Because the step-up has already eliminated historical embedded gains, in-kind is often the more tax-efficient choice — especially for a large, appreciated portfolio. The question is whether she wants those specific assets.

If the portfolio is sensible and she'd hold similar investments anyway, taking it in-kind and gradually rebalancing is typically the smarter path. If the holdings are illiquid, overly concentrated, or simply inappropriate — cash may be worth the trade-off in simplicity.
III The Prudent Strategy — Take In-Kind & Restructure Gradually Recommended Approach
▶ Why This Approach Makes Sense The rationale

If the inherited portfolio is well-structured and she hasn't yet determined her ideal investment strategy, accepting in-kind preserves all her options while the money continues working for her.


The Core Principle

Don't let urgency be manufactured where none exists. A well-structured inherited portfolio affords the luxury of patience — which is itself a significant financial asset. Hasty decisions with large sums are one of the most common and costly mistakes with sudden wealth.

▶ Benefits of Gradual Restructuring Why slow and deliberate wins
1

Avoids panic decisions. She has time to research and determine what portfolio truly fits her goals, risk tolerance, and timeline.

2

Tax management. Spreading sales across multiple years keeps annual capital gains on post-death appreciation manageable.

3

Discovery value. She may find significant portions of the portfolio are already excellent and worth keeping, saving unnecessary transaction costs.

4

Continuity. Money doesn't sit idle in cash while she figures out what to do — it remains invested throughout.

▶ Immediate Action: Build the Inventory First practical step

Before any restructuring, obtain a clear accounting of the inherited holdings:

1

Individual positions — every holding by name, type, and amount.

2

Stepped-up basis values — the official valuation as of the date of death for each position.

3

Income-generating assets — dividends, bonds, rental income; understand the ongoing cash flows.

4

Illiquid or complex holdings — any real estate, private equity, or alternative investments that require special handling.

This inventory becomes her roadmap for the entire restructuring process.
IV The Fee-Only Fiduciary Financial Advisor Professional Guidance
▶ What Is a Fiduciary? Legal duty explained

Fiduciary Standard

A fiduciary is legally and ethically required to act in your best interest — not their firm's, not their own. They can be held legally accountable if they violate that duty.

This sounds like the baseline standard for any financial advisor. It is not. Many advisors are only held to a "suitability" standard — meaning they can recommend products that are merely suitable for your situation, even if better or cheaper alternatives exist.

▶ What Is "Fee-Only"? How they get paid matters enormously

Fee-Only ✓

  • Paid directly by you — flat fee, hourly, or % of assets
  • No commissions from third parties
  • No referral fees
  • No financial incentive to steer you anywhere

Fee-Based ✗

  • Charges you AND earns commissions on products sold
  • Name is deliberately similar — easy to confuse
  • Inherent conflict of interest on every recommendation
  • May favor products that pay them well over better options
Watch for this: "Fee-based" sounds nearly identical to "fee-only" and is often used deliberately to create confusion. Always ask explicitly: "Do you receive any compensation other than what I pay you directly?"
▶ Why You Need Both Together Neither term alone is sufficient
1

A fiduciary who earns commissions is still conflicted — even with a legal obligation to serve you, financial incentives can influence behavior.

2

A fee-only advisor who isn't a fiduciary has no legal duty to prioritize your interests over theirs or their firm's.

3

You want both simultaneously — the legal obligation and the clean compensation structure, with no financial incentive to steer you anywhere.

▶ How to Find One Practical next steps
1

NAPFA Directory — The National Association of Personal Financial Advisors maintains a vetted list of fee-only fiduciary advisors at napfa.org.

2

CFP Designation — Look for a CERTIFIED FINANCIAL PLANNER credential. CFPs are held to a fiduciary standard when providing financial planning advice.

3

Ask the right question — "Are you a fiduciary 100% of the time, and is your only compensation what I pay you directly?"

4

Given the scale — $2 million warrants professional guidance, particularly during an emotionally complex time. The advisor fee is modest relative to the decision's magnitude.
