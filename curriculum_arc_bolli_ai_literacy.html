Recommended 6-session arc

1

Is it safe to trust? — Demystify and calibrate

Open with the factual-accuracy question head-on. Show hallucination live, then show how to verify. Establishes psychological safety for the fearful and sets honest expectations for everyone. Anchor: "AI is a collaborator, not an oracle."

Barrier overcomers Familiarity seekers Foundation builders Factual accuracy concern
2

First conversation — orientation and basic fluency

Everyone logs in, types a first prompt on a topic they care about. Slow, narrated walk-through for the foundation group; practical appliers get immediate traction with their own interests. Include a 10-min pre-session desktop/browser primer as an optional warm-up resource.

Foundation builders Familiarity seekers Pre-AI computer anxiety
3

Prompting with purpose — from one-liners to dialogue

Move from single questions to multi-turn conversations. Use participants' own interest areas as the raw material (art history, current events, medicine). Introduce iteration: "Ask again, push back, ask for sources." This session directly closes the "limited one-question manner" gap.

Practical appliers Familiarity seekers Foundation builders
4

Research and save — building a personal workflow

Focus on output: how to keep, organise, and revisit what AI helps you produce. Export to a document, build a reference file, create a tour outline or research brief. This is the "saving work in finished form" session the art docents and researchers are waiting for.

Practical appliers Foundation builders Wants to repeat the course
5

Capabilities and limits — what AI can and cannot do

Structured exploration of AI strengths (synthesis, drafting, explanation) vs. weaknesses (recency, specificity, bias). Include visual/multimodal capabilities for those who asked. SGL enrichment: using AI to design course materials and presentations.

Barrier overcomers Practical appliers Familiarity seekers SGL with advanced goals
6

Your AI, going forward — consolidation and next steps

Each participant shares one thing they'll actually use AI for. Revisit session 1's trust questions — what has changed? Provide a one-page "cheat sheet" and signpost where to go next. Build in a 30-day check-in or optional repeat session for those who need it.

Structural adjustments across all sessions

Layered pacing

Each session has a "floor" task (foundation group completes this) and a "ceiling" task (practical/advanced group stretches here). No one waits; no one gets left behind.

Interest-driven examples

Never use generic demo prompts. Use art history, French literature, Roman history, current events — the cohort's own stated interests — as the live examples every session.

Built-in repetition

Each session opens with a 5-min recap of the previous one and a "try this at home" prompt. Addresses the student who said one pass isn't enough.

Pre-session desktop primer

A short optional resource (video or one-pager) covering browser tabs, copy-paste, and window management — sent before session 2 for the student who flagged pre-AI computer gaps.
