Beyond the
Wall of Text

Language models produce language. But human understanding is not primarily linguistic — it is visual, spatial, and instantaneous. HTML bridges the gap.

When a language model responds to a question, it works the way language itself works, which is to say sequentially, one word after another, one sentence after another, one paragraph after another. That is fine for a novel or a letter but it is a poor fit for information that has structure, hierarchy, relationships, or comparative dimensions. The reader has to hold earlier sentences in working memory while processing later ones, mentally assembling a picture that the text never actually draws. The cognitive load is real, and it accumulates fast. The web solved exactly this problem for human-generated content starting in the early 1990s. HTML combined with CSS gave authors three tools that prose simply does not have. Color can signal category membership instantly, before a word is read. Position can show hierarchy, sequence, or opposition through spatial arrangement rather than through connective tissue like "on the other hand" or "in contrast." Typography, meaning choices of weight, size, face, and spacing, can create a visual grammar that tells the eye where to look first, second, and third.

Why the Saw Holds

A picture is worth
a thousand words

The visual cortex is the largest sensory processing system in the brain. It evolved to extract meaning from scenes instantly — not from sentences laboriously.

When information is encoded spatially and chromatically, the brain can pattern-match across the whole field at once. A table comparing six options across five criteria is absorbed in seconds. The equivalent prose can run to several hundred words and still leave the reader uncertain about the overall shape of the comparison.

The Recursive Insight

Using the process
to understand the process

You did not read an argument about HTML rendering. You experienced one. Every color choice on this page, every column, every typographic weight was simultaneously the subject of the claim and the proof of it.

This is what makes the insight recursive: the medium had to demonstrate itself. A prose essay about the limits of prose would be self-defeating. An HTML page about the power of HTML is self-confirming. The process was the understanding.
