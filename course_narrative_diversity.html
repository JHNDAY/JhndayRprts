Henry Morris
Radical Jewish Philosophies: A Legacy of Spinoza
Philosophy & Religion
Explores the evolution of contemporary radical Jewish philosophies (Reconstructionism, neo-Hasidism/Renewal, Feminism/LGBTQ movements) and traces their roots to Spinoza's 17th-century critique of traditional Judaism. Through systematic examination of three central concepts‚ÄîGod, Torah, and Israel‚Äîstudents discover how Jewish ideas evolve in response to new social and historical situations, with Spinoza's philosophy serving as a continuing catalyst for this transformation.
Kate (Ehrlich) Seideman
Women Fighting Injustice
History & Film Studies
Through five fiction films from the US, England, and Australia, this course examines historical examples of women confronting issues of suffrage, workplace discrimination, and abuse. Rather than focusing on individual heroism, the course explores the complex group dynamics of protest movements‚Äîhow women organize, navigate leadership challenges, manage internal conflicts, develop strategies, and mobilize resources to work effectively for their rights.
Judy Feldman
My Good Bright Wolf: Understanding Anorexia Through Memoir
Psychology & Health
A close reading of Sarah Moss's memoir provides students with deep understanding of the psychological, medical, and sociological aspects of anorexia and its treatment. The course examines early signs, family dynamics, life-cycle triggers, social pressures, treatment approaches, and recovery challenges. Students also explore how memoir serves as both therapy and education for patients and families dealing with this complex illness.
Ollie Curme
Understanding and Navigating Dysfunctional Family Relationships
Psychology & Personal Growth
A 10-week exploration of major psychological frameworks for understanding family dysfunction, including Bowen Family Systems Theory, Attachment Theory, and concepts like intergenerational trauma, narcissistic family systems, and codependency. Participants engage with theoretical frameworks while discussing their own experiences, examining topics from psychological boundaries and communication patterns to managing toxic relationships, estrangement, and paths toward healing.
Michael Sherrow
Louisiana Purchase & Lewis and Clark Expedition
American History
A PowerPoint-driven exploration of one of America's most consequential territorial acquisitions and the legendary expedition that followed. The course traces Indian and French history of the territory, the fur trade as commodity, French colonial ambitions, the French and Indian War, Jefferson's vision, Napoleon's problems, the purchase itself, and the complete Lewis and Clark journey from preparation through aftermath. Rich with graphics and historical detail.
Marlene Hobel
Grit & Grace: Short Stories of Larry Brown & Tim Gautreaux
Literature & Southern Studies
An examination of two contemporary masters of Southern storytelling who write from the working-class South but with strikingly different literary styles. Larry Brown's dirty realism offers raw, unflinching portraits of lives on the edge, while Tim Gautreaux's comic realism illuminates moments of grace and resilience amid failure. Reading these writers side by side, students explore how style, worldview, and region shape literary portrayals of class, family, masculinity, and morality in late 20th-century Southern literature.
Steve Ostrow
Roma Aeterna: The Ancient City of Rome
Ancient History & Archaeology
Beyond the breathtaking monuments and imperial grandeur, how did Rome's people actually live? This course explores the Eternal City through its surviving structures‚Äîmausolea, palaces, the Colosseum, bath complexes, aqueducts, and fortification walls‚Äîto uncover the daily realities of different social classes. From emperors to slaves, from native Romans to foreigners, students examine what the city's impressive visible infrastructure reveals about commerce, entertainment, religion, and the lived experience of all who inhabited this ancient metropolis.
Saul Shapiro
Religion and Politics
American History & Political Science
A sweeping 400-year examination of religion's profound influence on American politics, from the Puritans and Pilgrims to Project 2025. The course traces how religious movements have shaped‚Äîand been shaped by‚Äîpolitical developments including abolition, women's suffrage, Civil Rights, and contemporary battles over church-state separation. Through chronological study spanning the Colonial period, Great Awakenings, Civil War era, and the modern alliance of Christian Evangelicals with the political right, students explore how religious institutions and beliefs have served as both progressive and reactionary forces in American democracy.
Lois Ambash
War of Words: Language, Culture, and Public Policy in the Trump Era
Linguistics & Cultural Studies
How has the Trump decade fundamentally reshaped American language, culture, and civic discourse? This course examines the seismic changes in how Americans speak, debate, and understand each other through multiple lenses: social media algorithms, meme culture, visual language, censorship and book banning, conspiracy theories, and the attention economy. From permission structures in political speech to the pace of linguistic change in the digital age, students analyze Trump's pervasive impact on norms, monuments, patriotism, and the very words we're allowed to use‚Äîthen envision what the next decade might bring for American democracy and their grandchildren's future.

  • Judy Feldman (My Good Bright Wolf): Discussion-Based remains High, now clearly aligned with Hobel's approach. Both courses use close reading as the foundation for extensive facilitated discussion.
Pedagogical Insight: This discrepancy reveals why Session 2 (Course Analysis & Optimization) matters.
