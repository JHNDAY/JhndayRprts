Hip pain analysis — greater trochanter area

Why this area hurts during stairs

Your pain is centered at the greater trochanter — where the glute medius, TFL, and IT band all converge and attach to the bone. After 60 years of running:

  • Tight hip flexors constantly pulling forward
  • Weak glute medius can't stabilize the pelvis
  • TFL and IT band overcompensate and pull excessively on this attachment point
  • Stairs demand all three actions at once — the perfect biomechanical storm
The muscle imbalance

Running emphasizes: Hip flexors (repetitive knee drive), quads, calves

Running neglects: Hip abductors (glute medius), which stabilize sideways

Result: Your glute medius is underdeveloped relative to 60 years of flexor work. When you climb stairs — which require stability and abduction — that muscle can't do its job, so everything else (TFL, IT band) overloads.

Why stairs are the worst trigger

Running is mostly straight-line hip flexion. Your body is built for that. Stairs demand simultaneous hip flexion + abduction + stability. Your weak glute medius and tight hip flexors can't coordinate, so the load concentrates at the greater trochanter where all the tendons attach.

The fix

Stretch: Deep hip flexor and IT band work — not just running warm-ups

Strengthen: Glute medius focus — single-leg stands, side planks, clamshells, bridges

Rebalance: You've built 60 years of running fitness. Now redirect it toward hip abduction and stability. A physical therapist who works with older runners can build a program tailored to how you actually move.

This is absolutely fixable. You've proven consistency and adaptability over six decades. Stair pain is just asking you to address one specific weakness—the glute medius—that's developed as a side effect of all that forward motion.

Exercise guide

Hip flexor stretch

Setup: Kneel on one knee (on a cushion or mat). Place the other foot in front of you with knee bent, foot flat on the floor.

Action: Gently shift your hips forward until you feel a stretch along the front of the back hip. Keep your torso upright.

Hold: 30–60 seconds per side. Repeat 2–3 times.

Frequency: Daily, ideally after running when muscles are warm.

IT band foam roll (or massage)

Setup: Sit on the floor with a foam roller under your outer thigh (side of leg). Support yourself with your hands behind you.

Action: Slowly roll the foam roller up and down the outside of your thigh from hip to just above the knee. Pause on tender spots.

Duration: 60–90 seconds per leg. Can also use a lacrosse ball or massage stick.

Frequency: 3–5 times per week.

Single-leg stand

Setup: Stand upright. Hold onto a wall, counter, or chair back for balance if needed.

Action: Lift one leg slightly off the ground. Keep your hips level (don't let the lifted side drop). Stand on the supporting leg.

Duration: Start with 20–30 seconds per leg. Work up to 60 seconds.

Frequency: 3–4 times per week, 2–3 sets per leg.

Progression: Once stable, try closing your eyes or standing on a soft surface (pillow) for added challenge.

Clamshell

Setup: Lie on your side on a mat. Hips and knees bent to about 45 degrees. Feet together, knees apart.

Action: Keeping your feet touching, lift the top knee as high as you can without rolling your hips backward. Pause at the top.

Reps: 12–15 per side, 2–3 sets.

Frequency: 3–4 times per week.

Progression: Add a resistance band around your knees for more challenge.

Side plank

Setup: Lie on your side on a mat. Forearm parallel to your body. Legs extended or knees bent (for easier version).

Action: Press your forearm into the mat and lift your hips off the ground. Your body should form a straight line from head to heels (or knees).

Hold: Start with 15–20 seconds per side. Work up to 45–60 seconds.

Frequency: 3–4 times per week, 2–3 sets per side.

Note: If wrist discomfort occurs, rest on your forearm instead of your hand.

Bridge

Setup: Lie on your back on a mat. Knees bent, feet flat on the floor hip-width apart, arms at your sides.

Action: Press through your heels and lift your hips toward the ceiling. Squeeze your glutes at the top. Lower slowly.

Reps: 12–15 per set, 2–3 sets.

Frequency: 3–4 times per week.

Progression: Hold a weight across your hips, or try single-leg bridges (lift one foot slightly off the ground).

Important: Start gently and progress gradually. If any exercise causes sharp pain (not just muscle fatigue), stop and consult your physical therapist. Pain-free discomfort is fine; sharp pain is a red flag.
