03 — Emerging Counter-Drone Architecture
● Kinetic Swarm (Epstein Vision)

Ukrainian "Shahed Killers"

Mass-produced interceptor drones delivering kinetic energy on impact. Pilots using FPV goggles or automated tracking. The concept Epstein described — realized in a $1,000 commercial package.

Cost/engagement: $1,000–2,000
● Kinetic — Expendable

Coyote Block 2

Jet-powered tube-launched interceptor drone with kinetic warhead. Paired with KuRFS radar for automated target handoff. Cheaper than missiles, but still 500× the cost of a Shahed.

Cost/engagement: ~$100–200K
● Non-Kinetic — Recoverable

Coyote Block 3NK

Defeated 10 drones in Oct 2025 Army demo, then recovered by net for reuse. Employs microwave or EW payload to disable electronics — no warhead, no kinetic impact, redeployable.

Recoverable: reused per sortie
● Directed Energy

Iron Beam (Israel)

Ground-based high-energy laser. Operational since 2025. Engages rockets, mortars, and drones that would otherwise consume expensive missiles. Economics are transformative at scale.

Cost/engagement: ~$3.50 electricity
● Electronic Warfare

RF Jamming / GPS Denial

First line of defense for RF-controlled and GPS-guided drones. Low cost, rapid response, minimal collateral. Effectiveness declining against fiber-optic guided FPV drones — an emerging gap.

Effectiveness: degrading vs. fiber-optic
● Directed Energy — Area Effect

High-Power Microwave (HPM)

Emits bursts that disable electronics across multiple drones simultaneously — ideal against swarms. Unlike lasers (one-at-a-time) or missiles, HPM is inherently swarm-scalable with near-zero marginal cost.

Swarm scalable: wide-area neutralization
04 — Assessment

Strategic Verdict

▶
Epstein's kinetic swarm concept is now operationally real — Ukraine proved it at scale with $1–2K interceptors. The physics were always right; it took commercial drone manufacturing to make it affordable.
▶
The U.S. military's version still has a cost problem: even the Coyote family at $100–200K per unit is not cost-advantageous against a swarm of $200 FPV drones. Volume and price must fall further.
▶
The long-term answer is layered and tiered: cheap swarm interceptors thin the mass; HPM/EW handles swarms; lasers handle high-value single targets; missiles handle ballistic and cruise threats. No single solution wins.
▶
The DARPA MAV program's deeper legacy: it seeded the autonomy, MEMS, and miniaturized propulsion research that now powers both the offensive drone swarms and the next-generation interceptors trying to defeat them.
