Reading Guide · American Short Fiction

The Lottery

Shirley Jackson  ·  The New Yorker, June 26, 1948

Context

  • Published three years after World War II; readers responded with shock and outrage
  • Set in an unnamed American village of ~300 people on a summer morning
  • The lottery is an annual community ritual of long, murky origin
  • Original purpose and procedures have largely been forgotten over generations
  • Other nearby villages have quietly abandoned the practice

Themes to Watch For

  • Blind tradition — continuing a practice simply because it has always been done
  • Conformity and social pressure — the cost of standing apart from the crowd
  • Scapegoating — singling out one individual for the community's benefit
  • The normalization of violence through ritual and bureaucratic routine
  • Fairness — what does it mean, and who decides?

Characters & Roles

  • Mr. Summers — civic official who runs the lottery
  • Mr. Graves — postmaster; assists Mr. Summers
  • Old Man Warner — the village's eldest resident
  • Tessie Hutchinson — a village housewife
  • Bill Hutchinson — Tessie's husband
  • Nancy, Bill Jr., Little Davy — the Hutchinson children
  • Mrs. Delacroix — Tessie's neighbor
  • Mrs. Dunbar — draws in place of her husband
  • Mr. & Mrs. Adams — village couple
  • Bobby Martin — a village boy
