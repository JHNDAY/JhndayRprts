Vienna · 1939 – 1941

Lettersfrom Vienna

Correspondence received by Maximilian (Max) Alter · Translated from German & French

Max Alter was born on March 27, 1925, in Vienna, Austria. At fourteen years old, in the summer of 1939, he emigrated alone to the United States — one step ahead of catastrophe. These ten letters are the only surviving record of his correspondence with his parents and extended family during the years leading up to the Holocaust.

His parents did not survive the war.

The People

A family divided
by an ocean & a war

The letters move between two worlds: Vienna under Nazi occupation and the safety of America, with couriers and intermediaries threading the gap. Each voice is unmistakable.

★ Central Figure
Maximilian "Maxi" Alter

The recipient of nearly every letter. A 14-year-old boy alone in America — living first with the Pressner family, then with Uncle Isi and Uncle Eddy. He is the family's one hope and their only anchor to safety. He must navigate American bureaucracy, solicit relatives for money, and somehow remain a child doing well in school.

📍 United States (New York area)
Mother · Letter Writer
Anna Alter

Max's mother. The most prolific writer in the collection — her voice oscillates between maternal tenderness ("be kissed a thousand times") and barely-suppressed terror about their inability to escape. She reports the letters she sends, worries about whether they arrive, and obsessively tracks the status of ship tickets. She has an unresolved, bitter conflict with "Aunt Selma" — she will not discuss it in writing.

📍 Vienna → Dzialoszice, Poland (occupied)
Father · Letter Writer
Papa Alter

Max's father. Writes with paternal gravity and hard-earned wisdom. In Letter 6, his most personal letter, he urges Max to master English, build his future, not to judge America after only 17 days. He also manages the practical logistics — the K.G. organization, freight costs, ship quota cards. He shares Anna's optimistic targets (end of May!) that are never realized.

📍 Vienna → Dzialoszice, Poland (occupied)
Relative · Vienna
Aron

Writes Letter 2 alongside Anna and Papa's June 3 letter — almost certainly enclosed with it. Possibly Max's uncle. He mirrors the parents' bitterness about Aunt Selma and echoes their desperate appeals for ship tickets. "If we remain alive, we will tell each other everything."

📍 Dzialoszice area, occupied Poland
Relative · Vienna
Josef & Family

Authors of Letter 4, written from Vienna in late 1939/early 1940. Josef writes to American relatives with a detailed and anguished account of the failed attempt to secure ship tickets through "Karta" (quota cards). He explains the Joint Distribution Committee's system meticulously, as though he has memorized it. He signed at the bottom; his wife wrote the main letter.

📍 Vienna, Austria
Relay · Germany
Aunt Gusti Krauthammer

Lives in Bottrop, Germany (Ruhr region), away from the occupied territories. Acts as an intermediary — relaying information between Max in America and his parents, who can no longer write freely from Dzialoszice. Her letter (Letter 5) is typed on personal stationery and contains the clearest statement of the parents' exact location: Ul. Krakówska 23, Dzialoszice.

📍 Bottrop, Ruhr, Germany
Human Courier
Martha Hermann

One of the most poignant figures in the collection — a woman who physically carried a letter from Max's parents across Europe to reach him. She writes her own brief note (Letter 10) to accompany the delivered letter, passing on greetings and begging Max to send his parents any sign of life. Her son Erich and daughter-in-law Rosa also appear as possible relay contacts.

📍 Vienna → Budapest (carried letters by hand)
American Relatives
Uncle Isi, Uncle Eddy, Uncle Max, Aunt Fannie

The critical nodes of the American support network. Max lives with at least two of them. They are repeatedly appealed to for the one thing that could save the family: ship tickets and guarantee papers. The difference between their acting and not acting is — in the letters' desperate logic — the difference between life and death.

📍 United States

August 25, 1939
Written BY Max · Paris, France
Letter 7 — Petition to the Prefect of Police
"I arrived in France with a transit card issued by HAPAG, an American visa, and a passage ticket from Cherbourg to New York on the SS 'DEUTSCHLAND.' I possess sufficient means and will in no case become a burden on the public."
The only letter written BY Max. He is 14, alone in Paris, stranded when the SS Deutschland cannot sail because World War II has just begun. He formally petitions the Paris police in fluent, formal French for temporary residence — a 14-year-old navigating bureaucracy alone. His ship ticket, his visa, his whole escape plan: suspended by the outbreak of war.

Letter 8 — Through Switzerland
"Since we have received no news from Lia, nor from the dear Pressner family, about how you are, my precious child — we are trying sending a letter via Switzerland, and hope that it will reach you."
Just one day after Britain and France declared war on Germany, Anna writes in deep worry. Direct postal service has been severed. She routes this letter through Dr. Behrens, a physician in Davos, Switzerland — a neutral country — including instructions for Max to use a double-envelope system so replies can be forwarded back. The war is one day old and communication is already fractured.

Late 1939 / Early 1940
From: Josef & Family · Vienna, Austria
Letter 4 — The Ticket System Explained
"The Joint registers the cards of the local Jewish community organization in our names. Because in the event that one is not able to travel, the deposited money is returned to you. So you, from the Joint, receive it back without loss."
Josef writes from Vienna with a meticulous explanation of the "Karta" (quota card) system. The Joint Distribution Committee in New York holds deposits; the Vienna Jewish community organization registers the names. Josef has clearly studied this system obsessively — it is the mechanism by which freedom can be purchased. He begs the American relatives to pay in two cards. His Christmas memory of their visit is heartbreaking against the current reality.

From: Anna (Mother) · Vienna
Letter 3 — "Be Glad You Are Not Here"
"My good child, what should I write you at length — we only wish that you stay healthy. Be glad that you are not here. The times are bitter and hard."
Anna writes warmly but with unmistakable anguish beneath the surface. She wonders if Mrs. Dr. Friedman visited Max as promised. She suggests a new forwarding route through Stefan Singer. She mentions the ship ticket anxiety again. Most strikingly: she tells Max to "be glad you are not here" — a rare moment where the terror of Vienna 1940 breaks through the maternal composure. She closes with greetings from a circle of named acquaintances, a world that will soon be gone.

December 1940
From: Papa · Vienna · Letter Nr. 3
Letter 6 — "You, My Only One"
"Now the serious time begins, and strive, my son, to be a good and decent person. You must still attend school, and make sure to master English in speech and writing — and perhaps also a world language — and then one can speak about one's future."
The most personal letter in the collection, opening with an astonishing salutation: "You, my only one that I have." Papa replies to Max's November 1939 letter, clearly Max had expressed homesickness and uncertainty. Papa's response is both wise and heartbreaking — he tells Max not to judge America after 17 days, urges him to study, plans their arrival for "end of May." He discusses freight costs, crates, neighbors who have gone to Israel. He has no idea end of May will come and go without them leaving.

"They have been for a short time now with Aunt Selme in Dzialoszice, District of Miechow, ul. Krakówska 23. They warmly ask you to write to them as soon as possible at this address. They themselves have no opportunity to write from there."

What Runs Through Every Letter

The recurring
architecture of desperation

These letters are not casual correspondence. Each one is a carefully constructed plea within a bureaucratic and political maze. The same themes return, letter after letter, because the same problems were never solved.

🚢
Ship Tickets

The central obsession of the collection. Two tickets. Always two — one for each parent. The process: American relatives pay into the Joint Distribution Committee in New York. The Vienna Jewish community organization registers the applicants' names. "Karta" quota cards must be filed. The deposit is refundable if travel fails — a detail Josef explains carefully to reassure reluctant relatives. In Letter 1, $300 has been paid for one ticket. The second is still missing. They never sailed.

8
✉️
Routes & Relays

Direct post between Vienna/occupied Poland and the USA was unreliable, censored, or impossible. The family built an improvised infrastructure: Swiss physicians, Yugoslav P.O. boxes, Budapest pension addresses, human couriers, family members in Germany (Bottrop) who could still communicate more freely. Each letter carefully instructs Max on where to write next. The logistics of staying in contact were themselves a full-time occupation.

6
📋
Papers & Documents

Emigration required: guarantee papers from American sponsors, valid visas, passage tickets, quota cards registered with the Joint, consular approvals that expired and had to be renewed. By March 1941 (Letter 5), the parents' papers have expired. Aunt Fannie must be contacted immediately. The bureaucratic requirements for leaving were enormous — and deliberately so. Every expired document was another month of danger.

5
💔
The Selma Conflict

A mystery in the letters: "Aunt Selma" is a source of profound, bitter, apparently irreconcilable conflict for both Anna and Aron. "Aunt Selma is, for me, finished forever — I do not wish to see her in my lifetime." Yet the parents' address is in Dzialoszice, at Selma's house. They are trapped near someone they despise, probably because it is the only place they have. The conflict is never explained — "if we remain alive, we will tell each other everything."

4
📍
Where Is Max?

Max moves between households across the letters: first with the Pressner family, then with Uncle Isi, then with Uncle Eddy. His parents track these moves anxiously — relieved he is with family, worried about his wellbeing, grateful to the relatives who shelter him. "We know that things are better for my precious child there." His parents' constant, tender concern for him is the emotional engine of every letter.

7
🙏
Conditional Survival

A quiet grammatical horror runs through the letters: "if we live," "if we remain alive," "perhaps we will see each other once more in our lives," "if God wills it." These are not theatrical expressions of grief. They are honest assessments of an increasingly clear reality — that survival is not guaranteed, and that the writers know it. The hedging grows more pronounced in the 1941 letters. It is the sound of people who have understood what is happening.

The Escape Mechanism

How emigration worked —
and why it failed

The parents were not passive. They worked every channel available. The system they navigated was real — and deliberately obstructed at nearly every turn by Nazi bureaucracy, quota limits, and the sheer cost of freedom.

1
American Relatives Pay

Uncles and Aunts in America must deposit funds at the Joint Distribution Committee (JDC) in New York — enough for ship tickets for each person emigrating.

2
The Joint Registers

The JDC registers "Karta" quota cards in the emigrants' names through the local Vienna Jewish Community Organization. Funds are theoretically refundable if emigration fails.

3
Consulate Issues Visa

American consulate in Vienna must approve and issue visas — with guarantee papers provided by American sponsors (Aunt Fannie, the uncles). These expire and must be renewed. By March 1941 they had expired.

4
K.G. Telegraphs New York

The K.G. (a Jewish aid organization in Vienna — likely IKG, the Israelitische Kultusgemeinde Wien) telegraphs relatives and coordinates. Papa notes that K.G. may pay half the ticket if relatives pay the other half.

5
Book Passage

A ship must be booked. By 1939–41, the choices were dwindling rapidly. Max himself had a ticket on the SS Deutschland in August 1939 — that ship could not sail because war broke out.

✗
Window Closes

By June 1941, the United States was moving toward war. Germany would close emigration for Jews in October 1941. The window — always narrow — had shut.

Geography of Displacement

From Vienna to Dzialoszice

The letters trace a movement across the map of occupied Europe. Max begins in Vienna, flees to Paris, reaches New York. His parents begin in Vienna and end in Dzialoszice — a small market town in the Miechow District of German-occupied Poland, southeast of Kraków.

ORIGIN
Vienna, Austria

After the Anschluss of March 1938, Vienna's Jewish community of ~200,000 came under Nazi rule. Systematic persecution, forced emigration, and deportation followed. By the summer of 1939, Max fled with a one-way ticket.

MAX'S ROUTE
Vienna → Paris → New York

Max traveled via Paris (21 rue de Calais) where he was stranded when the SS Deutschland could not sail at the outbreak of war. He eventually reached New York by other means, living first with the Pressner family, then with American relatives.

PARENTS' FATE
Vienna → Dzialoszice

By early 1941, Anna and Papa had been moved to Dzialoszice, Miechow District, German-occupied Poland — at the address of "Aunt Selma." On September 2–3, 1942, the Jews of Dzialoszice were deported to the Bełżec extermination camp. Of 10,000 Jewish residents, 4,000 were shot on the spot. The rest perished in the camps.

What Happened After the Last Letter

The silence after
June 3, 1941

The last letters in this collection are dated June 3, 1941 — written from Dzialoszice in occupied Poland. After this point, direct correspondence between Jews in occupied Poland and the United States became nearly impossible. Germany would formally prohibit Jewish emigration in October 1941. The window had closed.

In the letters, Max's parents speak of ship tickets still unpaid, guarantee papers expired, the K.G. still negotiating. They speak of "end of May" targets that have passed, of $300 deposited for one ticket when two were needed. They were not passive — they worked every mechanism available to them. But the bureaucratic and financial requirements for emigration were insurmountable in the time they had.

Max's Later Life

Maximilian Alter served in the United States Army, was part of the Army Specialized Training Program (ASTP), and then fought in Patton's Third Army — crossing the Rhine River shortly before Germany's surrender. He returned home after the war, married, earned a degree from Boston University on the GI Bill, and built a life in America. He carried these ten letters with him for the rest of his life. They were the only record of his parents that survived.
