Aging Across Generations

How Society's Views Shape Us All
5 Weeks
90 Minutes Weekly
In-Person at BOLLI
Intergenerational

Course Overview

Audience: BOLLI members and Brandeis students from Professor Sarah Lamb's Anthropology Lab course

Context: The demographics of the United States show our society is aging, with people 80+ years the fastest growing cohort. Census data suggests that the 80+ age group will outnumber the under 20 age group by 2050.

Core Questions: What are the ramifications of this demographic shift? Is an aging society a problem or an opportunity? What kind of society are we building for the future?

Approach: Conversation through an intergenerational lens will balance theory and lived experiences, enriching all perspectives. We'll examine demographics, ageism, invisibility, generational blame, governmental policies, historical perspectives, and intentional intergenerational communities.

Week 1

The Demographic Reality and What It Means for Everyone
▼

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the "age wave" and its implications across all age groups
  • Explore how demographic shifts create interdependencies between generations
  • Begin building intergenerational dialogue skills

Session Structure

Opening Activity 15 min
Paired introductions - one BOLLI member, one student share "a time when age mattered to me"
Content/Discussion 40 min
  • Brief presentation of demographic data (2050 projections)
  • Small group discussions: "What assumptions did you make about your future at age 20? 40? 60?"
  • How do demographic realities differently impact someone at 25 vs. 75?
Interactive Exercise 25 min
Timeline activity - groups map societal attitudes about aging across different decades (1960s-present)
Reflection/Homework 10 min
Each participant identifies one media example (ad, TV show, article) that portrays aging and brings to next session

Week 2

The Construction of "Old" - Ageism and Cultural Narratives
▼

Learning Objectives

  • Recognize ageism in its various forms (toward both old and young)
  • Understand how cultural narratives about aging are constructed and internalized
  • Examine invisibility and visibility across the lifespan

Session Structure

Media Review 20 min
Gallery walk of examples brought by participants - identify patterns and stereotypes
Facilitated Discussion 35 min
  • What is "old"? How has this changed? Who decides?
  • Explore ageism directed at youth ("OK Boomer") and at elders (dismissal, patronizing)
  • Student perspectives: "What do you fear about aging?"
  • BOLLI perspectives: "When did you first feel 'old' to society?"
Lived Experience Sharing 25 min
Intergenerational pairs share stories of feeling invisible or hypervisible due to age
Closing 10 min
Journal prompt: "How do I participate in ageist thinking?"

Week 3

Generational Interdependence vs. Generational Blame
▼

Learning Objectives

  • Move beyond generational stereotypes (Boomers vs. Millennials, etc.)
  • Understand economic and policy structures that create intergenerational tension
  • Identify areas of shared interest across age groups

Session Structure

Opening Debate Activity 25 min
Mixed-age teams debate propositions like "Social Security is unsustainable" or "Young people have it harder than previous generations" - then switch sides
Policy Exploration 30 min
  • Healthcare, housing, education, climate - how do age cohorts experience these differently?
  • What structural issues create real conflicts? What conflicts are manufactured?
Finding Common Ground 25 min
World café stations where rotating groups identify shared concerns (economic security, meaningful work, community connection, environmental future)
Synthesis 10 min
Class creates collective list: "What all generations need to thrive"

Week 4

Historical Perspectives and Alternative Models
▼

Learning Objectives

  • Examine how other cultures and time periods have structured intergenerational relationships
  • Explore intentional intergenerational communities and programs
  • Envision alternatives to age segregation

Session Structure

Comparative Perspectives 30 min
  • Brief presentations on multigenerational living patterns (historical American, contemporary global examples)
  • Guest speaker option: Representative from intergenerational housing or program
Case Studies 30 min
Small groups examine different models:
  • Intergenerational learning programs
  • Co-housing communities
  • Mentor/reverse mentor programs
  • Shared spaces (community centers, maker spaces)
Design Thinking Exercise 25 min
"If we were building an intergenerational community, what would it include?" Groups create visual maps
Reflection 5 min
What one element would you personally want to participate in?

Week 5

Building the Future - Personal and Collective Action
▼

Learning Objectives

  • Synthesize course learning into actionable insights
  • Identify personal commitments and collective possibilities
  • Celebrate intergenerational connection

Session Structure

Course Reflection Circle 30 min
  • What surprised you? What changed for you?
  • What stereotypes did you release?
  • Share one insight from someone of a different generation
Action Planning 35 min
  • Individual: "One thing I'll do differently"
  • Collective brainstorm: "What could BOLLI/Brandeis do to foster intergenerational connection?"
  • Volunteers form small task force to develop proposal for ongoing intergenerational programming
Final Sharing 20 min
"Letter to the Future" - participants write brief messages about the intergenerational society they want to build (option to share aloud)
Closing Celebration 5 min
Appreciation circle

Ongoing Elements Throughout Course

▼

Weekly Practices

  • Rotating intergenerational seating each week
  • "Story of the Week" - volunteer shares 5-minute personal narrative related to theme
  • Shared Google Doc or folder for resources, articles, and reflections

Assessment/Participation

  • Attendance and engaged participation
  • One reflection paper (2-3 pages) connecting course themes to personal experience
  • Contribution to final session action planning

Recommended Resources

▼
  • Readings from Ashton Applewhite's This Chair Rocks
  • Selected articles from Prof. Sarah Lamb's research
  • Demographics graphics and data visualizations
  • Media clips illustrating ageism and alternative narratives

Course Philosophy: This structure emphasizes dialogue over lecture, balances theory with lived experience, and uses the intergenerational composition of the class as its greatest resource.

Each session includes active learning and opportunities for both age groups to learn from and teach each other.

Developed in collaboration with Professor Sarah Lamb, Brandeis University Anthropology Department
