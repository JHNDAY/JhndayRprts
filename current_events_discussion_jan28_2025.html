‚öñÔ∏è Alex Pretti Murder: A Potential Tipping Point

‚ñº
Extended discussion of Alex Pretti's murder by ICE agents, examining whether this could be a transformational moment like Emmett Till or Kent State

Who Was Alex Pretti?

John: "A young man who was obviously, from all accounts, an exemplary person. The type of person that you would want your children, nieces, nephews to emulate. He was a wonderful person, and he was just murdered."
  • Profession: VA ICU nurse
  • Character: "Young man of unsaleable character and a responsible gun owner"
  • Context: Murdered while at protest
  • Previous incident: About a week before his death, confronted by ICE agents who sat on him and broke a rib (he was released but injured)

Bipartisan Congressional Response

John showed NPR article documenting emerging bipartisan reaction:

  • Republican senators: Tom Tillis, Bill Cassidy speaking out
  • House: Representative Thomas Massey
  • State level: Minnesota Republican gubernatorial candidate walked out and issued strong statement - "didn't want to be affiliated with people who would do these kinds of things"
  • Senate action: Senate Democrats blocking DHS funding in response
John: "The Opposition is appearing even on the Republican side, and these things start as a small trickle sometimes, and they grow."

Jennifer Rubin Article: "Why for Too Many, Alex Pretti's Murder Counted More"

George shared key excerpts emphasizing need to find sympathy for those unlike ourselves

Rubin: "Sadly, a larger swath of Americans, white men and gun owners, for example, might only have been stirred when the victim turned out to be someone who closely resembled themselves and people they know."

New term introduced: AWFUL - Affluent White Female Urban Liberal

George: "I had never seen that before. I think we probably have a number of awful people on this Zoom."
Rubin's conclusion: "The moral disgrace comes from refusing to recognize that those bearing little or no similarity to us deserve respect, care, and defense simply because they're human."

George: "I was somewhat moved. I thought it was an excellent article."

Historical Parallel: Emmett Till

John: Emmett Till case from 1955 - young Black man murdered in the South after being thought to harass white woman. Mother courageously demanded open casket funeral to show his mangled body. "It was one of those moments where the national conscience, certainly on race, changed. It didn't bring about the civil rights movement, but it certainly moved it along."

Parallels to Pretti case: Obviously many differences (Pretti was grown man, at protest by choice), but potential for similar catalytic effect

The Central Question

John: "The question isn't whether Alex Pretti's case is identical to Emmett Till's. The question is whether we're witnessing the moment when enough people say not one more. And the political cost of maintaining the status quo becomes unbearable."

Why Hasn't This Been a "Kent State Moment"?

Bob Fishman's question: "Where are the college students? It's usually the college students... whether it was anti-Vietnam, whether it was anti-apartheid, whether it was George Floyd... but I've seen nothing about that so far on either of these killings."

Possible reasons discussed:

  • David Rattner: College students don't have "same skin in the game" - males in Vietnam era had draft lottery numbers, friends being called up, friends shot. Much more immediate threat.
  • Ralph Loomis: Works weekly with group of half adults, half teenagers/college kids for 3 years - hasn't found one who's gone to demonstration or has a sign. "Their parents are individuals who have not demonstrated... Kids today don't see any of that, nor do they hear anything about that in their family, or among their friends."
  • Scott: His children at Brandeis/MIT saw Gaza protests but "wisely stayed away from both sides." Social media has fragmented unified narrative - "We don't have a unified kind of media... Everybody's absorbed in the social media." Also: "The oligarchs... learned, let's not have a draft" - system learned from mistakes.
  • Bob: Wondered if Trump has "terrorized" or "cowed" college activists, given what he's doing to universities and deportations

Kent State Historical Context

Sally: None of those cops were prosecuted. Kent State happened earlier in Vietnam War - if it happened in 1971-72 might have had different reaction. Also, video on phones didn't exist then - might have changed response.

David Rattner (who was there): Actually there WAS a lot of video of Kent State - images very widespread in Time Magazine (number one publication at time) and Walter Cronkite on CBS News. This was around time of bombing Cambodia and Laos.

Taking Trump Seriously

Sally: "If you assume that Trump is crazy, demented or whatever... assume he's not sane... our news media... are sort of treating him seriously. When he says these crazy things, they take it seriously and react like he's a normal human being. But I think he's not."

David's Forceful Response

David: "Whether you think Trump is demented or not, underestimate him at your peril. I take everything he says seriously. Because he will follow through, whether it's a crazy idea or not, he's a fighter, and he's committed... while I may agree with you or have my suspicion about his mental competency, I would never underestimate him."

Tipping Points & Historical Precedents

Francesca: Referenced Judge Welch and McCarthy - "Have you no decency, sir, at long last?" That really started something, "unleashed a dam" of people against McCarthy.

Francesca: "In every instance, there has to be a tipping point where things just... people won't take it anymore. But I don't know... we have to depend on Republicans to not take it anymore if anything's gonna get done in Congress."

Susan Wagner (independent, has voted Republican): "This is a little bit over the top... it's just too much. I don't know what the president thinks he's doing. The killings just make it worse."

Labels & Extremism

Francesca on "AWFUL" label: "I hate labels of any kind. I was an independent voter for many, many years... I don't care for extremism of any kind, because the world really can't function that way, and we need to see human beings as human, not the other."

Francesca: "Extremism, whether it is from the far right or the far left, is just plain toxic."

George: "From the day Trump walked down the escalator 10 years ago, he made a name for himself by campaigning against the other."

Francesca: That's what set his whole campaign and presidency - "gave those who wanted it permission to begin to do acts of violence... more than a 100% increase in violence in these incidents."

Selective Outrage? Israel Dimension

Ron Marcus: Noted that students demonstrate about Gaza/Palestine but not about Iran, Sudan, Mozambique, or other places with persecution. "There's no question that if Israel is not involved, there are no demonstrations from students."

Ralph Loomis: Disagreed that Gaza protests are purely anti-Semitic - "Certainly part of it is," but polling suggests "deep belief that what's happening over there is unfair to the Palestinians... a lot of Jewish people who are with them." Also noted protests were concentrated in maybe 20-30 schools, not widespread.

üö® ICE Warrants & Fourth Amendment Rights

‚ñº
David Rattner led extended discussion on Wall Street Journal article about ICE claiming authority to enter homes without judicial warrants - sparked major constitutional debate

The Wall Street Journal Article

David's Introduction: Presented article arguing ICE doesn't need judicial warrants to enter homes for immigration enforcement

Key Claim: Article suggests illegal aliens don't have same Fourth Amendment protections as citizens

David positioned himself as "devil's advocate" - presenting argument group would likely oppose to stimulate critical thinking

Geoffrey's Critical Question

Geoffrey/Hilary: "If someone has applied for asylum, are they considered, by definition, illegal aliens at that point, or not?... If they've applied for asylum, then maybe they do have the right."

Highlighted potential hedge in WSJ argument - distinction between "illegal aliens" and asylum applicants

David's Response: Judicial Hearings

David: "I mean, as I said, I'm a doctor, not a lawyer... what's in the article says that these people have already had a judicial hearing before an immigration judge, and their petition's been denied."

If petition already denied, individuals may be here illegally

Acknowledged: "I'm not an immigration lawyer, and I'm sure there's lots of nuances to this"

Sally's Critical Intervention

Sally: "When you say administrative judge, do you mean a real judge, or are you talking about these people that are actually not part of our judiciary?"

Key distinction: Immigration judges are NOT part of federal judiciary

They're administrative employees of the Justice Department, not independent judges

Scott's Comprehensive Analysis

Scott emphasized need for sophisticated source analysis:

"We have to be careful when reading rhetoric coming from the administration or the Wall Street Journal... We should look into that, and then also prepare with responses to it."

Referenced USA Today: "ICE says it doesn't need judicial warrants to enter homes"

USA Today noted this interpretation "would upend legal scholars' interpretation for a long time"

Scott on Fourth Amendment: "The right of the people to be secure in their persons... you can question, what is 'the people'? Is it anybody who lives in the country, or is it just citizens?"

Practical Problem: "You don't have a judicial warrant to enter a house, you don't actually know who's in the house yet... there's a lot of U.S. citizens caught up in this stuff"

Examples of Citizens Affected

  • George: "There was the Filipino guy who got dragged out of the house in his underwear and his flip-flops"
  • Sally: Corrected - "Vietnamese guy. The Hmong... in his underpants"

Demonstrated how enforcement affects U.S. citizens and legal residents, not just undocumented individuals

David's Nuanced Conclusion

David: "Everybody is condemning ICE in this group... my knee-jerk reaction is it's pure revenge and spite. When you actually read what their attorneys are saying and what the briefs are saying, they have a point, and you have to acknowledge it."

Harvard Example: Referenced Globe's Spotlight series on Harvard negotiations with federal government

"You start to understand at least where the government's coming from"

"It wasn't a slam dunk for Harvard, and it's not a slam dunk for the government either"

üï∞Ô∏è Kent State Through Different Eyes

The Living Memory: David Rattner brought the weight of lived experience - "I was right there for the Kent State thing." His first-hand account reminded the group that for some participants, these aren't just historical reference points but personal memories. The images in Time Magazine, Walter Cronkite's coverage during the Cambodia and Laos bombings - these weren't textbook entries but the news of their youth.

The Draft's Shadow: What made Kent State different wasn't just the violence - it was the existential threat hanging over an entire generation. As David explained, "For the males on this call, many of us had draft lottery numbers, and our friends were being called. If their number was under 100, you were likely to end up serving, and so we all had friends who were shot."

The Question That Haunts: Bob Fishman's query - "Why wasn't this a Kent State moment?" - captured the session's central generational puzzle. The group grappled with why Alex Pretti's murder, despite its clarity and horror, hasn't sparked the mass mobilization that defined their youth.

üéì Where Are the College Students?

The Pattern Breakers: Bob noted the historical pattern - "It's usually the college students, the mobilization, whether it was anti-Vietnam, whether it was anti-apartheid, whether it was George Floyd." College campuses provided natural staging areas where young people, aggregated together, could organize and respond. But this time? Silence.

Ralph's Three-Year Window: Perhaps most telling was Ralph Loomis's sustained observation. For three years, meeting weekly with a group half adults, half teenagers and college kids, he reported: "I haven't found one that has gone out to a demonstration, or who's got a sign." The absence isn't theoretical - it's empirical, observed week after week.

Susan's Gentle Reminder: When the discussion grew heated, Susan offered perspective: "I think it might take a little time for all of this to congeal." Perhaps the group's generation, schooled in protest, expects immediate mobilization. Today's students may operate on different timelines.

üë®‚Äçüë©‚Äçüëß‚Äçüë¶ Our Children, Our Grandchildren

Scott's Personal Lens: Scott brought the discussion into intimate territory, sharing about his own children - one a senior at Brandeis, another a graduate student at MIT. They saw the Gaza protests, "but they wisely stayed away from both sides." His kids represent a generation that sees the politics, understands the complexity, but chooses not to engage in the same ways their parents' generation did.

The Weekly Protests Scott's Family Attends: While his children abstain, Scott and his wife go to protests at Newton Center every week. The generational inversion is striking - parents marching while adult children stay home. "But my kids don't want to go," he explained, describing a gap not of values but of methodology.

Ralph's Cross-Generational Dialogue: Ralph's ongoing engagement with young people provided ground-level intelligence. These kids are "very busy... they've got all kinds of things to do." But more fundamentally, "there's nothing that they see, or feel, or understand that would make any sense" of protest. Their parents haven't demonstrated. They haven't seen it modeled. The tradition is broken.

üîÄ What's Changed: The Structural Shifts

No Skin in the Game

David's insight cut to the core: "I think the college students are not invested in the same way because they don't actually have the same skin in the game." No draft means no existential threat. The system learned from Vietnam - as Scott noted, "The oligarchs... learned, let's not have a draft."

Fragmented Media

Scott emphasized the transformation: "We brought up Walter Cronkite... we don't have a unified kind of media. Everybody's absorbed in the social media." Where once the nation watched the same newscast, now everyone inhabits their own information bubble. Collective outrage requires collective witness.

The Virtual World

Scott described how "the social media, the virtual world is the new, powerful world that the oligarchs are creating." Young people aren't apathetic - they're navigating a fundamentally different reality. "The world is virtual," and the old forms of physical gathering and protest feel increasingly antiquated.

Demoralized, Not Apathetic

Scott was emphatic: "I don't think the kids are apathetic. But they're kind of demoralized." They're watching older generations fail to hold power accountable. Bob wondered if Trump has "terrorized" or "cowed" college activists through attacks on universities and deportations. Fear may masquerade as disengagement.

‚ôªÔ∏è What's the Same: Timeless Patterns

The Technology Debate: Sally raised a fascinating parallel - Kent State guardsmen weren't prosecuted either. She wondered if video phones had existed then, "you might have had a different reaction." David countered that actually there WAS widespread video - Time Magazine, Walter Cronkite. The technology changes, but perhaps not the fundamental dynamics of accountability.

The Tipping Point Question: Francesca invoked Joseph Welch confronting McCarthy: "Have you no decency, sir, at long last?" Every era searches for its catalytic moment. John framed the Alex Pretti case through this lens: "The question is whether we're witnessing the moment when enough people say not one more."

Awaiting the Trickle to Become a Flood: John noted the bipartisan congressional response and observed, "these things start as a small trickle sometimes, and they grow." The group has lived long enough to see how social change actually unfolds - not in thunderclaps but in accumulations.

The Gut-Wrenching Constant: Despite all the changes, John's words captured what remains: "It's gut-wrenching and heartbreaking to see these things happening." Across generations, the emotional response to injustice endures. Perhaps that's what the group offers each other - a space to feel that gut-wrenching heartbreak collectively.

üí≠ The Conversation's Gift

This discussion revealed the unique value of a multi-generational group: those who remember Kent State firsthand sitting with those who observe today's students closely, parents who march while their children abstain, all trying to understand both continuity and rupture. The richness came not from reaching consensus but from the layers of perspective - lived experience, parental concern, historical pattern-recognition, and genuine puzzlement about a changing world. In trying to understand why Alex Pretti's murder hasn't sparked the mobilization they remember, the group illuminated something perhaps more important: how each generation must find its own forms of resistance, operating within constraints and possibilities their elders couldn't have imagined.
