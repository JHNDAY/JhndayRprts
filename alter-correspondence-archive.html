The Letters

Max Alter and His Family · Vienna to New York · 1939–1957

✦
August 1939

The Boy Who Left

On August 26, 1939, a fourteen-year-old named Maximilian Alter boarded a train in Vienna and left for Paris, traveling alone. He carried a HAPAG transit card, an American visa, and a ticket for the SS Deutschland, sailing from Cherbourg to New York. His parents, Jakob and Anna, stayed behind. No one expected the separation to last.

Six days later, Germany invaded Poland. The SS Deutschland did not sail. Within the week, Britain and France had declared war on Germany, and Max was stranded in a city that was no longer at peace, with documents that no longer had a clear purpose.

He typed a letter in French to the Prefect of Police of Paris, requesting temporary residence. The letter is careful and grammatically correct. He explains that he has all necessary emigration papers — visa, transit card, ship ticket — but that "the departure of the Deutschland cannot take place due to current events." He asks permission to remain until he can find another passage. He signs it in full: Maximilian Alter. It is the only document in the collection written in his own voice.

He was eventually given his passage and arrived in New York sometime in early September 1939, moving in with the Weitz family at 1725 Gleason Avenue in the Bronx.

Autumn 1939

Trying to Find Each Other

The mail between Vienna and the rest of the world had not yet stopped entirely, but it had become unreliable. On September 4, three days after the invasion of Poland, Anna Alter sat down and wrote to her son. She could not reach him directly, so she addressed the letter to a Dr. Behrens in Davos, Switzerland, asking him to forward it. She had not heard from Max since he left.

Our only wish — at least to receive one letter from you — grows greater with each passing day. — Anna Alter, Vienna, September 4, 1939

She wrote the forwarding instructions twice, carefully. A note added in the margin by a separate hand reads: Please, please keep writing — your mother. Three weeks later, she sent another letter carried by hand out of Vienna by a woman named Martha Hermann traveling west.

On October 7, Anna turned to a more official channel. She submitted a message to the International Committee of the Red Cross in Geneva, asking them to contact a family friend in Paris — Jules Pressner — to find out whether her son had arrived safely. The case was assigned number CM 3776.

Mme. A. would very much like to know if her son Maximilien Alter, who departed from Vienna on the 26th of August 1939, has arrived in Paris and whether he was able to depart for America. — Anna Alter via ICRC, October 7, 1939

Max, on the other side of the Atlantic, was doing the same thing in reverse. By late November 1939, he had submitted his own inquiry through the Red Cross and received a formal acknowledgment card: case number CM 22155. He was fourteen, alone in a foreign city, using the machinery of international humanitarian organizations to locate his parents.

We acknowledge receipt of your communication. We will do our best to follow it up accordingly. — ICRC Geneva to Max Alter, November 21, 1939
1940

The Ship Ticket Problem

By early 1940, Max was settled in the Bronx. From Vienna, the letters had a different texture. On March 11, Jakob wrote the most personal letter in the collection, labeled in his own hand "Letter No. 3." He addressed his son as "my Only One, what I have." He told Max to study, to master English, to learn another world language. He was trying to be a practical father across a very large distance. He also explained the machinery that could save them.

The Community has sent a telegram to New York regarding ship tickets for the relatives. It works out that when the relatives pay half, the Community pays the rest. We hope, my dear child, to be with you in a short time — end of May is the plan now. — Jakob Alter, Vienna, March 11, 1940

The Community was the Kultusgemeinde, Vienna's Jewish community organization, working with the American Jewish Joint Distribution Committee in New York. Two tickets for two parents. The plan was end of May. Then it wasn't. The route through Lisbon — the last open emigration corridor from Europe — would close entirely in late 1941.

Through 1940 and into early 1941, the letters become a sustained, increasingly urgent negotiation around this single problem. Each carries the reasonable assumption that with enough effort, the thing can still be done.

We do not want to go near Selma. Please — all of you dear brothers and sisters, including the dear brother in Boston — chip in together for the two tickets via Lisbon, and help us to emigrate. A stone would have moved sooner. — Max's parents, late 1940

The tickets never came.

1941

Selma

By February 1941, the letters from Vienna were traveling by airmail, stamped with German Reich postage and bearing the censor's mark. A word had appeared in the correspondence: Selma. It recurs across several letters as a destination to be feared above all else, something the writers would rather die than reach. It is almost certainly a code for deportation — a camp, a transport, a disappearance. The word is used with the familiarity of something everyone in the conversation already understands.

If — God forbid — you do not send us any cards, we will unfortunately soon be gone, near Selma, which I would rather wish death upon than face. — Vienna, February 18, 1941

On April 23, 1941, Jakob and Anna wrote jointly — the longest letter in the collection — referencing Joint Distribution Committee case numbers 3064 and 2533 at the top. Three hundred dollars had been deposited for one ticket. They needed three hundred more for the second.

It should be $600 laid out for two ship tickets, and then we would be saved. Unfortunately, since we have not yet received any ship tickets, they have taken Selma. I beg you most earnestly — please arrange for an additional $300. — Jakob & Anna Alter, April 23, 1941

By mid-1941, a letter from Tante Gusti Krauthammer in Bottrop, Germany — a relay station in the network — carried news that Max's parents had been moved from Vienna to the town of Działoszyce in occupied Poland. They could not write freely from there.

We write to you from Dzialoszice. — Max's parents, mid-1941

On December 25, 1941 — a date noted in red ink on an envelope, almost certainly in Max's hand — Jakob and Anna Alter were deported from Vienna to Poland.

1942

Letters from the Ghetto

Działoszyce was a market town in the Kraków district with a Jewish community of roughly ten thousand people. Anna Alter was there, in the deep of a Polish winter, writing postcards to a woman named Frau N. Rainer at Grüne Torgasse 34 in Vienna — a conduit who could receive mail and forward packages. She could not write to Max directly. In her postcards he appears only as "der Bube" — the boy — a careful anonymity that protected both of them from the censor.

From the sister-in-law in Bottrop we received a letter — that he is healthy, and is said to be one of the best students there. So: God be thanked for it. At least from afar, something good about the boy. — Anna Alter, Działoszyce, January 25, 1942

The postcards through 1942 document a slow deterioration. The cold is severe. Prices are "impossible to put into writing." Tante Gusti in Bottrop, who relayed packages, can no longer do so — she has apparently been deported herself. Anna asks Frau Rainer not to forget them.

Here there is a dizzying inflation — prices impossible to put into writing, unbelievable figures. As long as my sister-in-law was in Bottrop, life was still not quite so terrible — now everything is denied us. A second winter — I would not want to experience here. — Anna Alter, Działoszyce, May 25, 1942

Anna was taken away on September 3, 1942, during the mass deportation from the Działoszyce ghetto. A handwritten annotation on the envelope — in red ink, almost certainly Max's hand, written years later — records the date: taken away. The transport went to Bełżec extermination camp.

We have so much to write, but the cold does not allow it. We greet you from the heart — I kiss your hands. — Anna Alter, Działoszyce, c. September 1942 (last surviving letter)
Of the approximately ten thousand Jews in Działoszyce, roughly four thousand were shot during or after the September 1942 deportation. The remainder were transported to Bełżec.
1942 – 1943

The Father

Jakob Alter survived the September deportation. By October 1942, he was in the Prokocim labor camp near Kraków. He wrote postcards on German Reich stationery bearing Hitler's portrait — the only paper available. He addressed them to two Vienna contacts: Frau Rainer, and a man named S. Seidl at a tobacco shop on Rossauerlände. He did not know where Anna was.

Long, no news. Why? From that address — no sign of life. My health is not good. — Jakob Alter, Prokocim, October 27, 1942

Through 1943, Jakob moved between camps — Prokocim, then Biesanów, a sub-camp of the Kraków-Płaszów concentration camp. He could not write to Max in America. His letters ask for photographs, for small amounts of money to pay an overseer who demanded payment for bearable conditions.

My health is not good. Herr Hungeri is mainly to blame for this. Please — perhaps it is possible to send me 50 Zloty, two or three times per month, in order to stop Herr Hüngeri's mouth. — Prokocim camp, October 13, 1942

The last surviving letter in the entire collection is postmarked November 7, 1943, from a location written as "Pióra." It ends mid-sentence.

[November 7, 1943 — the final postmark in the wartime correspondence] — Unknown sender, Pióra, November 7, 1943
March 1943

What Singer Knew

On March 20, 1943, a man named Stepan Singer, in exile in Havana, Cuba, wrote to Frau S. Weitz at 1725 Gleason Avenue in the Bronx — Max's aunt. Singer was a Yugoslav refugee who had been a business acquaintance of the Alter family in Vienna. He confirms, in quiet and careful language, that Max's parents were deported to Poland. He also discloses that Anna Alter, on the eve of her deportation, gave him two small objects to hold for Max.

On the occasion of my last stay in Vienna, Frau Alter gave me two small things to keep in safekeeping. We took them with us during our flight and have them here now. Once we come to the USA we will pass the things on to Maxi. — Stepan Singer, Havana, March 20, 1943

What the two objects were, and whether Singer eventually delivered them, the correspondence does not say.

1944 – 1946

The Soldier

There are no letters from the years when Max served. He entered the United States Army and was assigned to General Patton's Third Army. He crossed the Rhine in the final weeks of the war in Europe, days before Germany's surrender in May 1945. He was nineteen. He had left Vienna at fourteen.

He came home.

1946 – 1957

The Postwar Letters

In October 1946, a letter arrived from Vienna. It was from a woman who signed herself Tante Mimi — Mimi Urmann — part of the same community that had sustained the correspondence through the war. She congratulated Max on his recent marriage, expressed grief that his parents had not lived to see it, navigated the chaos of postwar currency controls, and then: she had kept something.

What is truly precious are the jewelry pieces of your dear mother — on those I have kept a closer watch than on my own things. — Tante Mimi Urmann, Vienna, October 21, 1946

Tante Mimi wrote to Max for more than a decade. Her letters report on damaged care packages, currency restrictions, the fate of mutual acquaintances scattered across London, Leeds, and New York. In 1947, a photograph arrived in Vienna: Max's baby daughter. Tante Mimi wrote back that she was "an enchanting child." She asked for more pictures.

The last letter in the collection is dated January 31, 1957. Tante Mimi has been writing to Max for more than ten years. She had kept his mother's jewelry through the war, watched Vienna be occupied and liberated, welcomed news of every milestone in the life he was building in America. She had been the keeper of what remained.

Dearest Maxi — always with love. Yours, Mimi. — Tante Mimi Urmann, Vienna, January 31, 1957

The correspondence ends there. Not because anything happened. Tante Mimi lived. Max lived. It simply ends, the way ordinary correspondence ends, when the letters trail off and life continues without a written record.

The Collection

These fifty-one letters and postcards span eighteen years, five countries, four languages, and the full arc of the Second World War as it was experienced by one Jewish family from Vienna. They were written in formal French, in old German cursive, on ICRC stationery, on Nazi-issued postcards bearing Hitler's portrait, on airmail paper stamped by Reich censors, and on ordinary writing paper in peacetime Vienna and postwar Boston. Max Alter kept all of them.
