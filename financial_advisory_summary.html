$500K–$5M tier · 0.30% · Dedicated CFP

VNTC — Vanguard National Trust Company

Vanguard's trust subsidiary can serve as successor trustee for financial assets. However: (1) it requires a pre-acceptance review of the trust and is not guaranteed to accept; (2) it cannot administer real estate, business interests, or insurance products; (3) administrative situs is Pennsylvania regardless of client's state. Must be named in trust documents drafted by an independent attorney.

Beneficiary Designation System

IRA Accounts — direct designation
  • Name spouse as primary (by name or by relationship — "person I'm married to at time of death")
  • Name individuals, trust, charity, or estate as contingent
  • Can split among multiple beneficiaries in any percentage
  • Relationship designation auto-includes future family members
  • IRA designation overrides the will — always
  • Updatable online anytime via Account Maintenance
Non-Retirement Accounts — TOD Plan
  • Transfer on Death (TOD) plan for taxable accounts
  • Passes assets directly to named beneficiaries without probate
  • Applies to individual and JTWROS accounts
  • Can name individuals, an existing trust, or charities
  • On joint accounts, TOD kicks in after last surviving owner
  • IRA and non-retirement designations are completely separate
Critical Rule

Beneficiary designations override wills — on every account. If the will names the spouse as IRA beneficiary but the IRA form names the children, the children inherit. This applies equally to the Alight 401(k). Each account's own form controls distribution regardless of any other document.

Estate Administration After Death

Three separate streams · No single orchestrator

With a Vanguard account, a separate Alight 401(k), and real estate, the estate involves three entirely separate processes that no single institution coordinates. After the second spouse dies, heirs must manage all three simultaneously.

Stream 1 — Vanguard
  • Death certificate to Vanguard portal
  • TOD / beneficiary designations trigger direct transfer
  • Account frozen during processing
  • SECURE Act 10-year rule for non-spouse heirs
  • VNTC activates as trustee if pre-named

Timeline: 5–10 business days (with beneficiaries named)

Stream 2 — Alight 401(k)
  • Contact Alight directly — Vanguard has zero involvement
  • Beneficiary form controls distribution (overrides will)
  • Surviving spouse can roll over to own IRA (best option)
  • Non-spouse heirs: 10-year distribution rule applies
  • No beneficiary named → estate → probate

Timeline: Weeks (with beneficiaries named)

Stream 3 — Real Estate
  • No financial institution handles this
  • If in trust: trustee controls transfer — no probate
  • If JTWROS: passes to surviving spouse automatically
  • If sole ownership / no trust: full probate required
  • Florida condo: Florida ancillary probate (separate process)

Timeline: Weeks (trust/deed) to 6–18 months (probate)

The Coordination Gap

After the second spouse dies, heirs must simultaneously manage three entirely separate processes at three institutions operating independently. Vanguard cannot see the Alight account. Alight cannot see Vanguard. Neither knows about the real estate. There is no default orchestrator. Inconsistent or outdated beneficiary forms can override carefully drafted trust documents.

Alight 401(k) — Critical October 31 Deadline

If the trust is named as beneficiary on the Alight 401(k), a copy of the trust document must be delivered to Alight (the plan administrator) by October 31 of the year following death. Missing this deadline can cost heirs the favorable 10-year distribution schedule, potentially triggering a 5-year forced payout instead — a much larger annual tax bill. Heirs must be informed of this deadline in writing before the death occurs.

Living Trust — Implementation Gaps

The trust document alone is not sufficient

Having a living trust is the right foundation. But a trust only controls what is actually connected to it. Three implementation gaps are common and consequential:

Requires Attorney Review
Alight 401(k) — See-Through Trust
The 401(k) cannot be titled in the trust during lifetime. Naming the trust as beneficiary is legal, but the trust must qualify as a "see-through trust" under IRS rules (effective Jan 1, 2025) or heirs face a 5-year forced payout. If the trust is over 3–5 years old, review is essential. A copy of the trust must also be filed with Alight before death. Employer plans still require this documentation — the 2025 IRS rule change did not eliminate this requirement for 401(k)s.
Must Be Executed
Real Estate — Deed Transfer Required
Describing or listing property in the trust document does NOT transfer ownership to the trust. A new deed must be recorded for each property. The Massachusetts primary residence deed and the Florida condo deed must each be separately transferred. For the Florida condo, a Florida-licensed attorney must prepare the deed (Florida law governs Florida real property). Until the deeds are transferred, the properties are not in the trust regardless of what the trust says about them.
Recommended
Vanguard Account — Re-Title into Trust
The taxable Vanguard account can be re-titled directly into the trust's name (e.g., "John and Jane Smith Revocable Living Trust, dated [date]"). This is not a taxable event. Once re-titled, the trust owns the account and the trust terms govern distribution — no beneficiary designation form is needed. This is the cleanest approach. Alternatively, name the trust as TOD beneficiary — this also works but is slightly less clean during lifetime.

See-Through Trust Requirements (IRS — 4 criteria)

  • Valid under state law — an existing Massachusetts trust likely meets this
  • Irrevocable at death — a revocable living trust automatically becomes irrevocable when the grantor dies; this requirement is satisfied
  • All beneficiaries must be identifiable individuals — charitable remainder language or non-individual remainders may disqualify the trust
  • Copy of trust document delivered to Alight (plan administrator) by October 31 of the year after death — this is a hard deadline
If the Trust Was Drafted Before 2023

Older trust documents often contain formula clauses tied to prior federal exemption amounts or pre-SECURE Act distribution rules. Massachusetts raised its estate tax exemption from $1M to $2M in January 2023. A trust drafted with a $1M credit shelter formula will produce unintended results under current law. IRS final RMD regulations effective January 1, 2025 also changed some see-through trust rules. Any trust more than 3–5 years old should be reviewed.

Massachusetts Residency — Key Issues

Estate tax · Non-portable exemption · Florida ancillary probate
Massachusetts — Primary Residence
Estate tax: Yes. $2M exemption per person (raised from $1M in January 2023). Rates 7.2%–16% on amounts above $2M.

Portability: None. The surviving spouse cannot inherit the deceased spouse's unused exemption. Each person gets one $2M exemption — the first spouse's exemption is wasted if everything passes to the surviving spouse outright.

Out-of-state real estate: Florida condo value is excluded from Massachusetts estate tax calculation. Massachusetts taxes only Massachusetts-situs property.
Florida — Condo (Non-Residents)
Estate tax: None. Florida has no state estate tax and no inheritance tax.

Ancillary probate: Risk. As non-residents owning Florida real property, the estate must go through Florida ancillary probate for the condo unless it is titled in the living trust. This is a separate Florida court process — 6–12+ months, requires a Florida attorney — running in parallel with the Massachusetts estate process.

Solution: Deed the condo into the trust. One deed transfer eliminates ancillary probate entirely.
Massachusetts Exemption — Not Portable

With $2M in investments + Alight 401(k) balance + Massachusetts home value, the surviving spouse's estate almost certainly exceeds $2M at the second death. Without a credit shelter trust provision, Massachusetts estate tax applies to the entire excess. On a $3.5M surviving estate, the tax is approximately $100,000. On a $4M estate, approximately $180,000. This is avoidable with proper planning.

The Credit Shelter Trust Solution

A Credit Shelter Trust (also called AB Trust or Bypass Trust), built into the living trust document, captures the first spouse's $2M exemption at death rather than letting it expire. At the first spouse's death, up to $2M is directed into an irrevocable credit shelter trust for the surviving spouse's benefit — not counted in the surviving spouse's estate. The surviving spouse benefits from the assets but they are excluded from the taxable estate at the second death. This alone can save $39,000–$180,000+ in Massachusetts estate tax.

Transferring Between Vanguard and Fidelity

ACATS · 5–7 business days · No taxes triggered

No public data exists on transfer frequency between these specific firms. Transfers between Vanguard and Fidelity are routine — both participate in the ACATS (Automated Customer Account Transfer Service) system, making the mechanics straightforward.

How the transfer works
  • Initiated at the receiving firm (not the sending firm)
  • Positions move in-kind — no selling required, no taxes triggered
  • Cost basis transfers intact — original purchase dates preserved
  • Typically completes in 5–7 business days for electronic transfers
  • Vanguard charges a $100 fee for accounts under $5M transferring out — Fidelity typically reimburses this for accounts at this size
Key friction points
  • Vanguard mutual funds (VTSAX, VBTLX, etc.) cannot transfer in-kind to Fidelity — must be converted to ETF equivalents (VTI, BND) first, or sold
  • Selling Vanguard mutual funds in a taxable account triggers capital gains — this is the main cost consideration before switching
  • Fidelity ZERO funds cannot transfer to Vanguard (proprietary)
  • Fractional shares are sold during transfer (minor tax event)
Practical Guidance for This Couple

If the $2M Vanguard account holds appreciated Vanguard mutual fund positions (not ETFs), converting to ETF equivalents (e.g., VTSAX → VTI) before initiating a transfer is the standard approach — it avoids a taxable sale. The conversion itself is not taxable. This is a one-time step. The operational mechanics of switching are genuinely not a major obstacle at this account size — the capital gains question on appreciated positions is the only material consideration.

Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

Tax-deferred accounts only — not all funds

RMDs apply only to tax-deferred retirement accounts — not to all funds held at a financial institution. This is a critical distinction: the $2M Vanguard account is a taxable (non-retirement) account and carries no RMD obligation whatsoever.

Accounts Subject to RMDs
  • 401(k), 403(b), 457(b) plans — including the Alight account
  • Traditional IRAs
  • SEP IRAs and SIMPLE IRAs
  • Rollover IRAs
  • Inherited IRAs (different distribution rules apply)
Accounts NOT Subject to RMDs
  • Roth IRAs — no RMDs during the owner's lifetime (a major Roth advantage)
  • Regular taxable brokerage accounts — the $2M Vanguard account falls here
  • Savings accounts, CDs, money market accounts
  • Any taxable investment account at any institution
Implication for This Couple

The $2M Vanguard discretionary account has no RMD obligation. Withdrawals can be taken on any schedule, in any amount, at any time. The only RMD exposure in this picture is the Alight 401(k).

Age 75 — RMDs Are Already Mandatory

Under the SECURE 2.0 Act, RMDs begin at age 73 (changed from age 70½ under prior law). At age 75, the couple is already past the mandatory start age by two years. If the Alight 401(k) has not been distributing RMDs annually, this must be corrected immediately. Missing or under-taking RMDs carries a 25% excise tax penalty on the shortfall. The IRS offers a correction procedure, but the sooner it is addressed the better.

RMD Calculation Reference

RMD amounts are calculated from the prior December 31 account balance divided by an IRS Uniform Lifetime Table factor. At age 75 the factor is 24.6. Example: a $300,000 Alight balance requires a ~$12,200 RMD for the current year. A $500,000 balance requires ~$20,325. The Alight plan administrator is required to calculate and notify you of the annual amount — but you are responsible for ensuring it is actually taken.

Confirm Immediately
Verify Alight RMDs are being taken
Log into the Alight account and confirm that annual RMDs have been correctly calculated and distributed each year since age 73. Missing distributions must be corrected promptly to avoid the 25% excise tax penalty.
Tax Planning Opportunity
Model Roth conversion scenarios
If the Alight 401(k) balance is substantial, converting a portion to a Roth IRA may reduce future RMD obligations. Roth IRAs have no RMDs during the owner's lifetime. The conversion triggers income tax on the converted amount — but eliminates future RMDs on that balance. An advisor should model this against projected bracket exposure.
Coordinate with Advisor
RMD + withdrawal sequencing strategy
The CFP at Vanguard or Fidelity should model annual income from all sources: Alight RMDs, Social Security, and any discretionary Vanguard withdrawals. The goal is staying within target tax brackets and avoiding IRMAA surcharges on Medicare premiums triggered above specific income thresholds.
