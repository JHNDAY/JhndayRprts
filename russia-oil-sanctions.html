Sanctions Timeline

The Sanctioned Regime ‚Äî Event by Event

The G7 moved quickly after the invasion, but building a truly effective sanctions regime requires global buy-in that never fully materialized.

FEB 2022
Russia Invades Ukraine
The full-scale invasion triggers the G7 sanctions process. Russia controls what will become roughly one-fifth of Ukraine's territory.
JUN 2022
First EU Sanctions Package
The EU targets ~90% of its oil imports from Russia ‚Äî a major blow to Moscow's export revenues, as Europe was its largest customer.
DEC 2022
G7 Price Cap: $60/barrel
Western companies may still ship, insure, and finance Russian crude ‚Äî but only if it trades at or below the cap. Paradoxically, this made Russian oil cheaper for non-Western buyers.
2025
Price Cap Lowered to $47.60
Trump administration also targets Rosneft and Gazprom directly. Russia's oil still flows ‚Äî now through a more complex web of intermediaries and a shadow fleet.
The New Buyers

Who Fills the Gap When Europe Steps Back?

China, India, Turkey, and Central Asian states have absorbed the Russian barrels Europe stopped buying ‚Äî each with distinct motivations.

üá®üá≥ China
World's Largest Oil Importer
Imports over 11 million barrels per day ‚Äî by far the most of any country. China buys from every available seller, using its sheer volume as leverage. Chinese companies remain critically dependent on U.S. banking infrastructure, giving Washington theoretical coercive power it has so far not exercised over Russian purchases.
üáÆüá≥ India
The New China ‚Äî Fastest Growing
India's oil appetite is surging. It's become one of the most active buyers of discounted Russian crude ‚àí25 to 35% and has also emerged as a significant re-exporter of processed Russian energy ‚Äî with Europe as a key destination, raising questions about sanctions efficacy.
üáπüá∑ Turkey
Buyer & Curious Re-Exporter
Turkey covers only ~20% of its domestic oil needs from local production, yet it has become a small but growing oil exporter. Its strategic position between Russia and Europe makes it a natural intermediary, taking advantage of discount pricing and resale opportunities.
üåç Central Asia
The "Stans" ‚Äî Sanction-Immune
U.S. and EU sanctions have almost zero impact across Kazakhstan and neighboring states. The price cap has, perversely, made Russian oil cheaper for them while creating new export arbitrage opportunities. G7 leverage here is minimal.

Market Conditions & Geopolitics

The Oil Market Has Moved On

Despite the war, prices have settled. Crude trades near $60/barrel ‚Äî dragged down partly by the price cap itself. Natural gas sits at lows not seen since the 1990s.

The peace proposal mentioned by the Trump administration caused a brief $3/barrel dip ‚Äî quickly reversed. Markets had already priced in the "new low normal."

As McKnight puts it: the goal of buying gasoline under $2/gallon isn't remotely achievable if other countries genuinely stopped purchasing Russian oil.

"Is it a flawed sanctions regime to blame ‚Äî or is the global oil market simply too big for any group of countries, even the G7, to actually influence?"

‚Äî Scott McKnight

Despite the "no-limit friendship" announced at the 2022 Beijing Winter Olympics, China has not sent forces, replenished weapons stocks, or meaningfully disrupted U.S. financial pressure points. The bromance burns ‚Äî the material support remains ambiguous.

Bottom Line

Russian Oil & Gas Continues to Flow ‚Äî Just Differently

The G7 sanctions regime successfully redirected where Russian energy goes, but failed to meaningfully reduce how much flows or how much money flows back. The price cap made Russian oil cheaper, ironically incentivizing China, India, Turkey, and Central Asia to buy more of it. War funding continues.

The peace proposal ‚Äî if it materializes ‚Äî may reshape the market once more. But for now, the new global oil architecture looks durable.
