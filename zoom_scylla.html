The Digital Odyssey · Book XII, Revisited

Navigating the Abyss

Being a true & faithful account of Zoom, the Scylla and Charybdis of our age

Fig. I. — The mortal navigator, confronted on the left by six-headed Scylla (audio dropout, frozen video, echo, lag, unmuted chaos, and the dreaded “you’re on mute”) and on the right by Charybdis, the whirlpool of the Leave Meeting button pressed too soon.
The Invocation

Sing in me, Muse, and through me tell the story of that learned soul skilled in all ways of contending with bandwidth throttled and the cursor spinning — who dared to click the Zoom link at precisely 9:58 a.m. and found himself, as heroes always do, between two horrors neither one escapable.

Scylla
a.k.a. The Zoom Interface

Six-headed goddess of the toolbar below, she lurks where the gallery view meets the screen-share toggle. Each head devours a different mariner: one swallows those who cannot find the Mute button; another takes those who share their entire desktop rather than one window; a third consumes all who have never updated their client since 2021.

She cannot be slain. She can only be endured.

Charybdis
a.k.a. The Leave Meeting Button

Three times daily she swallows the dark waters of the meeting whole, and three times she belches them back out as a new call that must be joined again with a fresh link. She sits, blood-red, in the lower right corner of every session, calling to weary navigators: "End it. End it all."

Many have clicked. Few have clicked wisely. Some have ended the meeting for everyone.

The ancient Circe counselled Odysseus thus: “Hug Scylla’s rock — lose six men, keep your ship. Row too wide to avoid her and Charybdis takes the whole vessel.” The modern counsel is identical: mute yourself, lose your voice entirely for the duration; attempt instead to manage audio AND video AND the chat AND the participant list simultaneously, and the whirlpool takes your entire presentation, your dignity, and your lunch hour.
The Oracle Speaketh

“Thou shalt always have a backup dial-in number.
Thou shalt test thy audio before the call begins.
Thou shalt never, ever, click End Meeting
when thou meanest only to Leave.”

The Homecoming

“Row hard,” said Circe. “Keep your eyes forward. Do not look at Scylla as she takes her toll — if you watch her work you will stop rowing and all will be lost. Simply accept that someone in the meeting will have a dog that barks, someone will forget to mute, and someone will join six minutes late and ask to have everything repeated from the beginning.

This is the nature of the wine-dark internet. Row through it. You cannot hold your meeting in a calmer strait than this. There are no calmer straits. There never were.”
