THE NEW YORKER

July 27, 2016

The Greatest Man in the World

After James Thurber

A modern political fable

Looking back on it now, from the vantage point of 2020, one can only marvel that it hadn't happened long before it did. The United States of America had been, ever since the Tea Party movement, blindly constructing the elaborate petard by which, sooner or later, it must be hoist. It was inevitable that some day there would come roaring out of the primetime TV lineup a candidate of insufficient intelligence, background, and character successfully to endure the mounting orgies of glory prepared for politicians who spoke offscript or went on Twitter rants. Both Romney and McCain, fortunately for national decorum and international amity, had been gentlemen; so had our other famous candidates. They wore their laurels gracefully, withstood the awful weather of publicity, married excellent women, usually of fine family, and quietly retired to private life and the enjoyment of their varying fortunes.

No untoward incidents, on a worldwide scale, marred the perfection of their conduct on the perilous heights of fame. The exception to the rule was, however, bound to occur and it did, in June 2015, when Donald Trump, erstwhile real estate mogul and reality TV star, descended the golden escalator in Trump Tower and announced his run for the presidency on the Republican ticket.

Never before in the history of American politics had such a campaign as Trump's ever been dreamed of. No one had even taken seriously the outlandish policy proposals, like the great border wall, invention of the nationalist advisor Steve Bannon, upon which Trump placed full reliance.

When the real estate tycoon, a pot-bellied, ruddy-faced man of seventy, appeared on the debate stage in 2016, slowly waving his stubby fingers, and announced "Nobody builds great big walls like me," the newspapers touched briefly and satirically upon his projected campaign. Political analysts dismissed the idea curtly, implying that he was a buffoon who wouldn't last.

Trump, however, after calling on his daughter Ivanka, who helped run his global business empire centered in New York, a daughter whom he later described as "a hot little number," climbed nonchalantly onto the platform at the Republican National Convention on the memorable night of July 21, 2016, gave two thumbs up to the still air, and accepted the party's nomination, carrying with him only his oversized ego and six misspelled tweets drafted on his phone.

When Trump thundered into the general election, the papers were forced to record, in all seriousness, that a mad, unknown candidate—his name was variously misspelled—had actually set out upon a preposterous attempt to capture the White House in a wave of populist anger, trusting to divisive rhetoric that played on racial grievances and economic fears. As the tiny-fingered vulgarian barreled across the country holding one raucous rally after another, spluttering and fulminating all the while, the headlines, which long since had crowded everything else off the front page, swelled to unprecedented size.

It was noticeable, however, that the accounts of the epoch-making campaign touched rather lightly upon the candidate himself. This was not because facts about the hero as a man were too meagre, but because they were too troubling.

Reporters, who had been rushed out to New York City when Trump's hardline nationalism was first sighted over the airwaves at Fox News, to dig up the story of the great man's life, had promptly discovered that the story of his life could not be printed. His ex-wife, a sullen Aspen socialite locked in divorce proceedings, met all inquiries as to her husband with an angry "Ah, the hell with him; I hope he drops dead of a heart attack from all those cheeseburgers." His father appeared to be a tax cheat and slumlord with suspicious ties to the Mob; his young daughter Tiffany spent all her time on Instagram and seemed only to want his money.

These alarming discoveries were still piling up at the very time that Donald Trump, the greatest rogue of the twenty-first century, beady-eyed, dead for sleep after five rallies in two days, half-deranged by visions of his name in giant gold letters on every building from sea to shining sea, was piloting his crazy ego trip toward the general election, headed for the far greater glory than any candidate of his time had ever known.

The necessity for printing some account in the papers of the candidate's career and personality had led to a remarkable predicament. It was of course impossible to reveal the facts, for a tremendous feeling in favor of the bloviating strongman had sprung up among the white working class like a grass fire, as he crushed his primary opponents one by one. He was, therefore, described as a plainspoken businessman, gruff, blond, popular with his friends, popular with girls. The only available photos of Trump, taken at the Miss USA pageant, sternly judging the swimsuit competition, were touched up so that the bulbous vulgarian looked quite appealing. His twisted leer was smoothed into a pleasant smile. The truth was, in this way, kept from the candidate's ecstatic compatriots dreaming of making America great again; they did not dream that the Trump family was despised and feared by its fellow Manhattan elites, nor that the hero himself, because of his shady financial dealings and lawsuits, had come to be regarded in New York City as a nuisance and a menace. He had, the reporters discovered, once sued an entire Indian tribe to prevent them from opening a casino that would compete with his Atlantic City properties—not killing them, to be sure, but he had sued them; and on another occasion, surprised blowing money in a Dubai hotel bar that should have gone to repay loans, he had attacked the Persian owners with racial slurs, for each of these offenses he had paid hefty settlements.

Inwardly, the GOP establishment, both in New York and in Washington, prayed that an understanding Providence might bring disaster to the blustering billionaire and his preposterous campaign, which had aroused cable news channels to heights of hysterical reporting. The GOP leaders were convinced that the character of Trump was such that the limelight of adulation on the campaign trail was bound to reveal him, to all the world, as a congenital scoundrel, a narcissist with authoritarian impulses. "We must stop this dangerous demagogue before he brings the whole tent down on our heads," said the Senate majority leader in a secret meeting at the Capitol, by which he referred to Trump's potential to despoil the GOP brand forever.

It was, however, too late for that—Trump had captured the hearts of enough working class whites in rust belt states to snatch the electoral college victory, carrying the deindustrialized wastelands of the upper Midwest by razor thin margins. At three minutes after two o'clock on the dreary morning of November 9th, 2016, the real estate tycoon brought his idiotic campaign to a squeaker win over the establishment favorite Hillary Clinton.

It had, of course, been out of the question to arrange a modest concession speech, a call for national unity, for the upset victor. He was received at his garish headquarters with such elaborate and pretentious ceremonies as rocked the world. Fortunately, however, the worn and spent leader promptly swooned, had to be removed bodily from his podium, and was spirited from the arena without having opened his mouth once. Thus he did not jeopardize the dignity of this first reception, a reception illuminated by the rictus grins of Mike Pence, Rudy Giuliani, Chris Christie, and a brilliant array of nationalist thinkers and media provocateurs. Trump did not, in fact, come to in time to take part in the gigantic hullabaloo arranged for his inauguration. He was rushed to a secluded room at the Trump International Hotel and confined to bed.

It was days before he was able to get up, or to be more exact, before he was permitted to get up and greet the clamoring throngs who had now made him, for better or worse, the most powerful man on earth, the exemplar of American greatness to friends and foes alike across the entire globe. Meanwhile, using executive orders and Twitter decrees, the machinery of government was grinding into motion to reshape itself around the unstable fulcrum that was now the occupant of the Oval Office.

* * *

On the day that Trump was finally allowed to get up and dress and, for the first time in weeks, indulge in his favorite fast food, he was permitted to receive the newspapermen—this by way of testing him out. Trump did not wait for questions. "You guys," he said—and the Times man winced—"you guys can tell the whole world that I beat Crooked Hillary, see? Yeah—an' made fools of those loser Republicans who tried to stop me." The "loser Republicans" was a reference to the sixteen primary opponents who, in attempting to derail his candidacy, had, months before, unhappily been crushed. The Times man was bold enough, at this point, to sketch out for Trump the accepted formula for interviews in cases of this kind; he explained that there should be no arrogant statements belittling the achievements of other leaders, particularly leaders who had served the nation with dignity. "Ah, the hell with that," said Trump. "I won it, see? I won it, an' I'm talkin' about it." And he did talk about it.

None of this extraordinary interview was, of course, printed. On the contrary, the newspapers, already under the disciplined direction of a secret directorate created for the occasion and composed of party strategists and editors, gave out to a panting and restless world that "Donald," as he had been arbitrarily nicknamed, would consent to say only that he was very happy and that anyone could have done what he did. "My achievement has been, I fear, slightly exaggerated," the Times man's article had him protest, with a modest smile. These newspaper stories were kept from the President-elect, a restriction which did not serve to abate the rising malevolence of his temper. The situation was, indeed, extremely grave, for Donald Trump was, as he kept insisting, "rarin' to go." He could not much longer be kept from a nation clamorous to lionize him. It was the most desperate crisis the United States of America had faced since the Cuban Missile Crisis.

On the afternoon of the fifteenth of January, Trump was spirited away to a conference room in which were gathered party leaders, government officials, behaviorist psychologists, and editors. He gave them each a limp, moist paw and a brief unlovely grin. "How ya doin'?" he said. When Trump was seated, the Speaker of the House arose and, with obvious pessimism, attempted to explain what he must say and how he must act when presented to the world, ending his talk with a high tribute to the President-elect's courage and integrity. The Speaker was followed by the Senate Majority Leader, who, after a touching declaration of faith, introduced a distinguished diplomat, the gentleman selected to coach Trump in the amenities of public ceremonies. Sitting in a chair, with his red tie hanging loose and his shirt straining at the buttons, unshaved, eating a cheeseburger, Donald Trump listened with a leer on his lips. "I get it, I get it," he cut in, nastily. "You want me to act like a wimp, huh? You want me to act like that loser Obama, huh? Well, forget it, see?" Everyone took in his breath sharply; it was a sigh and a hiss. "President Obama," began a United States Senator, purple with rage, "and President Bush—" Trump, who was checking his phone for tweets, cut in again. "Bush!" he exclaimed. "Oh for God's sake, that moron—" Somebody shut off his blasphemies with a sharp word. A newcomer had entered the room. Everyone stood up, except Trump, who, still busy with his phone, did not even glance up. "Mr. Trump," said someone, sternly, "the outgoing President of the United States!" It had been thought that the presence of President Obama might have a chastening effect upon the President-elect, and the former had been, thanks to the remarkable cooperation of the press, secretly brought to the obscure conference room.

A great, painful silence fell. Trump looked up, waved a hand dismissively. "How's it going?" he asked, and began typing another tweet. The silence deepened. Someone coughed in a strained way. "Man, it's hot, isn't it?" said Trump. He loosened two more shirt buttons. The great and important men in the room, faced by the most serious crisis in recent American history, exchanged worried frowns. Nobody seemed to know how to proceed. "Come on, come on," said Trump. "Let's get the hell out of here! When do I start the parties, huh? And what's in it for me?" He rubbed a thumb and forefinger together meaningly. "Money!" exclaimed a party chairman, shocked, pale. "Yeah, money," said Trump, flipping his phone onto the table. "An' big money. The biggest." He began scrolling through his phone again. "The biggest money," he repeated, grinning. He tilted back in his chair, and leered at each gentleman, separately, the leer of an animal that knows its power, the leer of a leopard loose in a henhouse. "Oh for God's sake, let's get some place where there's cameras," he said. "I been cooped up plenty long enough!"

The crisis came to a head on a sweltering July afternoon in Trump's third year in office. He had grown increasingly erratic and vengeful, lashing out against enemies real and perceived. As he prepared to depart on Marine One for a campaign rally, aides pleaded with him to tone down his rhetoric on Twitter and at rallies.

"Come on, come on, enough of this crap," Trump snarled, stepping toward the waiting helicopter on the South Lawn. "Get me to the rally so I can really give it to those America-hating Dems."

The Secretary of State looked mournfully at the Chief of Staff and the National Security Advisor, who both nodded grimly. John Kelly, the battle-hardened former Marine General turned Chief of Staff, strode forward purposefully toward Trump. Without breaking stride or hesitating, he grabbed Trump's shoulder with one hand and the waistband of his too-long red tie with the other, propelling the President headlong into the swirling jet engine intake of the waiting helicopter.

In a flash Trump disappeared with a startled yelp into the roaring metal maw. The engine hissed and sprayed fluids, its turbine mangled by the remains of the forty-fifth President.

"My God, he's fallen into the engine!" yelled the Secretary of Homeland Security.

"Get me away from here, now!" cried Mike Pence, suddenly looking ashen as the reality set in that he was now the President. Chaos erupted as the Cabinet members hurried Pence back into the White House.

John Kelly immediately took charge like the general he was. Working with the Defense Secretary and intelligence chiefs, he outlined a story they would all have to agree on—that a deranged lone gunman had somehow slipped onto the White House grounds and pushed Trump into the helicopter engine in a crazed assassination attempt.

In a word, Kelly skillfully set the stage for the unprecedented constitutional crisis, secretly relieved to have the volatility and menace of Trump neutralized, though it had required desperate measures for the greater good of the nation.

The state funeral was lavish and solemn, befitting the demise of the bombastic leader who had held the country in his grip for three endless years. Only Kelly and a few others knew the truth, which they took to their graves—the nation had been spared by righteous treason against a demagogue who, left unchecked, may have put American democracy itself through the helicopter engine. In time a bronze bas-relief sculpture was placed discreetly in the Capitol rotunda, with Trump's gilded visage and outstretched thumbs-up frozen in bronze, to gently remind future generations that the unthinkable, no matter how bedecked in red power ties and stars-and-stripes panoply, can happen here.
