üéØ Creating Course Objectives

‚ñº

Course objectives define what participants should know or be able to do by the end of your course. They guide your entire course design process.

Key Questions to Ask:

  • What should participants know by the end of the course?
  • What should they be able to do?
  • What skills, facts, or theories will they gain?

Example: Objectives for "Ready, Set, Go!" Workbook

Participants will engage in a process to design a new BOLLI course:

  • Generate ideas and shape concept
  • Explore different class delivery models
  • Create an enticing course description and objectives
  • Develop and divide material into logical sessions to create a syllabus
  • Engage with other prospective member SGLs
  • Understand how to inspire and moderate class discussions
  • Learn about available resources for help

Outcome: By the end, you will be ready to prepare and submit your proposal.

üó∫Ô∏è Approaches to Course Development

‚ñº

üìê Begin with the End in Mind (Understanding by Design)

Your course objectives guide course design, helping you refine your topic and eliminate material that doesn't support these objectives.

Process:

  1. Decide what you want students to learn (facts, skills, theories)
  2. Use objectives to figure out how to structure the course
  3. Plan backwards from desired outcomes to current starting point

Resource: Washington State University's "Backward Design: A Planning Framework" provides an in-depth guide.

üìñ Narrative Arc Approach

Think of your course as having a narrative arc‚Äîa sequence of topics that build upon each other toward a desired end.

Mountain Hike Analogy:

Think of your course as a 10-day hike in the mountains:

  • Each class takes you through different terrain to a different place
  • All classes are connected as you climb
  • Look back to see where you've been
  • Look ahead to see where you're going
  • Each class builds on previous learning
  • By the summit (end), there's synthesis where pieces fit together

Structure:

  • Beginning: Introduce foundation concepts
  • Middle: Develop and explore themes progressively
  • End: Synthesize learning and achieve objectives

Note: Even with a narrative arc, you still want clear course objectives to ensure the "hike" reaches the summit.

üìö Choose Your Materials

‚ñº

üìñ Primary Source Approach

Pick a primary source (book, play, movie, series) and let it structure your course organization.

Single Source:

  • Author has done the organization for you
  • Ideal for literature courses
  • Class time spent deciphering meaning
  • Benefit: Built-in structure
  • Drawback: Only one perspective (consider supplementing)

Examples:

  • Single Work: "Joseph Mitchell's New York" or "Old Crimes: Stories of Jill McCorkle"
  • Comparative: "Representations of Female Defiance: Jane Eyre and Wide Sargasso Sea"
  • Thematic: Short story course on motherhood
  • Perfect Fit: "The Age of Choice" (5 chapters = 5 weeks)
  • Supplemented: "Private Lives of the Impressionists" (book + articles + videos)

üí° Tip for Discussion:

Add contrasting viewpoints through supplementary readings to create discussion and even healthy controversy in your classes.

üåê Open Source Approach

Collect a variety of readings, websites, academic papers, and videos, then curate the most fascinating content into a cohesive collection.

Process:

  • Research widely across multiple sources
  • Borrow organizing principles from relevant books
  • Create your own website with summaries and links
  • Stitch together the most compelling materials

Trade-offs:

  • Benefits:
    • Maximum flexibility
    • Combine diverse amazing materials
    • Unique perspective and organization
  • Considerations:
    • Takes longer to research and assemble
    • More preparation required

Examples:

  • "The Indo-Europeans" (comprehensive website)
  • "City Visions: Three Great Cities Seen Through Artists' Eyes"

üé≠ Classroom Style

‚ñº

Consider: Are you a subject matter expert eager to share knowledge, or a guide facilitating shared learning? Perhaps a combination?

üé§ Lecture Format

Instructor does most of the talking, often with slides.

When Appropriate:

  • Esoteric or arcane subjects
  • When lecturer has significantly more expertise

‚ö†Ô∏è Danger:

Failing to keep participants engaged. Students may drift off, check phones, or multitask.

Making Lectures Work:

  • Stop frequently to invite participation
  • Take down slides so everyone can see each other
  • Encourage discussion, not just Q&A
  • Be a storyteller ‚Äì weave stories and examples into teaching
  • Invite contribution from participants to each other, not just to you

Best BOLLI Lecturers:

They are storytellers who leave behind dry textbook material and weave engaging narratives, stop for comments, and encourage participant-to-participant interaction.

üí¨ Facilitated Discussion

A structured process where the SGL guides group conversation to achieve specific goals.

Benefits:

  • Involves the class in the learning experience
  • Achieves shared understanding
  • Enables analysis and problem-solving
  • Creates learning dialogue among participants

Facilitator Role:

  • Guide conversation toward specific goals
  • Encourage participation from all
  • Ask probing questions
  • Moderate to ensure all voices are heard
  • Get participants to listen and respond to each other

Example: Short Story Course

SGL asks a series of questions that lead the group through the story. Their collective exploration leads to understanding from multiple perspectives.

More information about creating good facilitated discussions is provided in Week 4.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Show What's in It for Them

4Ô∏è‚É£ Highlight Key Features

Hook: What if the stories we tell shape the world we live in?

We'll begin with Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's TED Talk, "The Danger of a Single Story," as a foundation for discussing the stakes of storytelling in everyday life. From there, we'll read a mix of texts‚Äîranging from fiction and memoir to essays and visual narratives‚Äîthat open up alternative ways of seeing and being:

  • Claudia Rankine's Citizen: An American Lyric, a hybrid text of poetry, essay, and visual art that confronts the insidiousness of everyday racism
  • Jordan Peele's film Get Out, offering a sharp cultural critique through the lens of horror and satire

Together, we'll examine how personal and collective stories are shaped by race, gender, memory, and power‚Äîand how they in turn shape us. You'll be invited to connect these works to your own life experiences through discussion and short reflective writings.

No prior experience with literature, theory, or storytelling is required‚Äîjust curiosity and a willingness to engage.
