Big Ideas,
Big Blinders

Paradigm change in the global oil industry How the oil industry keeps getting caught off guard by the next big shift

Scott McKnight, PhD · Munk School of Global Affairs · August 2022

Central argument: A paradigm's incumbents, through structural 'power over ideas', develop built-in blinders that constrain their ability to incorporate challengers and address competing ideas — making stability paradoxically breed instability. Traced across four paradigms in the global oil industry since the 1920s. The big idea: Whoever is running the oil industry at any given moment gets so locked into their own way of thinking that they can't see the challenges building against them — until it's too late. This has happened four times since the 1920s, and it looks like it may be happening again right now.

§ 01 — The ArgumentThe Argument

Why Dominant Ideas Create Their Own Undoing Why the people in charge always miss what's coming

Click each item below to drill into the reasoning. The argument builds on constructivist political economy and challenges both "increasing returns" stability theory and exogenous-shock accounts of paradigm change. Click any item to dig deeper. The core argument is simple: the more firmly a group controls an industry, the harder it becomes for them to see — let alone respond to — the people who are about to overturn them.

Incumbents of a dominant paradigm make use of structural power over ideas — the ability to persuade others of the general validity of their approach. This gives the paradigm its "taken-for-granted" quality. However, this very power paradoxically constrains the ability of incumbents to acknowledge or incorporate challengers and competing ideas.

"The power of a paradigm 'not to listen' to competing ideas or aggrieved challengers constitutes built-in blinders, which opens the possibility of paradigm change without system-shattering shocks from the outside."
Endogenous instability Carstensen & Schmidt (2016) Hall (1993)

When a group — say, a cartel of oil companies, or a bloc of oil-exporting governments — becomes dominant enough that everyone accepts their rules as "just how things work," something interesting happens: they start to believe their own story. They mistake the fact that everyone goes along with them for proof that they are right. And that mistake makes them dangerously slow to spot — or take seriously — anyone who disagrees.

This paper argues that built into every dominant group is a kind of structural blind spot. The very tools they use to stay in control — setting the terms of debate, deciding who counts as a legitimate player, dismissing challengers as unrealistic — are the same tools that eventually prevent them from adapting. The group that runs the show becomes the last to understand that the show is ending.

Think of it as the oil industry version of "fighting the last war": every ruling group keeps solving the problems that brought down the previous rulers, while missing the new problems building under their feet.
Theories challenged
  • Increasing returns (Pierson 2000): Institutions strengthen over time via path dependence, making fundamental change "virtually impossible to reverse" without an outside shock. McKnight argues instability is built in, not exogenous.
  • Exogenous shock accounts: Change requires a system-shattering external event. McKnight argues challengers and rival ideas can grow to sufficient strength within a paradigm.
  • State-centrism in IPE: All four actor types — advocacy coalitions, epistemic communities, social movements, ideational leaders — played important roles.
What McKnight retains
  • Ideas matter: Builds on two generations of "power of ideas" scholarship (Blyth, Berman, Hall, Parsons).
  • Material factors also matter: No cause is "autonomous" from others (Daigneault & Béland, 2015).
  • Agency matters: Actors consciously deploy ideas to attain goals, leading to institutional change (Blyth et al., 2016).
  • Process-tracing: Qualitative, detective-like causal inference across a century of evidence.
Common view #1: Outside shocks cause change

The usual story is that big industries or governments only change when something dramatic hits from outside — a war, a crash, a natural disaster. McKnight says: not quite. The seeds of collapse are already growing inside the existing system. The ruling group's blind spots are what allow those seeds to take root. You don't need an asteroid; the volcano builds from within.

Common view #2: Dominant systems only get stronger

Another popular idea is that once a system is entrenched, it just keeps getting more entrenched. McKnight argues the opposite: the more entrenched a system gets, the more brittle it becomes. Success breeds overconfidence. Overconfidence breeds blind spots. Blind spots breed collapse.

Both big ideas are real, but neither tells the whole story. McKnight traces this through 100 years of actual history in the oil industry to show how the pattern repeats.

Incumbents tend to mistake the spread and perceived acceptance of their ideas as "common knowledge" (Culpepper, 2008) for the correctness of those ideas. This is Merton's "self-negating pathology": the paradigm's apparent success leads incumbents to double down on established ideas in times of crisis — even when those same ideas are partly causing the crisis.

"The stability of the paradigm paradoxically breeds instability." — Minsky (1986) as applied by McKnight
Merton (1968): self-negating pathology Minsky (1986): stability breeds instability MacKenzie (2008): popularity ≠ correctness

Here's the core irony: the more widely a group's ideas are accepted, the more convinced they become that those ideas are simply correct — rather than just currently popular or powerful. And so when problems start appearing, they double down on the same thinking that caused the problems, instead of trying something new.

The sociologist Robert Merton called this a "self-negating" dynamic — a belief system that, by being acted on, creates the very conditions that will prove it wrong. In the oil industry, this happened every time: the ruling group's greatest strength became the source of its eventual failure.

The excluded players — the ones nobody listened to — were meanwhile getting organized and gaining credibility. The ruler's refusal to engage them actually helped them: it made the alternative look more and more reasonable to everyone watching.

The paper uses a controlled case study (Slater & Ziblatt, 2013) examining the global oil industry since the 1920s. The oil industry is an excellent test case because paradigm change in oil has only sometimes followed the dominant paradigms of the broader global political economy, and because the actor array (MNCs, national oil companies, governments of both importers and exporters, civil society) provides an excellent opportunity to test a more agency-oriented approach.

The methodological approach is Y-centric process-tracing (Beach & Pedersen, 2013): tracing backward from the outcome — paradigm change — to the causal process, establishing what mechanism connects ideas to the observable change in resource governance. Both ideational and material factors are considered, because "no cause is 'autonomous' from others" (Daigneault & Béland, 2015).

McKnight traces the global oil industry from the 1920s to 2022 — four distinct eras, each with a different group calling the shots. The oil industry is a great test case because it involves an unusually wide cast of characters: giant corporations, national governments, oil-rich states, importers banding together, and now civil society and environmental groups. It doesn't follow the same script as the rest of the global economy, which makes the patterns that do emerge especially meaningful.

The method is essentially detective work: starting with the outcome (a big change in who controls the industry and how) and working backward to trace exactly what caused it, step by step. The goal is to show that ideas — not just money, armies, or geography — are what actually drive these shifts. But ideas don't work alone: physical reality, prices, and political events all matter too.

Hall's classic definition: a paradigm is "a framework of ideas and standards that specifies not only the goals of policy and the kind of instruments that can be used to attain [those goals] but also the very nature of the problems they are meant to be addressing." Actors draw from these assumptions to define problems, formulate solutions, and legitimize strategy. Ideas "provide the recipes, guidelines and maps for political action" (Schmidt, 2008). A paradigm achieves a "taken-for-granted" quality. This paper's central outcome of interest is third-order change: change in the overarching paradigm itself.

Paradigm change is distinguishable from incremental adjustment: it involves a fundamental shift in goals, instruments, and problem-definitions — not just the settings of existing instruments.
→ Hall (1993); Carson et al. (2009); Schmidt (2002); Kay (2007)

Every era has a set of beliefs about how something should be run that become so widely accepted they feel like common sense rather than choices. In the oil world, these have included things like: "private companies should manage global oil, not governments" (1920s–60s), or "the free market will sort out energy prices" (1980s–2000s). Nobody voted on these ideas — they just gradually became the water everyone swam in.

The key thing about these ruling ideas is that they define more than just policy — they define who counts as a legitimate player and what counts as a real problem. This is what makes them powerful, and also what makes them dangerous: anything that doesn't fit the framework gets ignored.

The political scientist Peter Hall called this a "paradigm." When a paradigm breaks, it's not just a policy change — it's an entire worldview being replaced.

The ability of incumbents of the dominant paradigm to persuade others of the general validity of their approach. This gives the paradigm its "taken-for-granted" quality and allows theorizing beyond coercive and institutional forms of power (Barnett & Duvall, 2005). Crucially, power over ideas is never final — always potentially open for challenge and contestation. Big ideas are "constantly evolving malleable structures subject to continual reconstructions by sentient agents" (Carstensen, 2016).

The key mechanism: incumbents exercise the power "not to listen" — but this only accelerates the legitimization of rival ideas among those who feel excluded.
→ Carstensen & Schmidt (2016); Barnett & Duvall (2005); Parsons (2016)

The most powerful thing a group can do isn't force people to agree — it's make their way of thinking seem so natural and obvious that alternatives don't even seem worth considering. When the Seven Sisters oil companies in the 1950s said "this is how global oil works," most governments just nodded along.

But here's the catch: that same power to define what's "normal" also prevents the group from taking seriously any challenge to it. The Majors couldn't take producer-country nationalism seriously because nationalism simply didn't fit their mental model. The more completely they controlled the terms of debate, the less capable they were of engaging with anyone who questioned those terms.

This is McKnight's central insight: the power to control ideas is also the power not to listen — and that power not to listen is exactly what allows challengers to grow unimpeded until it's too late.

Civil-society actors possess little compulsory, institutional, or structural power — yet they successfully shame institutionally powerful actors into conformity with their norms and ideas. This is how the "leave-it-underground" paradigm emerged: NGOs, the IPCC, and divestment campaigners had no coercive power over banks or pension funds — but they successfully shifted the moral calculus, causing over 500 institutions representing $40.57 trillion in assets to commit to fossil fuel divestment by 2022. The evolution from "power through ideas" to "power over ideas" marks the transition from challenger to incumbent status.

→ Carstensen & Schmidt (2016); Risse et al. (1999)

Environmental groups, climate scientists, university endowments — these players had almost no direct leverage over the oil industry. They couldn't pass laws, didn't control supply chains, and had no armies. Yet they managed to fundamentally change how banks, pension funds, and governments relate to fossil fuel investment.

How? By making it embarrassing to keep investing. They reframed the moral stakes — not "here's the economic calculation" but "here's what kind of organization you are." Harvard, the Norwegian sovereign wealth fund, and hundreds of others divested not because it was the most profitable thing short-term, but because continuing to invest had become a reputational liability.

This is how change often happens when you're on the outside: you don't fight the power head-on — you change what "respectable behavior" looks like, and power follows.

Merton's concept applied: incumbents mistake the popularity or dominance of their ideas for their correctness. In times of crisis, they return to established ideas rather than entertaining alternatives — even when those same ideas are partly responsible for the crisis. This appears in all four paradigms: the Majors boycotted nationalizing countries rather than revising concession terms; OPEC members cheated on quotas while invoking sovereign rights; IOCs doubled down on shareholder value logic while the climate crisis mounted.

"The widespread adoption of the big idea of competition reflected Merton's 'self-negating' pathology, in which the ascendancy of their ideas was mistaken for their correctness, bringing incumbents to return to these established ideas in times of crisis despite those same ideas in part causing the crisis."
→ Merton (1968); MacKenzie (2008); Culpepper (2008)

When everyone around you agrees with your worldview, it's easy to conclude that your worldview must simply be correct — rather than just currently dominant. This is the trap every ruling group falls into. When problems start appearing, instead of questioning the worldview, they double down. They treat trouble as a technical glitch to be fixed with more of the same medicine, not as a signal that the whole approach needs rethinking.

This happened in every era. The oil Majors responded to nationalization threats with tighter coordination — the very thing producers resented. OPEC members responded to falling prices by insisting more loudly on sovereign rights, which made joint action impossible. IOCs responded to environmental pressure by focusing more on shareholder returns. Each group turned back to the same playbook at the moment they most needed a new one.
Layering

New rules introduced alongside existing ones, when challengers lack capacity to fully replace the original rules. Example: in the 1990s, instead of fully privatizing NOCs, many governments listed minority shares on stock markets — adding market logic and external shareholders on top of state-ownership structures. The formal apparatus of sovereign control persisted; competitive incentives were layered on top.

Conversion

Formal rules remain unchanged but are interpreted and enacted in entirely new ways. Saudi Arabia's 1985 pivot is the clearest example: same legal control over the same oil wells, but the Kingdom shifted from benevolent swing producer (restraining output to support others' prices) to coercive hegemon (flooding the market to punish quota-cheaters). Same law. Opposite behavior.

→ Streeck & Thelen (2005); Thelen (2003)
Layering: adding new on top of old

When a new era takes hold, it rarely wipes the slate clean. New rules get added on top of old structures. In the 1990s, instead of fully privatizing national oil companies, many governments listed minority shares on stock markets — keeping state ownership while adding the discipline of public investors. The old structure remained; new expectations were layered on top.

Conversion: same rules, new behavior

Sometimes the formal structure stays identical but is used in a completely different way. Saudi Arabia's clearest example: in the early 1980s, the Kingdom held back its own production to prop up world prices for everyone (the "benevolent" role). Then in 1985, with the same legal control over the same oil wells, it suddenly flooded the market to punish quota-cheaters. Same law. Opposite behavior.

Process-tracing is the paper's core method, chosen because it is "particularly useful in coping with the methodological challenge of studying ideational causation" (Kay, 2009). McKnight deploys Y-centric theory-building: starting from the outcome of interest (paradigm change) and tracing backward to the causal process. The study covers nearly 100 years, following Jacobs' (2015) recommendation of "expansive empirical scope" when establishing the causal impact of ideas. The analysis aims to be "clear enough to be proven wrong" (Sabatier, 2007).

Both ideational and material factors are considered because "no cause is 'autonomous' from others" (Daigneault & Béland, 2015). Ideas matter insofar as they generate tangible, measurable consequences — that is how they are tested empirically.
→ Beach & Pedersen (2013); Jacobs (2015); Gerring (2007); Kay (2009)

Proving that ideas caused something is harder than it sounds. You can't run experiments on history. So McKnight traces the chain of events backward from the outcome — a major shift in who controls oil and how — and asks at each step: what made this happen? What made people think this was the right thing to do? What made them ignore the warning signs?

The approach covers nearly 100 years, because you need that sweep to see the pattern repeat across multiple cycles. One era might be a coincidence; four in a row is a structure.

Both ideas and physical reality matter — oil prices, wars, discoveries. But ideas determine which physical developments get treated as problems worth solving, and which get ignored. That's what makes them the key variable.

Core Belief: Coordination Prevents Ruinous Competition The core belief: someone has to keep a lid on it

The fundamental premise was that individual oil companies, if left to their own self-interest, would overproduce — leading to destructive price wars and rapid depletion of reserves. A small set of big multinational firms — the "Majors" — should therefore coordinate production and sales, suppress competition among themselves, and exclude actors outside the oligopoly from decision-making. Western governments, especially the US and UK, generally deferred to the Majors on exploration, transport, refining, and sales.

"The central problem proved not to be endlessly replacing new barrels for extracted ones, but rather how to constrain output from these abundant reserves so as not to overwhelm market demand, crush prices and destroy profitability."
Achnacarry 'As Is' Agreement (1928) Anti-overproduction logic Long-duration concessions

Oil was being found faster than anyone could sell it. Left alone, companies would pump as much as possible, flood the market, and crash prices — ruining everybody. So a small group of giant Western oil companies (eight of them, nicknamed the "Seven Sisters") quietly agreed to coordinate: divide up markets, set prices together, and keep outsiders from rocking the boat. Western governments were broadly fine with this arrangement. It kept oil cheap and plentiful.

In 1928, the bosses of Exxon, BP, and Shell secretly met at a Scottish castle called Achnacarry and agreed to freeze their market shares and stop competing on price. This handshake deal launched 40 years of coordinated control over most of the world's oil.

The Globe-Spanning Oligopoly It actually worked — for a while

In 1928 the heads of Exxon, BP, and Shell secretly met at Achnacarry Castle, Scotland, agreeing to freeze market shares "as-is" and end price competition. This launched five decades of globe-spanning oligopoly control. Eight companies controlled vast concession areas under extraordinarily one-sided terms.

By the 1950s, eight Western companies controlled the lion's share of the world's oil outside the US and Soviet Union. They delivered stable, affordable fuel that powered the post-war economic boom across Western Europe, Japan, and the US. Governments were happy to let them get on with it.

Fatal Blinder: No Space for Producer Governments or Outsiders The fatal blind spot: they couldn't imagine sharing power

The paradigm of big-company coordination had no ideational space for nationalistically-minded governments or for the "Independent" companies it could not accommodate. The Majors' narrow conception of problem-solving — balancing global supply with demand — could incorporate neither the national interests of producer governments nor competitors outside the oligopoly. When challenged, the response was joint boycotts, political lobbying, and shifting investments elsewhere.

When Iran nationalized its oil in 1951, the Majors organized a global boycott. Iran's oil exports collapsed from 650,000 barrels/day to just 20,000. It worked short-term — but the resentment it created was enormous and long-lasting.

The companies' entire model was built around keeping a small, exclusive club in control. There was simply no room in their thinking for the idea that producer countries had a legitimate claim to run their own oil industries — or that smaller rival companies deserved a seat at the table. When challenged, they responded with what they knew: boycotts, political lobbying, and shifting investment elsewhere.

Incapable of incorporating the Independents into their arrangement, the Majors acceded — unwittingly "letting the sovereign control genie out of the bottle" (Adelman, 1995). A "leapfrogging" of better deals spread around the world. The paradigm collapsed in the oil embargo and price quadrupling of 1973–74. The collapse came in stages. Libya's leader Gaddafi squeezed higher prices out of the Majors in 1970. Once one country got better terms, every other country immediately demanded the same. Then in October 1973, amid a war between Arab states and Israel, OPEC announced massive price increases and an oil embargo. Oil prices quadrupled. The era of big-company control was over.

Core Belief: States Must Lead the Commanding Heights The core belief: our oil, our rules

The state has the sovereign right of control over natural resources. Industries like oil — central to national development, jobs, revenue, and industrialization — must be led by the state. Allowing market forces to prevail only perpetuates colonial exploitation. It is no longer enough to seek better fiscal terms; states must take outright control of production, marketing, and prices.

"Post-colonial states assumed a central role in development above all in the control of natural resources, with little regard for which side one fell on during the Cold War." The paradigm provided an interpretation of what was wrong (foreign exploitation) and what to do (nationalize).

After decades of watching foreign companies extract their wealth and call all the shots, oil-producing countries decided enough was enough. The new idea was simple: oil belongs to the country it comes from. Governments — not foreign companies — should control production, set prices, and decide how to use the revenue. Getting better royalty payments wasn't enough anymore. Countries wanted to own and run their own oil industries outright.

This wasn't just about money. It was tied to a broader movement among newly independent nations who saw controlling their natural resources as fundamental to national dignity — not just a revenue stream.

Nationalizations, NOCs, and the Price Shocks It worked spectacularly — at first

Algeria, Iraq, and Libya led nationalizations in the early 1970s. By mid-decade virtually every oil-producing government in the Middle East, Venezuela, and smaller producers had nationalized their sectors. Each created a National Oil Company (NOC) as a multi-purpose vehicle: rent generator, employer, energy supplier, development tool, and geopolitical instrument. In October 1973, Arab oil states cut production and embargoed sales to the US and Netherlands. The "first shock" quadrupled world oil prices. A "second shock" in 1979–80 drove prices above $100/bbl.

Algeria, Iraq, and Libya led the way with nationalizations in the early 1970s. By mid-decade, almost every major oil-producing country had taken over their oil sectors. Each country created a national oil company to actually run things. During the 1973 Arab-Israeli war, Arab oil states cut production and refused to sell oil to the US and Netherlands. Prices quadrupled. A second big shock hit in 1979–80 following the Iranian revolution. For a few years, oil-exporting countries held enormous power over the global economy.

Three Unintended Consequences Three problems nobody expected

Problem 1: Resource CurseThe windfall didn't fix things

Many oil-producing economies were no better off — and very likely worse off — than before the windfall. High-volume oil producers ended up more authoritarian (Silje, 2010), more indebted (Sachs, 1989), more prone to violent conflict (Bannon & Collier, 2003), and less industrialised (Karl, 1997). The idea that states could straightforwardly convert oil rents into sustainable development proved wrong.Many oil-rich countries found that suddenly having enormous revenues made things worse, not better. Money flooded in but it often fueled corruption, inequality, and political repression rather than development. Economists later gave this the grim name "resource curse." By the late 1980s, it was hard to argue that the producing countries had actually gotten richer in any sustainable way.

Problem 2: Demand Destruction & New SupplyHigh prices backfired

High prices cooled oil demand by sending OECD economies into recession. Oil-importing countries adopted permanent measures to reduce demand — efficiency standards, conservation, alternative fuels. Meanwhile, high prices incentivised exploration in otherwise expensive areas (Alaska, the North Sea, Gulf of Mexico), generating surging non-OPEC supply by the early 1980s.When oil got very expensive, Western countries got serious about using less of it — better fuel economy, new energy sources, insulation campaigns. Meanwhile, oil companies started drilling in expensive places (Alaska, the North Sea) that suddenly made financial sense at high prices. By the early 1980s, there was too much oil and prices started falling.

Fatal Blinder: Sovereignty Prevents Collective Action The fatal blind spot: sovereign rights made collective action impossible

The paradigm was inherently limited in devising collaborative solutions to overproduction: each country viewed itself as the rightful sovereign decider of what to do with its resources. OPEC quotas (introduced early 1980s) were systematically cheated because the core principle — sovereign control — made restraint politically unacceptable at home.

"Incumbents were inherently limited in devising collaborative solutions to overproduction because each country viewed itself as rightful decider — ignoring ways in which this relentless pursuit of individual national interests progressively eroded the paradigm."

The whole idea of sovereign control meant every country decided for itself what to do with its own oil. That worked when they were all trying to squeeze more money from foreign companies. But when oil prices started falling and OPEC needed its members to pump less — to keep prices up — the same logic fell apart. Every member felt they had the sovereign right to produce as much as they liked. Cheating on production quotas became routine.

By 1985, Saudi Arabia had cut its own production from 8.5 million barrels a day to just 2.3 million — sacrificing enormous revenue to prop up OPEC prices — while other members quietly cheated. Eventually the Saudis had enough. They reversed course and flooded the market. Prices went from $28 to $11 a barrel in under a year. The era of OPEC dominance was over.

Core Belief: Markets Are the Most Efficient Allocators The core belief: let the market sort it out

Competition is the best method to increase value; the market is the most efficient allocator of resources. Restraining production to boost prices is suboptimal — whether by OPEC, the Majors, or a swing producer. Although oil production remained largely under state control (a legacy layer from the sovereign paradigm), competition should discipline both NOCs and IOCs alike. Publicly traded companies should shrink to "core competencies" to maximize shareholder value. The "inexorable globalizing logic" applied to oil as to everything else.

"The virtue of specialization and discipline of competition constituting 'common knowledge' for managing oil resources." (Culpepper, 2008)

After the chaos of the OPEC era, a new idea took hold: competitive markets work better than cartels or governments. Stop trying to fix prices or control supply — let companies compete, let financial markets set prices, and let the pursuit of profit guide investment. This was the same "Washington Consensus" thinking reshaping economies worldwide in the 1980s and 1990s: privatize, deregulate, compete.

For oil companies, this meant slimming down, focusing on core strengths, cutting costs, and maximizing returns to shareholders. For OPEC, it meant constant internal fighting about who was "really" following market principles.

Corporate Restructuring in a Low-Price Era Cheap oil everywhere — until suddenly it wasn't

Deprived of upstream acreage by nationalizations and under shareholder pressure during extended low prices (mid-1980s to early 2000s), IOCs outsourced specialized tasks to oilfield service companies and scaled down. They acted increasingly as financial vehicles — buying back stock, pursuing mergers. BP acquired Amoco; Exxon merged with Mobil; Conoco joined with Phillips. Several NOCs were fully or partially privatized; others listed minority shares on foreign exchanges.

From the mid-1980s to the early 2000s, oil was remarkably cheap. Companies cut costs, merged (Exxon bought Mobil; BP bought Amoco), and increasingly acted like financial companies rather than engineering ones — buying back their own shares, paying dividends, and acquiring other companies' assets rather than going out to find new oil. Several national oil companies were partly or fully sold off.

The Classic Overproduction Problem Returns The market kept creating its own problems

Internal contradictionsThe overproduction trap

Competition revived oil's classic overproduction problem: once steep upfront costs were sunk, every producer was incentivised to produce ceaselessly. Consistently low prices sent demand soaring across the Global South, while geopolitical tensions mounted — Iraq invaded Kuwait partly over quota revenue disputes; Venezuela openly ignored its OPEC quota.Once an oil company has spent billions drilling a well, it makes sense to keep pumping regardless of price — any revenue beats nothing. So every producer pumps as much as possible, which keeps prices low, which means everyone has to pump even more to cover costs. This drove prices to $10/barrel in 1998, threatening the finances of producer countries and companies alike.

The environmental challenge emergesThe climate challenge builds

Throughout this era, civil society actors were accumulating evidence and moral pressure against fossil fuel use. The UNFCCC was established (1994), Kyoto Protocol signed (1997), IPCC reports mounted. Meanwhile, cheap oil was accelerating the very emissions that made this pressure more urgent — the paradigm's success was fueling the challenge against it.Throughout this era, climate scientists were accumulating evidence that burning fossil fuels was heating the planet. The UN Framework Convention on Climate Change was signed in 1994. Environmental groups began pressuring financial institutions to stop funding oil. Meanwhile, cheap oil was accelerating the very emissions that made this pressure more urgent.

Fatal Blinder: Market Logic Has No Category for Externalities The fatal blind spot: markets don't count the cost to the atmosphere

The competition paradigm's incumbents, trapped in the self-negating pathology, were incapable of acknowledging the extensive "negative externalities" — environmental devastation — that burning fossil fuels caused. Market logic had no category for "unburnable carbon." The paradigm made oil cheap and abundant, spurring widespread use in China and India even as climate science accumulated.

"Accelerating the extraction of oil resources with little regard for environmental or social impacts, with this narrowness of vision generating unforeseen but not wholly unpredictable results."

The great failure of market logic was that it had no built-in way to account for the costs that burning oil imposes on everyone else — the warming atmosphere, the rising seas, the extreme weather. From the market's perspective, those costs were simply invisible. Oil companies were rewarded for producing as much as possible, as cheaply as possible.

The competition era's great success — making oil cheap and ubiquitous — was precisely what created the conditions for the next revolt. The cheaper oil was, the more of it was burned. The more that was burned, the stronger the case for the climate movement became. The era's triumph contained the seeds of its own replacement.

Core Belief: Fossil Fuels Must Stay in the Ground The core belief: it shouldn't be dug up at all

The question is no longer who should extract oil or on what terms — but whether it should be extracted at all. Burning fossil fuels produces greenhouse gases that cause climate change; this constitutes an emergency requiring that otherwise valuable hydrocarbon reserves be left unexploited. The paradigm blends "what's right" (principled) with "what works" (causal), as both address the climate emergency. Capital deprivation is the primary mechanism: by denying the world's most capital-intensive industry its financial lifeblood, the paradigm aims to prevent new extraction.

"Unlike the three paradigms that preceded it, the 'leave-it-underground' paradigm has been agnostic in its preference for market or state solutions — embracing an 'all hands-on deck' approach." (Hale, 2016)

For the first time, the debate shifted from who should run oil to whether it should be produced at all. Burning oil (and gas and coal) releases greenhouse gases that are heating the planet to dangerous levels. No matter how efficiently it's extracted or how fairly the revenues are shared, the act of burning fossil fuels is fundamentally incompatible with a livable climate future. So the goal became: keep it in the ground.

The tool chosen was surprisingly simple: cut off the money. The oil industry is extraordinarily capital-hungry — it takes billions to find and develop new fields. If banks, pension funds, and universities stop investing in fossil fuels, the thinking goes, the industry can't grow. Let it wither from lack of cash.

Capital Deprivation in Practice It's actually working — partially

Civil-society actors with little compulsory power convinced major financial institutions to divest from fossil fuels. Harvard University, the Norwegian Sovereign Wealth Fund, the Ford Foundation, and hundreds of others committed to divestment. The World Bank Group stopped financing upstream O&G projects in 2019. Investment constraints have tangibly reduced upstream capital expenditure since 2015.

Environmental groups, operating with no government authority and no military power, successfully convinced some of the world's most powerful financial institutions to pull out of fossil fuels. The divestment campaign spread from small universities to Harvard, to the Norwegian sovereign wealth fund, to major pension funds.

Emerging Blinder: Conflating Capital Constraints with Decarbonization The blind spot forming: cutting supply isn't the same as cutting demand

The leave-it-underground paradigm has been unable to grasp the complexity of a genuine low-carbon transition. It treats "denying capital to oil companies" as equivalent to "reducing emissions" — but these are not the same. Demand continues to grow in the Global South regardless of whether Western IOCs or Saudi Aramco meets it; and state-owned producers are better positioned to continue indefinitely.

"The 'leave-it-underground' paradigm will need to find ways to overcome its own biases and very optimistic assumptions about the pace of transitioning to a less carbon-intensive energy system. The leave-it-underground movement has been brilliant at reducing investment in Western, publicly traded oil companies. But it has confused this with actually solving the problem. If people still need oil and Western companies produce less, someone else just produces more — typically with less transparency, less environmental oversight, and no shareholder meetings where activists can raise concerns.

McKnight's argument moves beyond the "excessively structural focus of paradigms" to embrace agents who interpret — and re-interpret — institutional setups (Campbell, 2004; Mahoney & Thelen, 2010). For paradigm change to occur, two conditions must be met: (1) a competing set of ideas must exist, and (2) actors willing to deploy them must be present. All four types of change-agents have been important: advocacy coalitions (OPEC, the IEA, divestment networks); epistemic communities (IPCC, resource economists); social movements (environmental activism, resource nationalism); and ideational leaders (Qaddafi's price demands, Saudi Arabia's 1985 pivot, the IPCC's scientific mandate).

Actor-based constructivism: actors consciously make use of ideas to attain their goals, which then leads to institutional change (Blyth et al., 2016; Saurugger, 2013). The key moment is when problems within the dominant paradigm are interpreted as "irresolvable" — that interpretation is performed by agents, not given by structural conditions.

McKnight's research doesn't claim that change is impossible without collapse — just that it hasn't happened that way in the oil industry, and that there are structural reasons why it's very hard. The more completely a group controls the terms of debate, the harder it is for them to genuinely engage with a challenge rather than just dismissing it.

For successful adaptation to happen, a ruling group would need to: (1) actually hear and take seriously the complaints of excluded players, (2) revise their own core beliefs rather than just adjusting tactics, and (3) do this before the excluded players have become strong enough to simply overthrow them. In practice, those three things almost never happen together.

This is why understanding ideas — not just prices, not just politics — is essential to understanding how the oil industry actually changes. The blind spot is ideological before it becomes practical.

All four paradigms provided prescriptions for who should manage natural resources and how — and each bore real-world consequences for the cost and availability of energy. Four eras, four collapses, all following essentially the same arc. Each ruling group believed in its own approach, and each believed strongly enough that it couldn't genuinely engage with those who disagreed. And each time, the refusal to engage made the challengers stronger, not weaker.

1. Instability is endogenous1. Collapse comes from inside, not outside

Paradigm change does not require system-shattering exogenous shocks (contra Pierson, 2000). The blinders created by power over ideas generate internal contradictions that accumulate over time until challengers are strong enough to force change. This is a fundamentally different mechanism from path dependence or critical juncture theory.You don't need a war or a financial crash to bring down a ruling system. The blind spots created by being in control generate their own internal contradictions. The asteroid doesn't have to come from space — it builds underground.

3. Ideas + material both matter3. Ideas matter as much as money and force

The argument doesn't eliminate space for material factors — both are always relevant, because "no cause is 'autonomous' from others" (Daigneault & Béland, 2015). Material success reinforces ideas; material crisis creates interpretive openings. The key is that ideas determine which material developments are seen as problems and which are ignored.At every turn, it was the framing — who gets to define "the problem" and "the solution" — that determined what happened, not just oil prices or military power. Understanding the idea is the key to understanding the outcome.

2. Against state-centrism2. You don't need official power to change things

All four actor types — advocacy coalitions, epistemic communities, social movements, ideational leaders — played critical roles across the four paradigms. Any theory-building in this domain must adopt a non-state-centric approach to IPE. Civil-society actors with no compulsory power managed to reshape an industry with over $600 billion/year in annual investment.Environmental groups didn't control governments or oil fields. But by changing what "respectable behavior" looked like for financial institutions, they shifted hundreds of billions of dollars of investment. The weapon was shame and moral legitimacy, not authority.

4. Power is never final4. No ruling idea lasts forever

Ideational dominance is neither predetermined nor permanent. Competing ideas are always present, possibly gaining in strength and legitimacy. The big ideas are "constantly evolving malleable structures subject to continual reconstructions by sentient agents" (Carstensen, 2016). Every paradigm — including the current one — can be challenged.Every approach to running the oil industry that seemed permanent and inevitable eventually fell. That includes the current one. Which means the climate movement — however necessary its goals — will also need to adapt or be replaced by something that handles what it currently can't see.

Taking lessons from all three failed paradigms, the leave-it-underground paradigm will need to overcome its own biases and optimistic assumptions about the pace of low-carbon transition. The pattern is already repeating: the paradigm has deployed its "power not to listen" to the complexity of Global South demand, the structural role of petro-states, and the distinction between constraining publicly traded IOCs versus constraining total oil supply.

"This big idea has tended to exacerbate oil-price volatility, stoke inflation and threaten economic growth while increasing the global economy's dependence on 'petro-states', and gradually discrediting the big idea of decarbonization, no matter how noble or necessary it may be." — McKnight (2022)

McKnight is not arguing that fighting climate change is wrong. He's arguing that the current approach — focused on cutting investment in Western oil companies — shows the same pattern of overconfidence that brought down every previous era. The climate movement is mistaking the popularity of its ideas for their sufficiency. And it is struggling to take seriously the challenges that don't fit neatly into its framework.
