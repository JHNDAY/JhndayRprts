🔥 The U.S.–Iran War: Operation Epic Fury ▶
Operation Midnight Hammer (June 2025) to Operation Epic Fury (February 2026): A Timeline
The current conflict grew from a long diplomatic collapse. The Congressional Research Service documented the first major escalation: on June 21–22, 2025, in Operation Midnight Hammer, the U.S. struck three Iranian nuclear sites — Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan — using seven B-2 stealth bombers, 14 GBU-57 "bunker buster" bombs, and over two dozen submarine-launched Tomahawk missiles. Iran responded by striking Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar, though satellite imagery showed aircraft had been evacuated in anticipation.
The second, larger war began on February 28, 2026. The American Jewish Committee reported that the U.S. operation — called Epic Fury — and Israel's parallel campaign — Roaring Lion — together targeted Iranian nuclear facilities, military infrastructure, and senior leadership, including Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, who was killed in the strikes.
Key Facts — Operation Epic Fury (Feb. 28, 2026)
  • U.S. and Israel launched coordinated strikes on Feb. 28, 2026
  • Supreme Leader Khamenei killed; senior IRGC commanders also killed
  • Iran retaliated against U.S. bases in Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Jordan — and struck all six Gulf Cooperation Council states for the first time in history
  • Iran launched 500+ ballistic missiles and ~2,000 drones since Feb. 28 (as of Mar. 5)
  • Two Iranian missiles targeted the U.S./UK base at Diego Garcia (Mar. 20); one intercepted, one failed
  • Iran struck a school in Minab, southern Iran; preliminary U.S. findings suggested a U.S. munition may have been responsible (160+ deaths including children)
Wikipedia's 2026 Iran War article — heavily sourced from NBC, Reuters, BBC, and AP — noted that as of late March 2026, Trump threatened to destroy all of Iran's power plants, oil wells, Kharg Island, and desalination plants if a deal was not reached. Iran rejected a 15-point U.S. peace plan. Iranian President Pezeshkian said Iran would "no longer attack neighboring countries" but rejected Trump's call for unconditional surrender.
"What this war exposes is a failure not only of strategy, but of literacy. A mind tutored by history and literature knows that bombing a founding myth is more likely to consecrate it than to dissolve it."
— Shared in the session, sourced from a colleague's email citing unnamed scholar; echoes commentary in UK Parliament Research Briefing CBP-10521, March 2026
Wikipedia's account of the U.S.–Iran negotiations (citing Reuters, Axios, AP) recorded that the Trump administration cited an alleged Iranian plan to attack targets in California as partial justification. The IAEA stated there was "no evidence of a structured nuclear weapons program" when the 2026 war began. An anonymous Pentagon source told Congress in closed-door briefings there was no intelligence that Iran planned to attack U.S. forces first.

Estimated Iranian protester deaths in Jan. 2026 crackdown (non-government health officials); government figure: 3,117
Massive protests beginning December 28, 2025, over economic collapse and currency devaluation became the largest since the 1979 revolution — an estimated 5 million Iranians. ⚖️ Pay-to-Pardon: The Joshua Nass Extortion Arrest ▶
Lobbyist Tied to Trump Pardon Arrested for Mafia-Style Extortion
On March 14, 2026, CNN reported that Joshua Nass, 34, a Trump-connected lobbyist and founder of political consulting firm Merkava Strategies, was arrested outside his New York hotel by the FBI on charges of attempted extortion. Nass had previously secured a presidential pardon for Joseph Schwartz — a former nursing home executive convicted of a $38 million tax fraud scheme — who was pardoned by Trump in November 2025.
The Extortion Scheme — Key Facts
  • Schwartz agreed to pay Nass $600,000 for pardon lobbying; paid only $100,000
  • Nass contacted an FBI informant to "do anything and everything" to recover the remaining $500,000
  • In recorded messages (in English and Russian), Nass discussed having the informant physically assault Schwartz's son — "sock him a few times" — and offered $15,000 plus $5,000 per additional man
  • Nass was released on $5 million bond; faces up to 20 years if convicted
  • FBI Assistant Director James Barnacle: "Rather than honestly representing his client, Joshua Nass allegedly chose to shake him down by hiring an enforcer to extort payment"
The Daily Beast and The New York Times both noted the Nass case as emblematic of a broader "pay-for-pardon industry" that has flourished around the Trump White House. The Times had previously cited Schwartz's clemency as an example of a growing pattern in which well-connected lobbyists advertise access to executive clemency as a commercial service.

Total Trump pardons issued — including ~1,500 January 6th participants; 12 pardon recipients re-arrested for serious crimes including child sexual abuse (NYT, March 2026)
The broader pardon economy drew additional coverage: Rudy Giuliani reportedly received $2 million to facilitate a separate pardon. Jared Kushner received a reported $2 billion investment commitment from Gulf sovereign wealth funds to manage an investment fund — a transaction that drew scrutiny from multiple outlets, including The Washington Post and Politico, as a potential corruption-adjacent arrangement given Kushner's White House role and family relationship to the president.

🚨 Immigration: Abrego Garcia, CECOT, and the Deportation Crisis ▶
The Case That Became a Symbol: Kilmar Abrego Garcia
The case of Kilmar Abrego Garcia, whose wrongful deportation to El Salvador became a constitutional flashpoint, was ongoing at the time of the March 31 session. Britannica's detailed entry — drawing on AP, court records, and immigration law experts — traced the arc: Garcia, a Salvadoran immigrant living in Maryland with legal withholding-of-removal protection, was arrested on March 12, 2025, outside an IKEA while driving his five-year-old child. Three days later he was deported to CECOT in El Salvador — the country an immigration judge had explicitly barred him from being returned to.
Timeline of the Abrego Garcia Case
  • Mar. 15, 2025: Deported to CECOT despite standing withholding-of-removal order; DOJ later conceded it was an "administrative error"
  • Apr. 10, 2025: U.S. Supreme Court unanimously affirmed his deportation was unlawful; ordered government to "facilitate" his return
  • Jun. 6, 2025: Returned to U.S. — but immediately charged with human smuggling (his lawyers called the charges "trumped up and political")
  • Aug. 2025: Briefly released; re-detained by ICE days later
  • Late 2025 onward: Government sought to deport him to a third country (Liberia mentioned in reporting) to circumvent the 2019 El Salvador prohibition
  • Mar. 2026: Case remains unresolved; Garcia held in Pennsylvania detention center
Human Rights Watch published a major March 2026 report documenting that El Salvador is "forcibly disappearing and arbitrarily detaining" Salvadoran deportees. Of more than 9,000 Salvadorans deported since January 2025, families of those sent to CECOT have had essentially no contact with relatives — some for over a year. Relatives tried ICE's online locator system and found no results. Habeas corpus petitions in Salvadoran courts were refused.
"The government's position implies that it could deport and incarcerate any person, including U.S. citizens, without legal consequence, so long as it does so before a court can intervene."
— Justice Sonia Sotomayor, concurring statement, Noem v. Abrego Garcia, U.S. Supreme Court, April 10, 2025
The session also referenced the ~260 Venezuelans held in CECOT under the Alien Enemies Act — most without criminal convictions. A Wikipedia entry synthesizing Bloomberg, ACLU, and court filings noted that a Bloomberg investigation found approximately 90% of deported Venezuelans had no U.S. criminal record beyond traffic or immigration violations. The government's evidence against many detainees consisted primarily of tattoos and unverified informant tips.
Sources: Britannica · Human Rights Watch · ABC News (Timeline) · U.S. Supreme Court (24A949) · Al Jazeera

The Washington Post's Department of Data (March 2025) found that more than a quarter of all computer programming jobs vanished in two years — the worst downturn that industry has ever seen — with the U.S. now having fewer programmers than at any point since 1980.

🏛️ Post-Trump Accountability: A National Debate Begins ▶
Can Democracies Recover Without Holding Leaders to Account?
The session's debate about whether future Democratic majorities should prioritize accountability or governance is mirrored in a growing body of mainstream political commentary. The core tension — prosecute wrongdoing as a deterrent vs. focus on the urgent work of repair — has been explored by legal scholars, historians, and political strategists throughout 2025–26.
The New York Times and The Atlantic have both carried analyses of the "flood the zone" strategy — Steve Bannon's explicit doctrine of overwhelming opponents with simultaneous crises so that no single outrage can sustain media scrutiny. The group's observation that multiple major corruption stories (Nass, Giuliani pardons, Kushner $2B, Zambia HIV leverage) each received brief coverage then disappeared is a textbook example of this dynamic in operation.
The Accountability Dilemma — Key Arguments in Play
  • FOR prosecution: Without accountability, future leaders will read impunity as permission — the "it worked for Trump" problem
  • AGAINST prosecution as primary focus: It hands opponents a political narrative ("witch hunt") and consumes legislative bandwidth needed for governance
  • State-level prosecution: Presidential pardons cannot cover state crimes — an avenue multiple legal scholars have highlighted as the most viable deterrent path
  • Supreme Court immunity ruling (2024): The Court's ruling that official acts are presumptively immune substantially narrows federal prosecution options for presidential conduct
  • The McCarthy parallel: Senate censure in 1954 did not end McCarthyism — his loyalists remained loyal. History judges; it does not necessarily deter.
The question of the Justice Department's institutional integrity was central to the session. Politico and Reuters have tracked the politicization of DOJ under Pam Bondi — including the reported refusal of U.S. Marshals to carry out court orders because they report through a chain of command now running through a DOJ that itself declines to enforce judicial orders. This structural collapse of enforcement was raised in the session as one of the most alarming developments of the period.
Sources: New York Times · The Atlantic · Politico · Reuters
🌍 Middle East Diplomacy: What Was Lost ▶
The Abraham Accords Momentum — and Its Unraveling
The session's discussion of the collapse of a viable diplomatic track — Jordan as a stable partner, Saudi Arabia as a pragmatic business-interests-first actor, and the pre-2025 trajectory toward normalization — reflects a well-documented arc in mainstream foreign policy coverage. Reuters, BBC, and The Washington Post all tracked the moment at which Saudi–Israeli normalization (which would have built on the 2020 Abraham Accords) became politically impossible following October 7, 2023 and the subsequent Gaza campaign.
The session's observation that Israel (10 million people) cannot militarily control a region of 400 million — and that the diplomatic alternative was within reach before 2025 — is consistent with Foreign Affairs, the Council on Foreign Relations, and academic Middle East analysts who have argued that the June 2025 strikes and the February 2026 war foreclosed the most promising regional normalization window in decades.
ACLED's March 2026 Special Issue confirmed that Iran's retaliatory strikes — the first time in history it directly attacked all six Gulf Cooperation Council states — have fundamentally altered the regional security architecture, likely setting back the conditions for any diplomatic settlement by years or decades.
