Religion and Politics in the United States, 1600-2026

A 10-Week Course Outline
Spring 2026

COURSE STRUCTURE: 10 weeks, 90-minute sessions

PEDAGOGICAL APPROACH: Chronological progression with thematic scaffolding

═══════════════════════════════════════════════
WEEK 1: FOUNDATIONS - DEFINING RELIGION AND POLITICS

LEARNING OBJECTIVES:

  • Establish working definitions of "religion" and "politics" in American context
  • Introduce the analytical framework for the course
  • Preview major themes and tensions that will recur throughout 400 years

SESSION CONTENT:

Course overview and expectations (15 min)
What do we mean by "religion"? Beyond denominations to worldview, community, and moral authority (30 min)
What do we mean by "politics"? Power, governance, social order, and contested values (20 min)
Introduction to key tensions:
Individual conscience vs. communal order
Religious freedom vs. religious establishment
Prophetic witness vs. cultural accommodation
Progress vs. tradition
Preview of the course arc: How will we trace these themes? (25 min)

SCAFFOLDING FOR WEEK 2:

This foundational week establishes the analytical vocabulary and framework students will apply throughout the course. By ending with preview of tensions, students are prepared to see these patterns recurring in colonial America.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════
WEEK 2: COLONIAL FOUNDATIONS (1600-1740)

LEARNING OBJECTIVES:

  • Understand the diverse religious motivations for colonization
  • Analyze early experiments in religious governance and their political consequences
  • Trace the origins of American religious pluralism and its challenges

SESSION CONTENT:

Puritans in Massachusetts Bay: "City upon a Hill" as political theology (20 min)
Pilgrims and the Mayflower Compact: Covenant theology in practice
Roger Williams and Rhode Island: First experiment in separation of church and state
Pennsylvania Quakers: Religious toleration as political principle
Maryland: Catholic experiment and its failure
Virginia: Anglican establishment and emerging tensions (20 min)
The diversity problem: How did colonies handle religious difference? Persecution, toleration, indifference (25 min)
Anne Hutchinson, Salem witch trials: Religion, gender, and political power (25 min)

SCAFFOLDING FOR WEEK 3:

Students now understand the fundamental tension between religious establishment and religious freedom that characterized the colonies. This sets up the First Great Awakening as a force that would transform this landscape.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════
WEEK 3: THE FIRST GREAT AWAKENING AND REVOLUTION (1740-1790)

LEARNING OBJECTIVES:

  • Analyze how religious revivalism created new political consciousness
  • Understand the relationship between evangelical religion and revolutionary ideology
  • Trace the democratizing effects of religious enthusiasm

SESSION CONTENT:

What was the Great Awakening? Jonathan Edwards, George Whitefield, and the "new birth" (20 min)
Political implications of religious revival:
Challenge to established religious authority
Creation of voluntary associations
Emphasis on individual choice and conscience
Cross-class, cross-regional networks
(25 min)
New Light vs. Old Light: Religious division as rehearsal for political division
From religious liberty to political liberty: The conceptual connections (20 min)
The Black Church origins: African Americans and the Awakening (15 min)
Setting the stage: How the Awakening prepared colonists for revolutionary thought (10 min)

SCAFFOLDING FOR WEEK 4:

Students see how religious transformation can drive political transformation. This prepares them to understand how the Founders navigated religious diversity while creating a new government.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════
WEEK 4: THE FOUNDING FATHERS, THE CONSTITUTION, AND RELIGIOUS FREEDOM

LEARNING OBJECTIVES:

  • Analyze the religious beliefs and motivations of key Founders
  • Understand the competing visions of church-state relations at the founding
  • Examine how the First Amendment emerged from religious and political negotiation

SESSION CONTENT:

The religious landscape in 1787: Diversity as political problem and opportunity (15 min)
Religious views of key Founders:
Deists (Jefferson, Franklin): Nature's God and reason
Orthodox Christians (John Adams, John Jay)
Skeptics and the "wall of separation"
(20 min)
State establishments and the problem of national religion
The Constitutional Convention: Why no religious test? (15 min)
The First Amendment: "Congress shall make no law..."
Establishment Clause: Whose interpretation?
Free Exercise Clause: What are its limits?
The tension between the two clauses
(25 min)
What they created: Disestablishment without secularization (15 min)

SCAFFOLDING FOR WEEK 5:

Students now understand the constitutional framework that would govern church-state relations. This sets up the Second Great Awakening as a test of whether religion would thrive or wither without government support.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════
WEEK 5: THE SECOND GREAT AWAKENING AND ANTEBELLUM REFORM (1790-1860)

LEARNING OBJECTIVES:

  • Understand how disestablishment energized American religion
  • Analyze the relationship between evangelical religion and social reform movements
  • Examine how the same religious impulses could lead to both progressive and reactionary politics

SESSION CONTENT:

The Second Great Awakening: Voluntary religion and explosive growth (15 min)
The "Burned-Over District" and religious innovation: Mormons, Millerites, spiritualists
Camp meetings and democratic religion
The reform impulse: Perfectionism and the Kingdom of God on Earth (20 min)
Abolition as religious crusade:
Quakers and early abolition
William Lloyd Garrison and radical abolition
Frederick Douglass and the prophetic critique
John Brown: Terrorist or martyr?
(25 min)
Women's rights and religion: Seneca Falls and the evangelical connection
Temperance: Religion and social control (15 min)
The other side: Pro-slavery theology and biblical interpretation (15 min)

SCAFFOLDING FOR WEEK 6:

Students see how religion can be a force for both liberation and oppression, for reform and reaction. This prepares them for the post-Civil War period when these tensions intensify.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════
WEEK 6: CIVIL WAR TO WORLD WAR I - RELIGION IN THE GILDED AGE (1865-1920)

LEARNING OBJECTIVES:

  • Analyze how religion responded to industrialization, immigration, and modernization
  • Understand the emergence of the Social Gospel and its critics
  • Examine the cultural wars of the early 20th century

SESSION CONTENT:

Civil War as religious watershed: Lost Cause theology vs. Battle Hymn of the Republic (15 min)
The KKK as religious organization: Protestant nationalism and white supremacy
Immigration and religious anxiety: Catholics, Jews, and the "real America" (20 min)
Social Gospel: Walter Rauschenbusch and progressive Christianity
Prohibition: Religion, reform, and government power (20 min)
Women's suffrage: Religious arguments for and against
The Scopes Trial (1925): Science, religion, and public education (20 min)
Fundamentalism emerges: Defending orthodoxy in a changing world
Charles Coughlin and the radio priest: Religion, populism, and fascism (15 min)

SCAFFOLDING FOR WEEK 7:

Students understand how modernization created religious and political anxiety. This sets up the post-WWII period when "godless communism" would create new alliances.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════
WEEK 7: POSTWAR CONSENSUS AND CIVIL RIGHTS (1945-1970)

LEARNING OBJECTIVES:

  • Understand the Cold War religious consensus and its political uses
  • Analyze the Black Church as the moral and organizational center of Civil Rights
  • Examine the relationship between religious authority and social change

SESSION CONTENT:

"Godless Communism" and the religious Cold War (15 min)
Eisenhower's civil religion: "Under God," "In God We Trust," and generic faith
Billy Graham: Evangelical revival and political access (15 min)
The paradox: Religious consensus and religious anxiety
Catholic moment: JFK and the question of divided loyalty (15 min)
The Black Church and Civil Rights:
Theological foundations: Exodus, Prophets, Social Gospel
Martin Luther King Jr.: Beloved Community and nonviolent resistance
Malcolm X and the Nation of Islam: Alternative vision
Local churches as organizing centers
(30 min)
White religious response: From support to "southern strategy"
The turning point: How did religious coalitions shift? (10 min)

SCAFFOLDING FOR WEEK 8:

Students see how the Civil Rights movement represented both the apex of religious progressivism and the catalyst for conservative religious mobilization. This directly sets up the rise of the Religious Right.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════
WEEK 8: THE RISE OF THE RELIGIOUS RIGHT (1970-2000)

LEARNING OBJECTIVES:

  • Analyze the factors that led to evangelical political mobilization
  • Understand the alliance between religious conservatives and Republican politics
  • Examine the role of abortion, school prayer, and sexuality in political realignment

SESSION CONTENT:

The myth and reality: Was it abortion? Was it segregation?
Roe v. Wade and the mobilization narrative
Bob Jones University, tax exemptions, and the real catalyst
How abortion became the unifying issue
(20 min)
The Moral Majority: Jerry Falwell and political organization (15 min)
Reagan coalition: How evangelicals became Republican (15 min)
School prayer, "secular humanism," and the culture wars
Rise of megachurches and the prosperity gospel
Pat Robertson, James Dobson, and the institutionalization of religious conservatism (15 min)
The Christian Coalition: Electoral politics and grassroots organizing
The other side: Religious Left and why it failed to compete (10 min)
Consequences: How this alliance reshaped both religion and politics (15 min)

SCAFFOLDING FOR WEEK 9:

Students now understand the institutional and ideological foundations of the current religious-political alignment. This prepares them to analyze the Trump era and its relationship to this longer history.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════
WEEK 9: MAGA, THE SUPREME COURT, AND CONTEMPORARY CRISIS (2000-2026)

LEARNING OBJECTIVES:

  • Analyze the transformation of the Religious Right in the Trump era
  • Understand the Catholic conservative legal movement and the Supreme Court
  • Examine Project 2025 as culmination of decades of religious-political strategy

SESSION CONTENT:

From "values voters" to Trump: The transformation
How did evangelicals justify Trump?
"He fights" vs. "moral character"
Transactional politics and prophetic witness
(15 min)
The Supreme Court as religious project:
The Federalist Society and originalism
Opus Dei and Catholic conservatism
Six Catholic justices: What does it mean?
Overturning Roe, dismantling the administrative state, and the conservative legal agenda
(25 min)
Christian nationalism: Is America a "Christian nation"?
Seven Mountains Mandate
Dominionism and theocracy
January 6 and religious imagery
(20 min)
Project 2025: Religion and authoritarian governance (15 min)
Immigration, climate change, and religious divides
The resistance: Religious opposition to Trump and MAGA (15 min)

SCAFFOLDING FOR WEEK 10:

Students have the historical context and analytical tools to synthesize what they've learned and grapple with fundamental questions about meaning and trajectory.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════
WEEK 10: MEANING, MODERNITY, AND THE FUTURE

LEARNING OBJECTIVES:

  • Synthesize the course themes and identify recurring patterns
  • Analyze competing narratives about religion and politics in America
  • Develop informed perspectives on current debates and future possibilities

SESSION CONTENT:

What have we learned? Recurring patterns across 400 years:
Religion as force for liberation and oppression
The impossibility of separating religion from politics
The danger and necessity of religious engagement in public life
The evolution of the "wall of separation"
(20 min)
Competing narratives about America:
Christian nation vs. secular republic
Declension narrative vs. progress narrative
Pluralism vs. particularism
(20 min)
The current moment in historical perspective:
Is this time different? How?
What does the long view tell us about our current crisis?
The demography of American religion: What comes next?
(20 min)
The future:
The "nones" and the decline of religious affiliation
Multiracial democracy and religious pluralism
Climate crisis and religious response
Technology, AI, and the future of meaning
What role should religion play in American public life?
(30 min)
Final discussion: What does this history mean for you as citizens?

COURSE-LONG SCAFFOLDING THEMES

These themes are introduced in Week 1 and revisited throughout:

1. TENSION BETWEEN INDIVIDUAL AND COMMUNITY

Week 2: Colonial religious experiments
Week 3: Individual awakening vs. church authority
Week 4: Individual rights vs. majority rule
Week 5: Abolitionist conscience vs. social order
Week 7: Individual conscience in Civil Rights
Week 8-9: Individual freedom vs. Christian nation

2. ESTABLISHMENT VS. DISESTABLISHMENT

Week 2: Colonial establishments
Week 4: Constitutional settlement
Week 5: Voluntary religion flourishes
Week 6-9: Cultural establishment vs. legal disestablishment
Week 10: What comes after cultural establishment?

3. RELIGION AS REFORM VS. RELIGION AS REACTION

Week 3: Awakening as democratization
Week 5: Abolition and pro-slavery theology
Week 6: Social Gospel and fundamentalism
Week 7: Civil Rights and white resistance
Week 8-9: Liberation theology and Christian nationalism

4. PROPHETIC WITNESS VS. CULTURAL ACCOMMODATION

Week 5: John Brown vs. mainstream abolition
Week 7: MLK vs. white moderates
Week 8: Moral Majority vs. mainline churches
Week 9: Never Trump evangelicals vs. court evangelicals

ASSESSMENT AND PARTICIPATION SUGGESTIONS

Given the BOLLI context (adult learners, no formal grades):

WEEKLY PREPARATION:

Brief readings assigned (primary sources when possible)
Discussion questions posted in advance
Optional supplementary materials for deeper engagement

IN-CLASS STRUCTURE (suggested for each 90-minute session):

Brief recap and connection to previous week (5-10 min)
Presentation of historical content (40-50 min)
Small group or full group discussion (25-30 min)
Preview of next week and scaffolding connection (5-10 min)

OPTIONAL ENRICHMENT:

Documentary viewing suggestions
Museum/site visits (if local)
Guest speakers from religious communities
Primary source analysis workshops
