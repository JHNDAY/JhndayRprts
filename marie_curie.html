Light That Burns

A Laboratory Saturated in Radiation

For over three decades, Marie Curie worked directly with radioactive materials with no protective equipment. She carried radioactive isotopes in her coat pockets, stored them in her desk drawer, and kept radium samples in her home. Her notebooks ‚Äî still radioactive 90 years later ‚Äî must be handled with lead gloves.

From the 1900s onward: chronically sore and cracked fingers (radiation burns), progressive deterioration of eyesight including near-blindness (cataracts from radiation exposure), tinnitus, persistent fatigue, and extreme weight loss in her final years.

Aplastic Anemia ‚Äî The Fatal Diagnosis

Aplastic anemia occurs when bone marrow stops producing blood cells ‚Äî a known consequence of radiation exposure. It causes severe fatigue, bleeding, and immune failure. This was the disease that killed her in 1934, though it had been destroying her gradually for years.

While Curie's exposure was unintentional ignorance, the Radium Girls ‚Äî dial painters who were told to lip-point their radium brushes ‚Äî were knowingly put at risk by employers who understood the danger but suppressed it. Would That Kind of Support Be Needed Today?

Women now earn more than half of STEM bachelor's degrees in many countries ‚Äî a transformation Curie helped make conceivable. Yet women remain underrepresented in senior research positions, physics professorships, and national science academies worldwide.
