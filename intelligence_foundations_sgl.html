The Partnership Model

AI doesn't replace your expertise‚Äîit amplifies it. Think of digital intelligence as a research assistant who's read everything but needs your judgment to know what matters. Your organic intelligence provides the "why" and the "so what"; AI provides the "how" and the "help me organize this."

Course Development Example:

You bring: "I want to teach about the fall of the Roman Republic, focusing on how political dysfunction mirrors modern concerns, targeting students who love history but may not know the details."

AI brings: "Here are 6 different ways to structure that course, each emphasizing different aspects. Here's how each session could progress. Here are key figures to focus on. Here are contemporary parallels to explore."

Together you create: A course that reflects your wisdom about teaching and your passion for the subject, but structured more efficiently than you could have done alone.

Conversation ‚â† Perfection

Your first prompt doesn't need to be perfect. In fact, "messy" prompts are often better! AI can sort through your stream of consciousness, identify the key ideas, and organize them coherently. This is why we emphasize "fruit salad" prompts‚Äîthrow in all your ingredients, and AI helps you make the salad.

Real SGL Conversation:

The Balance Principle

Effective AI partnership means understanding which tasks play to each intelligence's strengths. You shouldn't try to do what AI does better, and AI can't do what requires your human judgment. The art is knowing which is which‚Äîand that's what this training teaches.

Red Flags: When Balance Is Off

You're asking AI to do too much when: You accept AI's first output without critique, let AI make pedagogical decisions you should make, or use AI-generated content without adding your voice and personality.
You're not using AI enough when: You're spending hours on formatting and organization, reinventing structures that could be templated, or avoiding technology because tasks feel overwhelming.
You've found the balance when: Course development feels like an energizing collaboration, you're spending more time on creative vision than logistics, and the final product sounds distinctly like you (not generic AI).

Practical Balancing Strategy

The "Three-Pass" Method:

Pass 1 (You Lead): Share your vision, expertise, and rough ideas with AI. This is your "fruit salad" prompt.

Pass 2 (AI Leads): Let AI organize, structure, draft, and generate options based on your input.

Pass 3 (You Lead): Critique, refine, personalize, and finalize based on your pedagogical judgment.

This rhythm‚Äîyou lead, AI leads, you lead‚Äîcreates balanced partnership.

üìñMeaning of Words

How AI learns meaning from patterns‚Äîa three-level understanding

Words Are the Interface

To understand how AI processes your prompts, we need to think about meaning at three different levels. Let's use a simple example‚Äîthe word "dog"‚Äîto see how meaning works in AI, from concrete to complex.

Level 1: The Concrete Instance

Example: "dog"

At the most basic level, "dog" refers to a specific, concrete thing: a four-legged domesticated animal, genus Canis. This is the dictionary definition‚Äîthe literal, denotative meaning. When you write "I want to teach about dogs," AI recognizes this as the animal.

For course development: When you use concrete, specific words‚Äî"Roman Republic," "climate change," "watercolor painting"‚ÄîAI has clear, unambiguous concepts to work with. This is your foundation.

Level 2: Contextual Transformation

Example: "You sly dog!"

Now watch what happens when context changes everything. "You sly dog!" doesn't refer to an animal at all‚Äîit's about a person behaving cleverly or cunningly. The word "dog" has been completely transformed by the context around it: the word "sly," the exclamation point, the phrase structure.

For course development: This is why context matters so much in your prompts. The same words mean different things based on what surrounds them. "Revolution" means one thing when discussing astronomy, another in political history, and something else entirely in technology. When you give AI context‚Äî"I'm teaching BOLLI students," "focus on social impact," "emphasize contemporary relevance"‚Äîyou're transforming how every word in your prompt gets interpreted.

This is why "fruit salad" prompts work! Even messy, stream-of-consciousness input provides contextual clues that help AI understand which meaning you intend for each word.

Level 3: The Vast Corpus of Patterns

Example: All the ways "dog" appears in millions of texts

Here's where AI's true power lies. During training, the AI has encountered "dog" in millions of different contexts across countless texts:

  • "dog" near "bark," "fetch," "leash," "veterinarian" (animal context)
  • "dog" near "sly," "crafty," "cunning" (personality metaphor)
  • "hot dog" near "baseball," "mustard," "bun" (food context)
  • "dog days of summer" near "heat," "lazy," "August" (idiomatic usage)
  • "underdog" near "competition," "victory," "unlikely" (metaphorical status)
  • "dog-eared pages" near "book," "worn," "well-read" (descriptive metaphor)
The key insight: AI doesn't have a definition of "dog" like a dictionary does. Instead, it has learned statistical patterns about how "dog" appears in relationship to millions of other words across billions of sentences. When you use a word in your prompt, AI activates all the patterns it has learned about that word and uses surrounding context to figure out which patterns are relevant.

Understanding Practical Application

Two Critical Word Challenges in Course Development

Challenge 1: Using Words to Identify Relationships

When structuring your course, you need words to describe how concepts relate to each other. The AI uses its corpus knowledge to understand relationship words: "leads to," "contrasts with," "builds upon," "provides context for."

Example: "Start with Enlightenment philosophy because it sets up our discussion of American Revolution which then contrasts with French Revolution outcomes."

The relationship words (in italics) tell AI how to structure your course logic.

Challenge 2: Using Words to Find Facts

With RAG (Research mode), you search for current information using words. Your search terms activate different patterns in the corpus, which determines what gets retrieved. Too generic and you get irrelevant results; too specific and you miss important connections.

Generic: "climate" ‚Üí returns everything from weather to office climates to political climates

Targeted: "climate policy implementation challenges" ‚Üí returns scholarly articles, policy documents, and expert analysis

The corpus patterns help AI understand what you're really looking for.

Why This Matters for SGLs

Your advantage: As an educator, you already understand how context shapes meaning‚Äîit's what you do when you explain concepts to students! When a BOLLI student asks about "revolution," you naturally clarify: "Do you mean political revolution, industrial revolution, or scientific revolution?" You're already expert at using words precisely and providing context.
The transition: Working with AI just means applying those same language skills in a slightly different way. Instead of clarifying for a student, you're providing context for a system that has seen millions of examples but needs you to specify which patterns are relevant. You're not learning a new skill‚Äîyou're adapting an existing one.
