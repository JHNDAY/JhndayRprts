Step 1: Creating Your Initial Narrative Foundational

Start with your "fruit salad of words"‚Äîthe raw material of your course

What is a "Fruit Salad of Words"?

Your initial narrative is an unorganized collection of thoughts, themes, questions, and ideas‚Äîexactly as they occur to you. Don't worry about structure, grammar, or completeness. Think of it as:

  • Scribbling in book margins ‚Äì quick notes and reactions
  • Stream of consciousness ‚Äì following your natural thought process
  • Brainstorm notes ‚Äì capturing what excites you about the topic
  • Personal observations ‚Äì your unique perspective and insights

üí° Key Concept: Embrace the Mess

The power of this approach is that you DON'T need to organize your thoughts first. AI excels at finding patterns and creating structure from disorganized input. Your job is simply to capture what you know and care about.

How to Create Your Narrative

  1. Choose your medium

    Start wherever you're most comfortable‚Äîa blank document, voice memo transcription, handwritten notes you scan, or direct typing into Claude.

  2. Dump your knowledge

    Write everything you know about the topic without worrying about order or completeness. Include questions you want to explore, themes that interest you, and specific examples you want to cover.

  3. Add your passion

    Include why this topic matters to you, what excites you about teaching it, and what you hope students will take away. This personal connection is what makes your course unique.

  4. Note any constraints

    Mention practical considerations like course length, student background, or specific BOLLI requirements, but don't let these limit your brainstorming.

üìù Example: Religion and Politics Course Narrative

Here's how one SGL started their course development:

"I'm interested in church-state relations in America. How religion has always been part of politics but not always in reactionary ways. Abolition, women's suffrage, Civil Rights‚Äîall had religious foundations. The Puritans, obviously. But also look at modern evangelical influence, the Catholic bishops, how Jewish and Muslim communities engage politically.

I want students to understand this isn't new‚Äîreligion and politics have always intersected. Maybe start with Puritans? Cover different time periods. Progressive religion vs conservative religion. Definitely need to address Project 2025 and Opus Dei since that's in the news.

10 weeks, 90 minutes each. BOLLI students are 55+, well-educated, politically aware but might not know religious history details. Need to be balanced‚Äîthis topic can be divisive."

Notice: This narrative is conversational, includes questions, mentions specific examples, notes constraints, and reveals the instructor's passion and concerns. It's perfect AI input material!

üí° Pro Tips for Your Narrative

  • Length doesn't matter ‚Äì Some narratives are 100 words, others 1000+. Write until you've captured your thinking.
  • Include questions ‚Äì "Should I cover X?" or "How do I balance Y and Z?" AI can help answer these.
  • Use examples ‚Äì Specific cases, stories, or instances you want to include
  • Express uncertainty ‚Äì "I'm not sure how to..." or "Maybe..." shows where you need AI support

Step 2: Crafting Your AI Prompt Core

Transform your narrative into a structured request that guides AI output

What Makes a Good Course Development Prompt?

A prompt is your instruction set to the AI. Unlike your free-flowing narrative, the prompt provides specific constraints and requirements that shape the AI's response. Think of it as the difference between brainstorming and giving an assignment.

üí° Key Concept: Constraints Create Quality

Paradoxically, the more specific your constraints, the better your results. "Create a course" is too vague. "Create a 10-week course, 90-minute sessions, progressively building from foundational concepts" gives AI clear parameters to work within.

Essential Prompt Components

‚úÖ What to Include in Your Prompt

  1. Course structure ‚Äì Number of weeks/sessions, session length
  2. Pedagogical approach ‚Äì How topics should scaffold and build
  3. Output format ‚Äì What you want to receive (outline, session descriptions, etc.)
  4. Audience context ‚Äì Student background and expectations
  5. Specific requirements ‚Äì Any BOLLI guidelines or personal preferences
  6. Reference to your narrative ‚Äì Explicit instruction to use your input

Building Your Prompt Step by Step

  1. Create the "Prompt" tab in your Google Doc

    Add a second tab to your document and name it "Prompt". This keeps your materials organized and makes them easy to reference later.

  2. Start with the structural requirements

    Begin by stating the basic course parameters clearly.

    Based on my narrative in the previous tab, please create a 10-week course outline. Each session should be 90 minutes long.
  3. Specify pedagogical approach

    Explain how you want topics organized and developed.

    The course should scaffold topics, building on concepts introduced in earlier weeks. Start with foundational historical context before moving to contemporary issues.
  4. Define output format

    Be explicit about what you want to receive.

    For each week, provide: - Week number and session title - 2-3 key learning objectives - Main topics to cover (4-5 bullet points) - Discussion questions for student engagement (2-3 questions) - Suggested readings or resources (optional)
  5. Add audience and context notes

    Help AI understand who you're teaching.

    Keep in mind this is for BOLLI learners (ages 55+) who are educated and politically aware but may not have deep background in religious history. Maintain balanced, scholarly tone while addressing contemporary debates.

üìù Complete Example Prompt

Here's how all these elements come together:

Based on my narrative in the "Narrative" tab, please create a 10-week course outline on religion and politics in America. Each session should be 90 minutes long. The course should scaffold topics, building concepts introduced in earlier weeks. Start with foundational historical context (Puritans, colonial period) before moving to 19th-20th century developments, then contemporary issues. For each week, provide: - Week number and session title - 2-3 key learning objectives - Main topics to cover (4-5 bullet points) - Discussion questions for student engagement (2-3 questions) - Suggested readings or resources Keep in mind this is for BOLLI learners (ages 55+) who are educated and politically aware but may not have deep background in religious history or theology. Maintain a balanced, scholarly tone while addressing contemporary debates. The goal is to help students understand the long historical relationship between religion and politics in America, not just current controversies. Please ensure the course addresses both progressive and conservative religious movements, and includes diverse faith traditions (Protestant, Catholic, Jewish, Muslim where relevant to political history).

Advanced Prompting Techniques

üéØ Refining Your Prompts

  • Be directive ‚Äì Use imperative language: "Create," "Organize," "Include"
  • Use examples ‚Äì "Like Week 3, each week should have..."
  • Request specific styles ‚Äì "Write session titles that are engaging but scholarly"
  • Build in flexibility ‚Äì "Suggest alternatives for weeks that might need more time"
  • Reference SGL competencies ‚Äì Connect to BOLLI's course development framework

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Prompt Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Being too vague ‚Äì "Create a good course" doesn't give AI enough direction
  • Contradictory instructions ‚Äì Asking for both "brief overview" and "comprehensive detail"
  • Assuming knowledge ‚Äì Don't assume AI knows BOLLI-specific requirements unless you state them
  • Forgetting the narrative ‚Äì Always reference your initial narrative so AI uses your ideas

Testing and Iterating

Your first prompt might not be perfect, and that's okay! The workflow is iterative. You can:

  • Run your prompt and see what you get
  • Refine the prompt based on the results
  • Add clarifying constraints
  • Request modifications to specific sections

Step 3: AI Processing & Response Core

Understand how AI synthesizes your input with its training data

What Happens During AI Processing?

When you submit your narrative and prompt to Claude AI, something remarkable happens: the AI doesn't just reorganize your words‚Äîit synthesizes your input with its contextual knowledge from training data, organizing and expanding your themes in sophisticated ways.

üí° Key Concept: Synthesis, Not Just Organization

AI processing involves three simultaneous operations:

  • Pattern Recognition ‚Äì Identifying themes and relationships in your narrative
  • Knowledge Integration ‚Äì Drawing from vast training data to contextualize your ideas
  • Structured Output ‚Äì Organizing everything according to your prompt specifications

This is why your "fruit salad of words" can become a coherent course outline‚ÄîAI finds the underlying structure.

Submitting Your Request to Claude

  1. Open Claude AI

    Go to claude.ai (use your BOLLI-provided account or personal account)

  2. Start a new conversation

    Click "New conversation" to ensure a clean context. Each course development should have its own conversation.

  3. Submit your narrative first

    Copy your narrative from the Google Doc "Narrative" tab and paste it into Claude. Add a simple instruction like: "Here's my initial thinking about a BOLLI course I want to develop. I'll follow up with specific instructions."

  4. Then submit your prompt

    In your next message, copy and paste your complete prompt from the "Prompt" tab. This two-step approach gives Claude clear context before instructions.

  5. Wait for the response

    Claude will process and generate a response. For a 10-week course outline, expect 1-2 minutes of processing time.

üí° Pro Tips for Better AI Responses

  • Provide context first ‚Äì Always share your narrative before your prompt
  • Use Projects ‚Äì Create a Claude Project for your course development to maintain context across sessions
  • Reference previous responses ‚Äì In follow-ups, you can say "Based on the outline you just created..."
  • Be conversational ‚Äì You can ask clarifying questions or request modifications naturally

What to Expect in the Response

Claude's response will typically include:

  • Structured outline ‚Äì Organized according to your prompt specifications
  • Expanded content ‚Äì More detail than you provided, drawn from AI's knowledge base
  • Balanced perspective ‚Äì Attempt to address multiple viewpoints (especially important for sensitive topics)
  • Suggested refinements ‚Äì AI may note areas that could use more development

Evaluating the AI Response

When you receive your response, ask yourself:

‚úÖ Quality Checklist

  • Accuracy ‚Äì Are the facts and examples correct?
  • Completeness ‚Äì Are all your key themes addressed?
  • Scaffolding ‚Äì Do topics build logically from week to week?
  • Balance ‚Äì For controversial topics, are multiple perspectives included?
  • BOLLI-appropriate ‚Äì Is the content suitable for your audience?
  • Teaching-ready ‚Äì Can you actually teach this, or does it need refinement?

Iterating on the Response

The first response is rarely perfect. You can refine it by:

Example follow-up requests: "Week 7 seems too ambitious. Can you split those topics across two weeks?" "Can you add more specific primary source suggestions for Week 3?" "The discussion questions feel too broad. Make them more focused on specific readings or concepts." "I'd like Week 5 to include more about Jewish political engagement. Can you expand that section?"

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: AI Has Limits

  • Verify specialized facts ‚Äì AI can make errors, especially with niche topics
  • Add your expertise ‚Äì You know your field better than AI; trust your judgment
  • Consider your students ‚Äì AI doesn't know your specific BOLLI audience like you do
  • Maintain your voice ‚Äì Don't let AI's style override your teaching personality

Step 4: Export to Google Docs Foundational

"Get out of the chatbot window" and into a real document

Why Export to Google Docs?

The chat interface in Claude is excellent for generating content, but it's not designed for serious editing, collaboration, or long-term storage. Think of it like using a restaurant kitchen‚Äîgreat for cooking, but you don't eat there. Moving to Google Docs is "getting out of the chatbot window" and into your actual workspace.

üí° Key Concept: Chat vs. Document

Claude's chat interface is for generation. Google Docs is for refinement. Once you have AI output that's close to what you need, export it immediately so you can:

  • Edit with full word processing capabilities
  • Share with colleagues for feedback
  • Track changes and revisions over time
  • Integrate with other course materials
  • Create a permanent record of your work

The Export Process

  1. Create the "Response" tab in your Google Doc

    Go back to your course development Google Doc (the one where you have "Narrative" and "Prompt" tabs). Create a third tab named "Response".

  2. Copy Claude's output

    In the Claude chat window, click the copy icon (üìã) at the bottom of Claude's response. This copies the entire formatted response to your clipboard.

    üí° Pro Tip: Copying Options

    Claude offers multiple copy options:

    • Copy ‚Äì Copies formatted text with basic styling
    • Copy as Markdown ‚Äì Preserves structure but removes formatting

    For most purposes, use the standard "Copy" option to maintain readability in Google Docs.

  3. Paste into the "Response" tab

    Switch to your Google Doc's "Response" tab and paste (Cmd/Ctrl + V). The content will appear with basic formatting preserved.

  4. Clean up the formatting

    Google Docs may import some odd formatting. Quickly scan and:

    • Ensure headings are properly styled (use "Normal text" dropdown)
    • Check that bullet points are formatted correctly
    • Verify that any tables or special formatting came through properly
  5. Add header information

    At the top of the Response tab, add context:

    Course Outline Generated by Claude AI Date: [today's date] Based on: Narrative and Prompt in previous tabs Status: Initial draft for review [Claude's response begins here...]

Step 5: Create Interactive Visualization Advanced

Generate HTML visualization of your course structure for exploration

Why Create a Visualization?

A text outline is excellent for linear reading, but an interactive HTML visualization allows you and your colleagues to explore your course structure in a more dynamic way. You can:

  • See the entire course at a glance
  • Collapse and expand sections to focus on specific weeks
  • Identify gaps or imbalances in topic distribution
  • Share an engaging preview with potential students or administrators
  • Navigate quickly between different parts of the course

üí° Key Concept: Data Visualization for Learning Design

Visualizing your course structure helps you think differently about your content. Linear text emphasizes sequence; interactive visualization emphasizes relationships, proportions, and overall architecture. Both perspectives are valuable for course development.

Creating Your Visualization with Claude

  1. Return to your Claude conversation

    Go back to the conversation where you generated your course outline (use the link you saved in Step 4).

  2. Request an HTML visualization

    Use a prompt like this:

    Please create an interactive HTML visualization of this course outline. Include: - BOLLI purple gradient branding (similar to my other materials) - Collapsible sections for each week - Clear visual hierarchy showing sessions, objectives, topics, and discussion questions - Smooth navigation between weeks - A course overview section at the top - Mobile-responsive design Make it visually appealing and easy to navigate, suitable for sharing with colleagues or presenting to students.
  3. Review and request refinements

    Claude will generate the HTML code. You can ask for modifications:

    • "Make the purple gradient more subtle"
    • "Add a table of contents with jump links"
    • "Include a timeline view showing the progression"
    • "Add icons for different types of content"
  4. Save the HTML file

    Claude will typically create this as an "Artifact" with a download option. Save it with a clear name like "Religion_Politics_Course_Visualization.html"

  5. Test the visualization

    Open the HTML file in your web browser to ensure:

    • All sections expand and collapse properly
    • Navigation works smoothly
    • The design is readable and attractive
    • No content is cut off or improperly formatted

üí° Pro Tips for Effective Visualizations

  • Keep it self-contained ‚Äì The HTML file should work offline without external dependencies
  • Match your brand ‚Äì Use BOLLI's purple gradient and professional styling
  • Test on mobile ‚Äì Many colleagues will view this on tablets or phones
  • Include metadata ‚Äì Add course title, your name, date created in the visualization
  • Version control ‚Äì Save different versions as you refine your course

Storing Your Visualization

You have several options for storing and sharing your HTML visualization:

üìÅ Storage Options

  1. Google Drive
    • Upload the HTML file to Google Drive

    • They can download and open it locally
  2. Link from Google Doc
    • Upload to Google Drive first
    • Get the shareable link

  3. Email attachment

What Makes a Good Course Visualization?

‚úÖ Quality Checklist for Visualizations

  • Clear hierarchy ‚Äì Easy to distinguish weeks, topics, subtopics
  • Readable typography ‚Äì Not too small, good line spacing, clear headings
  • Intuitive navigation ‚Äì Obvious how to expand/collapse, scroll, navigate
  • Visual consistency ‚Äì Unified color scheme and styling throughout
  • Responsive design ‚Äì Works on desktop, tablet, and phone
  • Fast loading ‚Äì Opens quickly without delays
  • Accessible ‚Äì Works with screen readers, keyboard navigation

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Visualization Mistakes

  • Too much information ‚Äì Showing everything at once is overwhelming. Use collapsible sections!
  • Poor color contrast ‚Äì Light text on light backgrounds is hard to read
  • No mobile optimization ‚Äì Many people will view on phones/tablets
  • Broken functionality ‚Äì Always test before sharing
  • Missing context ‚Äì Include course title, instructor name, term

Why Collaboration Matters

Course development benefits enormously from external perspectives. Your colleagues can:

  • Spot gaps or redundancies you might have missed
  • Suggest readings or resources from their expertise
  • Identify potential sensitive topics that need careful handling
  • Offer pedagogical improvements based on their teaching experience

  • Validate that your course meets BOLLI standards

üí° Key Concept: The Discussion Tab

  3. Prepare your sharing package

  4. Set Google Doc permissions

    Click "Share" in Google Docs and:

    • Add reviewers' email addresses
    • Set permissions to "Can comment" (not "Can edit") to preserve your original
    • Enable "Notify people" so they receive an email alert

The Complete Workflow in Action

Let's walk through the entire process using a real example: developing a course on "Religion and Politics in America from 1600 to 2026."

üí° Remember: This is Iterative, Not Linear

While we present these steps sequentially, real course development involves moving back and forth between stages. You might return to your narrative after seeing AI output, refine your prompt based on results, or loop through collaboration multiple times. That's not just okay‚Äîit's ideal.

Phase 1: Narrative (Week 1, Days 1-2)

üìù The Instructor's Process

Context: Sarah, a retired political science professor, wants to teach about religion and politics at BOLLI. She has expertise in American political history but isn't a religious studies specialist.

Her narrative creation:

Sarah spends about an hour brainstorming in a Google Doc. She writes about her concerns with church-state separation debates, notes that she wants to show religion's role in progressive movements (not just conservative ones), lists specific topics she knows well (Puritans, Civil Rights movement) and areas where she needs more depth (Catholic political engagement, Jewish political thought). She includes questions: "How do I balance coverage of different faiths? Should I go chronologically or thematically?"

Key insight: Sarah doesn't try to solve these questions in her narrative. She just captures them as part of her thinking. This helps AI understand both her knowledge and her uncertainties.

Phase 2: Prompt (Week 1, Days 2-3)

‚úèÔ∏è Crafting Effective Instructions

Sarah creates her prompt by answering these questions:

  1. Structure: "10 weeks, 90 minutes each"
  2. Approach: "Chronological with thematic threads"
  3. Audience: "BOLLI learners, 55+, politically aware"
  4. Tone: "Scholarly but accessible, balanced"
  5. Output: "Week number, title, objectives, topics, discussion questions"

She explicitly tells Claude to use her narrative as the foundation and to help her address the questions she raised about faith coverage and organization.

Phase 3: AI Processing (Week 1, Day 3)

ü§ñ What Claude Adds

Claude takes Sarah's narrative and prompt and:

  • Organizes her scattered topics into a chronological progression
  • Adds historical context she didn't mention (Second Great Awakening, Know-Nothing Party)
  • Suggests Catholic and Jewish perspectives to balance Protestant focus
  • Creates scaffolded learning objectives that build across weeks
  • Generates discussion questions that connect historical examples to contemporary issues
  • Recommends primary sources Sarah hadn't considered

Result: A 10-week outline that's about 80% ready but needs Sarah's expertise to refine.

Phase 4: Export (Week 1, Day 4)

üìÑ Moving to Real Documents

Sarah copies the Claude output into her Google Doc's "Response" tab. She immediately notices:

  • Week 3 combines too many topics‚Äîneeds to be split
  • Discussion questions for Week 7 are too broad‚Äîneed more specificity
  • Learning objectives are strong but could align better with Bloom's taxonomy

She makes notes about these in the document, knowing she'll address them during revision. The key is: she's now working in a real document where she can edit freely, not trapped in a chat interface.

Phase 5: Visualization (Week 2, Day 1)

üìä Creating the Interactive View

Sarah returns to Claude and requests an HTML visualization. She sees her course laid out visually and immediately recognizes:

  • Weeks 1-4 (colonial through antebellum) are well-paced
  • Weeks 5-7 (Civil War through mid-20th century) feel rushed
  • Weeks 8-10 (contemporary period) are overstuffed

This visual perspective reveals pacing issues that weren't obvious in linear text. She notes these for discussion with reviewers.

Phase 6: Collaboration (Week 2-3)

ü§ù The Feedback Process

Sarah shares her complete package with three reviewers:

  1. Dr. Rachel Goldstein (Jewish Studies) ‚Äì Suggests adding more on Jewish political activism in 1960s-70s, notes that treatment of Israel/Palestine politics needs careful framing
  2. Rev. Marcus Washington (Black Church History) ‚Äì Recommends stronger emphasis on Black church's role in abolition and Civil Rights, suggests specific primary sources
  3. Prof. Tim O'Brien (Catholic historian) ‚Äì Offers to provide readings on Catholic social teaching, notes that Catholic-Protestant tensions in 19th century deserve more attention

Sarah's response: She documents all feedback in her Discussion tab, categorizes by priority, and creates a revision plan. She accepts most suggestions but decides to keep contemporary Israeli-Palestinian politics out of scope, focusing instead on American Jewish political engagement.

Phase 7: Final Refinement (Week 3-4)

üîß Bringing It Home

Sarah makes final revisions:

  • Splits overloaded weeks into more manageable chunks
  • Incorporates specific primary sources suggested by reviewers
  • Adds more nuanced treatment of different faith traditions
  • Refines discussion questions based on BOLLI audience
  • Writes her course description for the BOLLI catalog

She returns to Claude for help rewriting the now-split Week 3 and generating a few more discussion questions that better match her refined direction.

Final product: A polished, balanced course that represents her vision, informed by AI assistance and colleague expertise, documented thoroughly in her four-tab Google Doc.

‚úÖ What Made This Successful

  2. Specific prompt ‚Äì Clear constraints and desired output format
  3. Critical evaluation ‚Äì She didn't accept AI output wholesale but evaluated it against her expertise
  4. Visual perspective ‚Äì The HTML visualization revealed pacing issues
  5. Targeted expertise ‚Äì She sought reviewers who could fill her knowledge gaps
  6. Iterative refinement ‚Äì Multiple rounds of revision based on different inputs
  7. Documentation ‚Äì Complete record of development process in structured Google Doc

  • Skipping collaboration ‚Äì Would have missed important perspectives on different faith traditions

  • No visualization ‚Äì Pacing problems might not have been apparent
  • Poor documentation ‚Äì Future revisions would require starting over
