Inherited Brokerage Accounts
vs. Your 401(k)

Step-up basis · Capital gains · RMDs · How your tax obligations differ
Your Question I have inherited over a million dollars invested in stocks and bonds and will maintain this in a brokerage account. I am familiar with a 401(k) — I have deferred income over the years without paying taxes, and now as I take yearly RMDs I pay taxes. I understand the cost basis was stepped up to fair market value at the time of the original owner's death. When I sell, I expect a taxable event occurs — but since different assets may have been acquired at different times, I'm not sure how to calculate what I actually owe in federal taxes on each sale. I want to understand brokerage withdrawals step by step — using my specific situation as the example — and see clearly how they differ from 401(k) withdrawals.

The Core Difference

In your 401(k), you never paid tax on the money going in — so when it comes out, every dollar is taxed as ordinary income, just like a paycheck. Your inherited brokerage account works the opposite way: the original owner invested after-tax dollars, and when you sell, only the growth above your stepped-up cost basis is taxed — and at the lower long-term capital gains rate, not ordinary income rates. This distinction is significant: a $50,000 RMD from your 401(k) is fully taxable; a $50,000 sale from your brokerage account where the gain is only $8,000 means you owe tax on just that $8,000.

Step-Up Basis — Your Key Advantage

When the original owner died, the IRS "reset" the cost basis of every asset in the account to its fair market value on that date. Any gain that accumulated during the original owner's lifetime is permanently erased for tax purposes — you will never owe tax on it. This is the step-up in basis, and it is one of the most valuable tax benefits in the entire tax code.

For your situation: if a stock was purchased for $30 per share decades ago and was worth $120 per share on the date of death, your cost basis is $120 — not $30. If you sell that stock today at $145, you owe tax only on the $25 gain since the date of death. The original $90 of appreciation is gone from the tax calculation entirely.

Important note about your trust situation: Because the assets were held in a family trust after the original owner's death, the step-up in basis was established at the date of death — not when the trust transferred assets to you. Your brokerage firm should have records of the stepped-up cost basis for each position. Request a cost basis report before you sell anything.

A Worked Example — The Numbers Step by Step

Suppose you sell 500 shares of a stock that was stepped up to $120/share at the date of death, and you sell it today for $145/share. Here is exactly how the federal tax is calculated:

Calculating Federal Tax on a Brokerage Sale

Original owner's purchase price (decades ago) $30 / share
Your stepped-up cost basis (date of death) $120 / share
Your sale price today $145 / share
Your taxable gain per share ($145 − $120) $25 / share
Number of shares sold 500 shares
Total taxable gain (500 × $25) $12,500
Long-term capital gains rate (typical for retirees) 15%
Federal tax owed on this sale $1,875
Total gain that existed — erased by step-up (500 × $90) $45,000 tax-free
Note: Inherited assets are always treated as long-term holdings regardless of how long you have personally held them — so the lower long-term capital gains rate (0%, 15%, or 20%) applies immediately. You do not need to wait a year. Whether you owe 0%, 15%, or 20% depends on your total taxable income for the year; your tax advisor can help you project this.

When Assets Were Acquired at Different Times

Because the step-up in basis is applied asset by asset as of the date of death, each holding in your brokerage account has its own individual cost basis — regardless of when the original owner purchased it. A stock bought in 1985 and a bond bought in 2010 both get stepped up to their respective fair market values on the same date: the date of death.

When you sell, your brokerage firm is required to report your cost basis on Form 1099-B, which you receive each January for the prior tax year. This form will show the proceeds of the sale, your cost basis, and the resulting gain or loss — the same three numbers you need to complete Schedule D of your federal tax return. Before selling anything, ask your brokerage for a full cost basis report so you can see every asset's individual basis and estimate your tax liability.

Practical tip: If you have both highly appreciated assets (large gain above basis) and some assets near or below basis, you have flexibility in which ones you sell first. Selling assets with smaller gains — or even small losses — can reduce your tax bill for the year. This is called tax-lot management and is worth discussing with a financial advisor or CPA.

Key Terms to Know

Cost Basis
The value from which your taxable gain is measured. For your inherited account, this is the stepped-up fair market value on the date of death — not the original purchase price.
Step-Up in Basis
The IRS rule that resets an inherited asset's cost basis to its fair market value on the date the original owner died, erasing all previously accumulated gain.
Capital Gain
The profit from selling an asset — calculated as sale price minus cost basis. Only this amount is taxed, not the full sale proceeds.
Long-Term Capital Gains Rate
The preferential tax rate (0%, 15%, or 20%) that applies to gains on assets held more than one year — or, in your case, all inherited assets automatically.
Ordinary Income Rate
Your regular tax bracket rate (10%–37%), applied to 401(k) withdrawals, RMDs, wages, and Social Security. Generally higher than capital gains rates.
RMD (Required Minimum Distribution)
The mandatory annual withdrawal from your 401(k) beginning at age 73. No equivalent requirement exists for a brokerage account.
Form 1099-B
The tax form your brokerage sends each January showing proceeds, cost basis, and gain or loss for every sale made in the prior year. The essential document for filing.
Taxable Event
A transaction that triggers a tax obligation. For your brokerage account, selling an asset is a taxable event. Holding it, inheriting it, or transferring it generally is not.

Practical Next Steps

Before making any sales, contact your brokerage and request a complete cost basis report showing the stepped-up basis for every position — this is the foundation of every tax calculation you will make going forward. When you do sell, your brokerage's Form 1099-B will report the gain automatically; you or your tax preparer will transfer those figures to Schedule D of your federal return. Given the size of this inheritance, a one-time consultation with a CPA or tax advisor who handles investment accounts is well worth the cost — particularly to project how brokerage gains interact with your 401(k) RMDs and Social Security in determining your overall tax bracket each year.
