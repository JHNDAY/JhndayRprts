Liberal Media's MAGA-Like Splintering

Challenges for 2028 Presidential Contenders
Axios | February 1, 2026 | Discussion Guide
Read original article →

Core Thesis

Liberal media is undergoing a transformation similar to the early MAGA movement, fragmenting from a few dominant national players (New York Times columnists, Rachel Maddow) into countless micro-factions and solo operators with large audiences. This fundamental shift forces 2028 presidential contenders to navigate a complex, multi-layered media ecosystem rather than simply courting a handful of traditional gatekeepers.

1. The Five-Tier Liberal Media Ecosystem

▼

Tier 1: The Partisans (Friendly Territory)

Safe spaces for Democratic candidates with little adversarial pushback. These outlets prioritize message amplification over challenging interviews.

Key Players:
  • MeidasTouch - Democratic-leaning commentary reaching millions
  • No Lie with Brian Tyler Cohen - Progressive analysis and interviews
"There's kind of this whole Rolodex of influencers who are even friendlier than MSNBC" - Jesse Lehrich, Democratic strategist

Tier 2: The Resistance (Ideological Enforcers)

The most strident anti-Trump voices who will challenge Democrats perceived as "squishy" on opposing the Trump administration. Loyalty tests for 2028 contenders.

Key Players:
  • "I've Had It" podcast - Jennifer Welch and Angie Sullivan, known for confrontational interviews
  • Heather Cox Richardson's Substack - Historical perspective with anti-Trump focus
Warning: Interviews aren't always friendly. Former Chicago Mayor Rahm Emanuel was "blasted" on "I've Had It" for breaking from orthodoxy. Candidates must prove "resistance bona fides" during Trump 2.0.

Tier 3: The Elites (Traditional Gatekeepers)

Legacy outlets that still matter for swaying elite discourse - donors, powerbrokers, and thought leaders who influence the party establishment.

Key Players:
  • MSNBC - Cable news standard-bearer
  • New York Times opinion pages - Elite print influence
  • The Atlantic - Long-form prestige journalism
  • The New Yorker - Intellectual liberal establishment

Tier 4: The Center Left (Distance from Progressives)

For Democrats looking to separate from left-wing elements, often featuring former Republicans and moderate voices critical of both parties.

Key Players:
  • Matt Yglesias (Substack) - Data-driven moderate analysis
  • The Bulwark - Founded by former Republicans, vocal Trump critics
  • Tim Miller - Former Jeb Bush communications director
Strategic Value: Several center-left voices have become "particularly cozy" with candidates like Pennsylvania Gov. Josh Shapiro, suggesting deliberate positioning away from progressive wing.

Tier 5: The Left Wing (Shifted Dramatically)

Major transformation since 2020. The "dirtbag left" Bernie Sanders voices have largely been replaced by pro-Palestine, anti-Israel focused outlets. Some Sanders voices have drifted right.

Key Players:
  • Mehdi Hasan's Zeteo - Pro-Palestine focus
  • Ryan Grim's Drop Site newsletter - Investigative progressive journalism
  • Hasan Piker - Streamer with 1.5 million Instagram followers
  • "The Adam Friedland Show" - Pitching appearances to reach young men
Avoidance Pattern: Many possible 2028 candidates have stayed away from this left-wing sphere, suggesting it may be seen as politically risky despite its reach.

2. Strategic Imperative: "Go Everywhere"

▼
"I'm going to continue to go on not just political podcasts, but everything from health and wellness podcasts all the way to sports podcasts. I'm going to continue to do as many as I possibly can." - Sen. Cory Booker (D-NJ), possible 2028 contender

The explosion of Democrat-friendly outlets has made it imperative for candidates to adopt an "omni-platform" strategy. The old playbook of courting the Times and MSNBC is no longer sufficient.

Star Progressive Shows (Established Power)

Major Players:
  • "Pod Save America" - Former Obama staffers, major Democratic influence
  • "The Daily Show" - Comedy with political impact
  • "The Breakfast Club" - Reaching diverse audiences
  • "The Ezra Klein Show" - Policy-focused intellectual conversations

Candidate Behavior Patterns

Wide Net Strategy: Candidates like Cory Booker advocate appearing on every possible platform, from political to health/wellness to sports.

Selective Avoidance: Many center-left candidates are staying away from the left-wing tier while cultivating relationships with moderate voices.

Building Personal Platforms: Some candidates creating their own media presence with mixed results.

3. The Republican Advantage: Non-Political Political Shows

▼
Key Distinction

Republicans have made inroads with influencers like Joe Rogan and Theo Von who don't host political shows but have shows that occasionally get political. This allows Republicans to reach new voters in non-political contexts, making the pitch feel less partisan.

Democratic Weakness: The growing partisan media system doesn't compensate for Republicans' advantage in reaching voters through lifestyle, sports, and entertainment content that occasionally becomes political rather than being explicitly partisan.

Emerging Democratic Crossover Venues

Strategists identify some emerging venues that could play a similar role for Democrats:

Potential Bridges to New Audiences:
  • Alex Cooper's "Call Her Daddy" - Women's lifestyle and relationships
  • Stephen A. Smith (ESPN) - Sports commentary with political asides
  • "Smartless" podcast - Three Hollywood stars (Jason Bateman, Will Arnett, Sean Hayes)
  • Scott Galloway's "The Prof G Pod" - Business and economics influencer
  • Bill Simmons' "The Ringer" - Sports and pop culture network recently hosting Kelly, Moore, Newsom

Recent Activity: "The Ringer" Courting Politicians

Bill Simmons' network has hosted several potential presidential candidates:

  • Arizona Sen. Mark Kelly
  • Maryland Gov. Wes Moore
  • California Gov. Gavin Newsom

This represents Democrats' attempt to replicate the Rogan model - reaching voters through entertainment/sports content rather than explicitly political programming.

4. Candidates Building Their Own Platforms

▼

Some potential candidates are attempting to create their own media platforms with vastly different results, suggesting that personal brand-building through media varies significantly in effectiveness.

Success Story: Gavin Newsom's "This is Gavin Newsom"

Launch: 2025

Format: Features Newsom amicably discussing issues with prominent right-wing figures (including Steve Bannon)

Performance Metrics:
  • Often one of the top-ranked liberal podcasts on Apple
  • 240,000+ YouTube subscribers
  • 137 videos produced
Strategic Insight: Newsom's approach of engaging with right-wing figures positions him as a bridge-builder willing to cross ideological lines, potentially appealing to moderate voters while maintaining liberal credentials.

Struggling Effort: Andy Beshear's Podcast

Kentucky Governor and possible 2028 contender

Performance Metrics:
  • 6,000 YouTube subscribers
  • 201 videos produced (more content than Newsom!)
Interpretation: Despite producing more content (201 vs 137 videos), Beshear has achieved only 2.5% of Newsom's subscriber base, suggesting that production volume alone doesn't guarantee audience growth. Name recognition, charisma, and content strategy all matter.

5. Implications for Democratic Politics and 2028

▼

Power Dilution

Legacy pundits and MSNBC stars have seen their gatekeeping power diluted by an explosion of new voices. No single outlet or personality can make or break a candidate anymore.

Ideological Fragmentation

The five-tier system creates different ideological tests and expectations. A candidate acceptable to "The Resistance" may be too progressive for "The Center Left" and vice versa.

Resource Demands

The "go everywhere" imperative requires massive time commitments and sophisticated media strategies. Campaigns need dedicated teams just to manage podcast and influencer relationships.

Authenticity Challenges

Appearing on outlets with contradictory ideological expectations creates risks of seeming inauthentic or opportunistic. How does a candidate satisfy both "The Resistance" and moderate voters courted through "The Bulwark"?

The Rogan Gap

Democrats still lack equivalent venues for reaching voters in non-political contexts. The partisan nature of most liberal media means candidates are mostly "preaching to the choir" rather than expanding their audience.

Personal Platform Gamble

The Newsom vs. Beshear contrast shows that building personal media platforms is high-risk. Success requires more than content production - it demands charisma, strategy, and possibly existing name recognition.

Discussion Questions for Current Events Group

  • Comparison to MAGA: Is the analogy to MAGA media's splintering apt? What are the similarities and differences between conservative and liberal media ecosystems?
  • Gatekeeping Then and Now: What did we lose and gain when power shifted from a few major outlets (NYT, MSNBC) to countless micro-factions? Is this democratization or chaos?
  • The Rogan Gap: Why have Democrats struggled to cultivate relationships with non-political influencers who occasionally discuss politics? Is this a strategic failure or a structural difference between the parties?
  • Ideological Tensions: How can 2028 candidates navigate the conflicting demands of "The Resistance" (proving anti-Trump credentials) and "The Center Left" (distancing from progressives)? Is it possible to satisfy both?
  • Left-Wing Avoidance: Why are many centrist candidates avoiding the pro-Palestine/anti-Israel left-wing tier? What does this suggest about the Democratic Party's internal divisions?
  • Personal Platforms: Should candidates build their own media platforms or focus on appearing across existing outlets? What explains the vast difference between Newsom and Beshear's results?
  • Quality vs. Quantity: The "go everywhere" strategy requires enormous time investment. At what point does quantity of appearances diminish the quality of a candidate's message and preparation?
  • Elite vs. Grassroots: Traditional elite outlets (Atlantic, New Yorker) still matter for donors and powerbrokers, but grassroots partisan outlets reach millions of voters. Where should candidates prioritize their time?
  • Historical Parallel: Is there a historical precedent for this kind of media fragmentation in American politics? How did candidates navigate media ecosystems in previous eras of technological change?
  • 2028 Prediction: Which potential 2028 candidates are best positioned to navigate this fractured media landscape? Does this system favor outsiders or establishment figures?
