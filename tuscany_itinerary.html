Days 1–3

Florence

Tuscany · Capital

The cradle of the Renaissance

Florence — Firenze in Italian — sits in a valley carved by the Arno River and backed by rolling Tuscan hills. Founded as a Roman colony in 59 BC, it rose to its greatest glory under the Medici banking dynasty in the 14th–16th centuries, when it became the intellectual and artistic capital of Europe and birthplace of the Renaissance.

The city gifted the world Brunelleschi's revolutionary dome on the Duomo — engineered without scaffolding, still the largest brick dome ever built — as well as Michelangelo's David, Botticelli's Birth of Venus, Dante's Divine Comedy, Machiavelli's The Prince, and Galileo's telescopes. Walking Florence is walking through the history of Western civilization.

Today, nearly three million people live in the greater Florence area, yet the historic center — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — retains an intimate, walkable character. Leather artisans, gelato makers, and neighborhood markets coexist with world-class museums at every turn.

Days 3–4

Chianti Countryside

Central Tuscany

Rolling hills, cypress lanes, and the world's most famous wine country

The Chianti region stretches between Florence and Siena across a landscape so perfectly picturesque it seems almost invented — steep vine-terraced slopes, silver-leafed olive groves, honey-stone farmhouses (case coloniche), and the iconic silhouettes of cypress trees lining every road. This is the heart of Tuscany as the world imagines it.

Chianti Classico wine, made predominantly from Sangiovese grapes, has been produced here since at least the 13th century. The "Black Rooster" (gallo nero) became the symbol of the historic wine-producing league in 1282, and remains on the neck of every certified Chianti Classico bottle today. Family estates — many owned for generations — offer tours through ancient cellars and vine-draped terraces.

The landscape itself is a living agricultural museum: medieval castles, Romanesque parish churches (pievi), and fortified villages (borghi) anchor a countryside that has been continuously farmed for over 2,500 years.

Day 4

Siena

Southern Tuscany

A medieval city frozen in time — and fiercely proud of it

Siena is one of the best-preserved medieval cities in Europe, its soaring Gothic skyline essentially unchanged since the 14th century when plague brought its golden age to a sudden halt. Founded, according to legend, by Senius — son of Remus — the city was Florence's great rival throughout the Middle Ages, producing its own brilliant school of art (Sienese Gothic) and a banking system that helped finance the papacy.

The city's extraordinary Duomo — striped in green and white marble, its facade encrusted with sculpture — was begun in 1215 and expanded ambitiously through the 1300s. The unfinished "New Cathedral" nave, abandoned after the Black Death, still looms above the city as a monument to interrupted ambition.

Siena is famously divided into 17 competing contrade (neighborhoods), each with its own flag, patron saint, museum, and fierce civic identity. Twice each summer — on July 2nd and August 16th — ten of these neighborhoods race bareback horses around the sloping Campo in the Palio, a tradition dating to 1644. Residents live and die by their contrada's honor; the rivalry is absolutely real.

Day 5

Pienza

Val d'Orcia

The world's first planned "ideal city" — and cheese capital of Tuscany

Pienza is a Renaissance thought experiment made real. In 1459, Pope Pius II hired the Florentine architect Bernardo Rossellino to transform his humble birthplace — a village called Corsignano — into a model city embodying Humanist ideals of proportion, light, and civic harmony. The resulting piazza, cathedral, and papal palace were completed in just three years, creating a miniature urban ensemble so complete and coherent that UNESCO now protects it as a masterpiece of urban planning.

Beneath the refined Renaissance surface lies something far older: Pienza sits atop an area extensively inhabited by the Etruscans, who carved tombs, chambers, and tunnels into the soft tufa rock. The surrounding Val d'Orcia — a UNESCO Cultural Landscape — is perhaps Tuscany's most iconic panorama: rolling golden hills dotted with isolated farmhouses, lone cypress trees, and winding white roads (strade bianche) disappearing into the distance.

Pienza is also famous for pecorino di Pienza, a sheep's milk cheese made in the surrounding hills and aged in cellars throughout the town — you'll find wheels of it in every shop window.

Days 5–6

Sovana

Maremma · Tufa Country

An Etruscan ghost town on a cliff of stone

Sovana is one of Tuscany's great hidden treasures — a tiny medieval village perched on a narrow ridge of tufa rock in the wild Maremma countryside. Once a significant Etruscan city and later birthplace of Pope Gregory VII (1015–1085), Sovana shrank to near-abandonment after a medieval malaria epidemic emptied the region. Today, only about 100 people live here, but the Romanesque cathedral and Etruscan necropolis surrounding it are extraordinary.

The Città del Tufo — the "City of Tufa" — is the name given to the broader archaeological landscape surrounding Sovana, Pitigliano, and Sorano. Tufa is the soft volcanic rock that defines this entire region: easy to carve, yet durable enough to last millennia. The Etruscans exploited it brilliantly, cutting temples, tombs, roads, and entire cliff-faces into it between the 7th and 3rd centuries BC.

The Vie Cave ("sunken roads") are the region's most haunting feature: deep, narrow corridors sliced through the tufa bedrock, some stretching for miles, their walls rising 15–20 meters above the traveler's head. Their exact purpose remains debated — sacred processional routes? livestock paths? defensive channels? — which makes walking them all the more mysterious.

Day 7

Pitigliano

Maremma · Grosseto

"Little Jerusalem" — a city that rises from the rock itself

Pitigliano is one of the most visually dramatic towns in all of Italy: a cluster of medieval buildings rising directly from a sheer cliff of tufa, seemingly growing from the rock itself, encircled by deep ravines on three sides. Seen from the approach road, it appears to float above the landscape like a vision from a fairy tale.

Its nickname — La Piccola Gerusalemme, "Little Jerusalem" — dates from the 16th century, when Spanish Jews expelled from the Iberian Peninsula found refuge here under the tolerant Orsini lords. The community established a ghetto (not a place of persecution but of self-organized community), a synagogue, a kosher butchery, a ritual bath, and a small bread oven for azzime (unleavened Passover bread). This community thrived for centuries, leaving behind a remarkably intact underground Jewish quarter that still draws visitors today.

Like Sovana and Sorano nearby, Pitigliano also sits atop an extensive Etruscan and Roman underground — cellars, passages, and cisterns carved through the tufa beneath the streets and accessible through ordinary doorways in people's homes.

Days 7–8

Maremma

Coastal Tuscany

Tuscany's wild west — cowboys, marshes, and a fiercely independent spirit

The Maremma is Tuscany's least-known face: a vast coastal plain stretching south of Grosseto, once malarial and nearly abandoned, now one of Italy's most prized natural landscapes. Where the Chianti region evokes refinement and art, the Maremma speaks of wildness — long beaches of white sand, scrubby macchia, Etruscan ruins half-swallowed by undergrowth, and the Parco Regionale della Maremma protecting miles of pristine coastline.

Most surprising of all: the Maremma has genuine cowboys. The butteri — Tuscan cattlemen who work the large estates called fattorie — have herded the long-horned Maremmana cattle and worked the distinctive white Maremmano horses here for centuries. Their traditions predate the American cowboy by hundreds of years. In 1890, Buffalo Bill reportedly challenged the butteri to a riding contest in Rome — and lost.

The Maremma was one of Italy's last great malarial regions, drained and reclaimed by Mussolini's land reform projects in the 1930s. That relative isolation is precisely what preserved its extraordinary wildlife, Etruscan sites, and traditional agricultural practices. The region now produces excellent Morellino di Scansano wine, olive oil, and some of the finest beef in Italy.

Day 9

Volterra

Province of Pisa

An Etruscan stronghold above the clouds

Volterra occupies one of the most commanding positions in Tuscany: a plateau nearly 550 meters above sea level, ringed by eroding clay cliffs called balze that have been slowly swallowing the town's edges for centuries — an ancient Etruscan necropolis on the western cliff has already partially collapsed into the abyss. The views from the town walls sweep across an enormous swath of Tuscany in every direction.

The city was one of the most powerful of the 12 Etruscan city-states (dodecapoli) between the 8th and 1st centuries BC. Its Etruscan museum holds one of the finest collections of cinerary urns in Italy — intricately carved alabaster containers that held the ashes of the deceased, often depicting scenes from mythology or the journey to the afterlife. The Romans later called the city Volaterrae and left behind a theater that was only excavated in the 1950s.

Today, Volterra is celebrated above all for its alabaster workshops. The soft, semi-translucent stone — quarried in the hills since Etruscan times — is carved by local artisans into lamps, sculptures, vases, and jewelry. Workshops line the main streets, and watching a craftsman transform a rough block into a glowing translucent form is quietly mesmerizing.

Day 10

Borgo a Mozzano

Lucchesia · Serchio Valley

Where the WWII Gothic Line cut through the mountains — and a bridge of legend

Borgo a Mozzano is a small town in the Serchio valley north of Lucca, best known for two things: a hauntingly beautiful medieval bridge and its place in one of the most brutal chapters of the Italian campaign in World War II.

The Ponte della Maddalena — also called the "Devil's Bridge" — is a medieval arched crossing over the Serchio River, its asymmetrical arches of different heights rising to a peak in the middle. Local legend holds it was built with the devil's help: a craftsman, unable to finish in time, promised Satan the first soul to cross. He cleverly sent a dog across instead. Built around the 11th century, it remains one of the most photographed bridges in Italy.

In 1944, the German Army's Gotenstellung — the Gothic Line — stretched across the Apennine mountains from coast to coast, a heavily fortified defensive line designed to hold the Allied advance through northern Italy. The Serchio valley saw fierce fighting; Borgo a Mozzano's local museum documents the wartime experience of the community caught in the middle of the campaign, preserving stories that are still vivid in local memory.

Days 9–11

Lucca

Northwest Tuscany

A perfectly preserved Renaissance walled city — hometown of Puccini

Lucca is perhaps the most livable city in Tuscany: elegant, intimate, unhurried, and enclosed within one of the finest intact sets of Renaissance walls in Europe. The city's 16th-century bastions — four kilometers of grass-topped promenades wide enough for a road — were never breached militarily and were instead converted into a beloved public park that Lucchesi have been strolling, cycling, and picnicking atop for 400 years.

Inside the walls, Lucca is a feast of Romanesque churches, aristocratic palaces, and Roman street-plans still legible in the city's oval Piazza dell'Anfiteatro — a perfect ellipse of medieval buildings enclosing the footprint of a 2nd-century Roman amphitheater. The city was an independent republic for nearly 500 years (1160–1799), longer than Venice, which left it with a fiercely self-sufficient civic character it retains to this day.

Lucca was the birthplace of Giacomo Puccini (1858–1924), composer of La Bohème, Tosca, Madama Butterfly, and Turandot — arguably the most beloved operatic repertoire in the world. His childhood home is now a museum; the city hosts an international opera festival in his honor every summer. Nearby Pisa — with its famous Leaning Tower and remarkable Piazza dei Miracoli — is just 20 minutes by train.
