üéØ Determining Your Goals

‚ñº

"If you don't know where you are going, any road will get you there." ‚Äì Lewis Carroll

Before diving into the content, take a step back. What do you want students to walk away with? Your answer shapes everything‚Äîfrom the syllabus to the teaching style.

üß≠ Three Fundamental Questions

  • What do you want students to know? (Content/facts)
  • What do you want students to be able to do? (Skills/thinking)
  • What do you want students to experience? (Feeling/transformation)

Example: Film History Course

  • Know: Key movements, directors, and films from silent era to present
  • Do: Analyze visual storytelling; make connections between films and historical context
  • Experience: Appreciation for how cinema shapes and reflects culture; confidence in discussing films critically

üìù Write It Down

Once you have clarity, write 1-2 sentences summarizing your course goals. This becomes your North Star when making decisions about content, pacing, and format.

üí° Pro Tip:

Keep your goals visible as you plan. When you're tempted to include that "really cool tangent," ask: Does this serve my goals?

üë• Defining Your Audience

‚ñº

"Know your audience." ‚Äì Every communication expert ever

BOLLI students aren't blank slates‚Äîthey come with rich life experience, curiosity, and preferences. Understanding them helps you pitch the right tone, depth, and pace.

üéì Who Are Your Students?

Consider their:

  • Background knowledge: Are they beginners, enthusiasts, or experts in your topic?
  • Interests & motivations: Why might they take this course? Intellectual curiosity? Social connection? Personal enrichment?
  • Learning preferences: Do they love deep reading or prefer discussion-based exploration?

‚öñÔ∏è Balancing Expectations

Not everyone will come with the same level of preparation. Some strategies:

  • If the course requires prior knowledge, state it clearly in the description (e.g., "familiarity with basic economics is helpful").
  • If you want to welcome everyone, design for a range: offer background readings for newcomers, deeper dives for experienced learners.
  • Make the first session an equalizer‚Äîan overview that brings everyone to a shared starting point.

Example: Philosophy Course

Open approach: "No prior philosophy required! We'll start with accessible texts and build from there."

Focused approach: "This course assumes familiarity with Kant and Hegel. We'll dive into advanced critiques from the start."

üóìÔ∏è Standard Formats at BOLLI

  • 5-Week Course: Focused and intense; great for a single text, concept, or theme
  • 10-Week Course: Room for breadth and depth; can cover multiple themes or texts with space for reflection

‚è∞ Session Length & Frequency

  • 90-minute sessions: Standard; allows for lecture + discussion
  • 2-hour sessions: More room for activities, group work, or in-depth discussion

üè¢ Delivery Mode

  • In-person: Best for interactive, discussion-heavy courses
  • Zoom: Accessible; works well for lecture-based or smaller discussion groups
  • Hybrid: Offers flexibility but requires careful planning

Matching Format to Content:

  • 5-week intensive: Single novel study, focused historical event, specific philosophical question
  • 10-week survey: Multiple authors, thematic exploration across time, complex topic requiring buildup

üí° Pro Tip:

If you're torn between 5 and 10 weeks, ask: Is this a "deep dive" or a "landscape view"? The answer will guide your choice.

‚úÇÔ∏è Narrowing to a Realistic Scope

‚ñº

"Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away." ‚Äì Antoine de Saint-Exup√©ry

One of the hardest parts of course design is saying no. You can't cover everything, and that's okay. A well-scoped course gives students depth over breadth.

Use AI to help research and refine your course outline

üö´ Common Pitfalls

  • The "Everything" Trap: Trying to cover an entire field or history in one course (e.g., "World History from Ancient Times to Today")
  • The "Greatest Hits" Problem: Cramming in too many famous works/figures without time to engage meaningfully
  • The "Perfectionist's Dilemma": Believing students need exhaustive background before discussing the "real" material

‚úÖ Strategies for Narrowing Scope

1. Start with Your Big Question

What's the central question or theme? Let that be your filter.

Broad: "American Literature"
Focused: "How did American writers respond to industrialization?"

2. Choose Depth Over Coverage

It's better to read one novel deeply than to skim five.

3. Embrace the Representative Example

You don't need to cover every philosopher‚Äîpick one or two as case studies that illuminate broader themes.

4. Trust the Learners

BOLLI students can handle complexity. You don't need to "dumb it down" or over-explain. Dive in.

Example: From Overwhelming to Focused

Too broad: "The History of Democracy"

Narrowed: "How Athens Invented Democracy‚Äîand Why It Failed"

Focus on a single historical case allows for rich discussion of democracy's strengths and vulnerabilities.

‚úÇÔ∏è The "One Less Thing" Rule:

Whatever you think you can cover, do one less thing. Students will thank you for the breathing room.

üéì Selecting Your Teaching Method

‚ñº

"Tell me and I forget, teach me and I may remember, involve me and I learn." ‚Äì Benjamin Franklin

How you teach matters as much as what you teach. BOLLI offers flexibility: you can lecture, facilitate discussions, lead activities, or blend methods.

üìñ Lecture-Based Learning

The SGL presents material through structured talks, often with slides or visuals.

When it works well:

  • Complex topics requiring expert explanation
  • Introducing new material or historical background
  • Large groups where discussion is harder to manage

Best Practices:

  • Keep segments to 15-20 minutes before shifting to Q&A or activity
  • Use visuals to illustrate concepts
  • Build in pauses for questions and reflection
  • End with a thought-provoking question to prime discussion

Example: Art History Course

SGL presents 20 minutes on Renaissance techniques, then shows multiple paintings and asks students to identify those techniques in action.

üí¨ Facilitated Discussion

A structured process where the SGL guides group conversation to achieve specific goals.

Benefits:

  • Involves the class in the learning experience
  • Achieves shared understanding
  • Enables analysis and problem-solving
  • Creates learning dialogue among participants

Facilitator Role:

  • Guide conversation toward specific goals
  • Encourage participation from all
  • Ask probing questions
  • Moderate to ensure all voices are heard
  • Get participants to listen and respond to each other

Example: Short Story Course

SGL asks a series of questions that lead the group through the story. Their collective exploration leads to understanding from multiple perspectives.

üìù Full Sample: "Stories to Live By"

Hook: What if the stories we tell shape the world we live in?

In this course, we'll explore how narrative‚Äîand who gets to tell it‚Äîshapes identity, culture, and power. Through literature, film, and personal reflection, we'll examine how stories help us make sense of the world‚Äîand how they can also limit or liberate our thinking.

We'll begin with Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's TED Talk, "The Danger of a Single Story," as a foundation for discussing the stakes of storytelling in everyday life. From there, we'll read a mix of texts‚Äîranging from fiction and memoir to essays and visual narratives‚Äîthat open up alternative ways of seeing and being:

  • James Baldwin's The Fire Next Time, a searing meditation on race, faith, and the American dream
  • Maxine Hong Kingston's The Woman Warrior, which blends memoir and myth to explore silence, voice, and cultural inheritance
  • Claudia Rankine's Citizen: An American Lyric, a hybrid text of poetry, essay, and visual art that confronts the insidiousness of everyday racism
  • Jordan Peele's film Get Out, offering a sharp cultural critique through the lens of horror and satire

Together, we'll examine how personal and collective stories are shaped by race, gender, memory, and power‚Äîand how they in turn shape us. You'll be invited to connect these works to your own life experiences through discussion and short reflective writings.

No prior experience with literature, theory, or storytelling is required‚Äîjust curiosity and a willingness to engage.
