Before we begin: what are these companies?

Both Vanguard and Fidelity are among the largest and most trusted investment firms in the world. They help everyday people grow their money by offering access to financial markets without needing a stockbroker. Think of them as the "bank" for your investments — except instead of savings accounts, they hold stocks, bonds, and funds on your behalf.

With $1–2 million to invest, you are in an excellent position. Both firms offer premium services at this level, and your costs will be very low. This guide walks through what each offers, what it costs, and which might suit you better.

Section 01

The Funds They Offer

A "fund" pools money from thousands of investors to buy a basket of stocks or bonds. Instead of picking individual companies (risky and time-consuming), a fund spreads your money across hundreds or thousands of them automatically. Below are the most important fund categories each firm offers.

Vanguard
"Owned by its investors"
Founded 1975 by John Bogle — the inventor of the index fund. Vanguard is uniquely structured: fund investors own the company itself, which means profits go back to you as lower fees. Over $9 trillion in global assets under management.
Fidelity
"For the long term"
Founded 1946, privately owned by the Johnson family. Fidelity competes on technology and breadth of services. Famous for offering several index funds with zero expense ratios. Over $14 trillion in assets under management.

What does "expense ratio" mean for your money?
On a $1,500,000 portfolio, a 0.03% expense ratio costs you $450/year. A 0.50% expense ratio costs $7,500/year. Both firms offer many funds well below 0.10% — making your annual fund costs under $1,500 on a $1.5M portfolio. This is extraordinarily low compared to traditional financial advisors who typically charge 1% ($15,000/year).

The $1M+ Advantage

Section 05

Which Is Right for You?

Neither firm is universally "better" — they serve slightly different investor profiles. Here's a plain-English summary of who should go where.

Choose Vanguard if…

You want simplicity & a pure investing philosophy

  • You want a "set it and forget it" portfolio using low-cost index funds
  • You value having a human CFP® advisor at 0.30%/year with a dedicated person at $1M+
  • You care that the company's structure aligns its interests with yours
  • You don't need banking features or frequent trading
  • You're attracted to Vanguard's legendary commitment to low-cost investing
  • You're comfortable with an older, simpler website interface
Choose Fidelity if…

You want features, flexibility & the lowest possible costs

  • You want the absolute lowest fund costs (ZERO funds cost literally $0)
  • You want a great mobile app, modern website, and 24/7 support
  • You'd use in-person branches or want to walk in for help
  • You want strong cash management with worldwide ATM reimbursements
  • You'd benefit from tax-loss harvesting, research tools, or 529 plans
  • You want access to actively managed funds alongside index funds
The honest truth
At $1–2 million invested in low-cost index funds, the difference in annual fees between Vanguard and Fidelity is likely less than $1,000/year — and could be $0 difference. The more important choice is your asset allocation (how you split between stocks and bonds), your advisor relationship, and staying disciplined during market downturns. Either firm will serve you exceptionally well. Many investors maintain accounts at both.
